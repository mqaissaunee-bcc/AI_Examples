AI Literacy · A Short Lesson

Everything Is a Remix

What a video series about how creativity actually works can teach us about generating with AI — and where the line sits.

The Idea

Creativity isn’t lightning from a clear sky.

In 2010, filmmaker Kirby Ferguson released a series called Everything Is a Remix. His argument was blunt: the things we celebrate as original — songs, films, inventions — are almost always built out of work that came before. A decade later he turned the same lens on AI image generators. This short lesson walks his argument, then asks the question your colleagues are really asking: what does it mean when the remixer is a machine?

Ferguson breaks every creative act into three moves. Tap each one.

  • Hip-hop — built on sampling
  • Star Wars — westerns + samurai films + Flash Gordon
  • The iPhone — existing parts, new combination
  • Led Zeppelin — the borrowers who got borrowed

“Nothing is original” isn’t an insult. It’s a description of how culture has always worked — and a relief from the pressure to invent from scratch.The core claim of Everything Is a Remix

The Turn

AI is a remix engine — turned up to maximum.

In 2023, Ferguson released a chapter focused entirely on AI image generators. His instinct fit the thesis neatly: these tools learn patterns from millions of human-made works and recombine them on demand. Copy, transform, combine — automated and instant.

If borrowing and recombining is how creativity has always worked, then AI generation isn’t a clean break from tradition. It’s the same old engine, running dramatically faster and at a scale no individual artist could match. That reframing is genuinely useful: it lowers the temperature on “is this even real creativity?” and lets us ask the sharper question instead.

The Honest Part

The question isn’t “remix or not.”

Almost everything is a remix — so that question is already settled. To his credit, Ferguson doesn’t pretend AI is simple, and neither should we. Two things are true at the same time:

The remix view

Drawing on existing work is legitimate and unavoidable. Demanding “pure originality” was always a myth — and it’s the myth that makes people afraid to create at all.

The artist’s worry

AI changes the scale and the speed. A model can absorb a living artist’s entire body of work and imitate their signature look in seconds — without asking, crediting, or paying them.

Both can hold. So the useful question becomes: what kind of remix is fair, and what kind crosses a line? The pattern below is the whole lesson in one sentence: the more you transform and combine — and the further you move from wholesale copying of one identifiable, living creator — the more clearly you’re just doing what creators have always done.

In Practice

What responsible remixing looks like

None of this requires swearing off AI tools. It requires the same judgment good creators have always used — applied to a faster engine.

  • ✓Transform, don’t just reproduce.Add enough of yourself that the result is genuinely new. A rough AI draft you then rework is different from a finished output you simply collect.
  • ✓Combine widely.Many influences blended together is the most defensible remix move there is. One influence copied is the least.
  • ✓Don’t trade on a name.Reach for a general style (“retro comic,” “watercolor,” “mid-century poster”) rather than invoking a specific living artist by name to stand in for your own voice.
  • ✓Be transparent.Say when AI was involved. Don’t pass a remix off as something it isn’t — in a classroom, transparency is the lesson.
  • ✓Hold the unresolved parts lightly.Whether training on scraped work without consent is fair is a live legal and ethical debate. Watch how norms and law develop rather than assuming the matter is closed.

Take It With You

A 5-minute reflection

Think of something you’ve made — a lesson, a slide deck, a piece of code, a playlist. Name its three biggest influences. Then ask yourself: at what point would leaning on one of those influences stop feeling like inspiration and start feeling like taking? And where, exactly, would adding an AI tool to the mix change your answer? Bring that line to the group.
