What is AI Resistance Studio?

A standalone tool for strengthening any assignment
⏱ 2 min

AI Resistance Studio is a browser-based tool that helps faculty make assignments more resistant to AI completion. You paste in any assignment, configure a few options, and the Studio generates a strengthened version with targeted requirements that make authentic human engagement genuinely necessary.

Nothing is installed. No account is required. Your text never leaves your browser.

🛡️ Detection vs. design AI Resistance Studio takes a design-first approach. Instead of trying to detect AI use after the fact — which is unreliable and can penalize students unfairly — it helps you design assignments where authentic human effort is clearly required and visible. Better assignment design is a more durable strategy than detection.

Paste Your Assignment

The starting point for everything
⏱ 2 min

Open the Studio and find the large text area on the left panel labeled "Paste or type your assignment prompt." This is where you enter the assignment you want to strengthen.

You can paste finished assignment instructions, a rough draft, or even a single sentence describing the task. The Studio works with whatever you give it — more specific input tends to produce more targeted output, but brevity is fine too.

💡 Try the Quick Examples first Five example pills appear below the text area: Essay, Discussion post, Research project, Case analysis, and Lab report. Clicking one loads a sample assignment. This is the fastest way to explore what the Studio produces — try a few before working on your own assignments.

Your original text is always preserved at the top of the output — the Studio adds resistance requirements after it, never replacing or rewriting your wording. What you type in is what students will read first.

Attach a Reference Document

Optional — for rubrics, readings, and case files
⏱ 2 min

Just below the assignment textarea, look for a dashed toggle button: "I have a reference document for this assignment." This is optional — use it when you have a file you want an AI tool to treat as primary source material when students work with the generated prompt.

📎 Reference Document Toggle
📎 I have a reference document for this assignment
When toggled ON →
📎 Reference document will be included in prompt ✓

When this toggle is active and a file is selected, the generated prompt includes a Reference Document block that instructs the AI to treat the attached file as the primary source — grounding its response in the document's content rather than general knowledge.

📌 Two-step process The Studio generates a text prompt you'll paste into an AI tool. Selecting a file here tells the Studio to include document-handling instructions in that prompt. You then upload the actual file in your AI tool (Claude, ChatGPT, etc.) alongside the copied prompt — just as you'd attach any file when chatting with an AI.

Good candidates for this feature include: a rubric you want the AI to follow, a case study students will analyze, a reading the assignment is based on, a dataset to interpret, or any primary source that should anchor the AI's output.

Set Your AI Use Policy

The AIAS Scale — five levels of permitted AI use
⏱ 3 min

The AIAS Scale (AI Integration and Accountability Scale) is a five-button selector that defines how much AI use is permitted on this assignment. Whichever level you select, the Studio embeds a clear, level-appropriate AI Use Policy statement in the output — students will read it as part of the assignment instructions.

AIAS Level — How much AI should be permitted?
1No AI
2AI-Assisted
3AI-Integrated
4AI-Collaborative
5AI-Enhanced
Level 3 — AI-Integrated: AI acts as a collaboration tool. Students must explain their decision-making and submit process artifacts alongside the finished product.
LevelNameTypical use
1No AIIn-class exams, handwritten work, foundational assessments
2AI-AssistedBrainstorming, grammar checking only — attribution required
3AI-IntegratedMost writing/analysis assignments — process artifacts required
4AI-CollaborativeCuration and critical synthesis of AI-generated content
5AI-EnhancedFull integration with required metacognitive reflection
Note: The AIAS level doesn't limit which resistance strategies you can select — it only controls the AI Use Policy language in the output. You can combine any level with any strategy.

5

Process Emphasis Slider

Product-focused to process-heavy — five positions
⏱ 2 min

Below the strategy cards, find the Process Emphasis slider. This is a separate control from the Process Over Product strategy card. It lets you dial in how much weight process documentation carries across the whole assignment — without necessarily enabling the full process portfolio.

Process Emphasis
Product-FocusedBalancedProcess-Heavy
Balanced — equal weight on process and product

The slider has five positions:

  • 1 — Product-Focused: No additional process language. Output emphasizes the final product only.
  • 2 — Lightly Process-Aware: Suggests documentation without requiring it.
  • 3 — Balanced (default): Equal weight on process and product in the output framing.
  • 4 — Process-Emphasized: Adds a process documentation section (unless the Process Over Product card is already active).
  • 5 — Process-Heavy: Strong portfolio requirements added — brainstorming artifacts, annotated bibliography, decision log, final reflection, and significant grading weight.
Tip: If you select the Process Over Product strategy card and set the slider to level 4 or 5, the card takes precedence and no duplicate process section is added — the Studio is smart enough not to double up.

6

Add Discipline Context

Optional — helps anchor the output in your field
⏱ 1 min

Near the bottom of the left panel, you'll find a short text field: "Discipline / Subject Area (optional)." Enter your discipline — Nursing, Business, History, CTE, Environmental Science, whatever fits — and the Studio appends a brief discipline context paragraph to the output.

Discipline / Subject Area (optional)
Environmental Science
Added to output: "This assignment is situated within Environmental Science. Your examples, sources, and analysis should reflect the standards, conventions, and real-world contexts of this field."

This framing matters because it makes generic AI responses easier to spot. A student in an Environmental Science course drawing on local field data will produce very different work from a generic AI response about ecology. Making that expectation explicit raises the bar.

Tip: The field is free-text — type anything. Sub-specialties work too: "Nursing — Critical Care," "Business — Entrepreneurship," "English Composition — Academic Writing for STEM."

Resistance Score — A percentage reflecting the combined strength of your selections. It's a design estimate, not a guarantee: the score is only as meaningful as how consistently you enforce the requirements in your grading rubric.

Copy Full Prompt — Copies the complete output: your original assignment plus all injected resistance sections. Paste directly into Canvas, a Word document, or an email.

Copy Original — Copies just your unmodified input text. Useful if you want to share the original alongside the strengthened version, or start a fresh round of strengthening.

What Was Added — A bulleted summary of every element the Studio injected. Use this as a checklist when updating your Canvas assignment to confirm nothing was missed.

Start Over — Resets all fields and options to defaults so you can strengthen a different assignment.

🛡️ Using the output in Canvas Paste the full output as the assignment description, or paste only the injected sections below your original wording — whichever fits your course formatting. Some faculty add a horizontal rule between the original assignment and the resistance requirements to make the structure visually clear to students.
🎯 Try It

Go back to the Studio, select two strategies, set your AIAS level, and click Strengthen. Review the What Was Added list. Does the output match what you intended? If any section feels too strong or not relevant, deselect that strategy and regenerate.
