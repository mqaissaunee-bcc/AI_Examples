Generating Visuals with AI

A practical guide for educators on what today's AI tools can produce graphically — from textbook-quality handouts and infographics to diagrams, slide decks, and data visualizations — and how to choose the right tool for the job.

Pick the Right Tool

What Should I Use For…?

There's no single "best" AI graphics tool. Match the job to the category. Here's a quick decision table for the visuals faculty most often need.

An illustrated handout or comparison chart ChatGPT (GPT-4o image)Gemini Imagen Newer image models render readable text and consistent multi-panel layouts — finally usable for textbook-style handouts.

A full slide deck GammaBeautiful.ai Purpose-built for decks — themed layouts, image placement, and speaker notes generated together. Faster than ChatGPT-to-PowerPoint.

Rule of thumb: if the visual contains real data or precise structure, use a tool that works from your inputs (charts from data, diagrams from text). If the visual is illustrative, an image model is fine. Image models invent details — that's a feature for art and a bug for facts.
How To · Step 1

Prepare Your Lesson Content

The visual is only as good as the input. Outline what you want to communicate before opening any AI tool.

1

Start from your learning objective

What do students need to understand or compare? A single objective produces a focused visual; a sprawling one produces a cluttered mess.

Sketch the structure on paper or in a doc:

  • The topic and audience (e.g., "Types of business ownership, intro business class")
  • The categories or items to compare (Sole Proprietorship, LLC, S-Corp, etc.)
  • The dimensions of comparison (liability, taxes, ease of formation)
  • Any required examples, icons, or domain conventions
How To · Step 2

Write a Generation Prompt

Be specific. Treat the AI like an instructional designer you've just hired — it needs a brief, not a hint.

2

Use a structured prompt

The same prompt shape works across most image-capable tools (ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude with image generation). Adapt the closing line for your tool.

Reusable Prompt Template
Act as an instructional designer and graphic designer. Create a high-quality, visually engaging handout for [audience: e.g., students in an intro business class]. Topic: "[Your topic]" Include: • A clear title and subtitle • A visual comparison chart or infographic comparing [list items] • Key categories: [list of dimensions to compare] • Clean, modern formatting suitable for print or PDF • Subject-relevant icons or illustrations to enhance visual appeal • Bold headings, section dividers, generous white space Style: looks like it belongs in a college-level [discipline] course.

For diagrams (Whimsical, Napkin, Claude+Mermaid), describe the relationships instead: "A flowchart showing how a customer ticket moves from intake → triage → resolution, with feedback loops…"

For charts (Claude, ChatGPT data analysis), upload the CSV and ask: "Make a bar chart of revenue by quarter, with our brand navy and gold colors."

How To · Step 3

Review Before You Use

AI visuals look polished even when they're wrong. Build a habit of checking.

3

Run the four-point check

  • Accuracy. Is every fact, label, and example correct? Image models routinely invent plausible-but-wrong details.
  • Spelling. Even the latest image models occasionally produce gibberish or mangled words in dense layouts.
  • Layout & readability. Can a student scan it in 30 seconds and grasp the main point?
  • Bias & representation. Are the people, examples, and contexts inclusive? Image models default to narrow stereotypes unless prompted otherwise.

If revisions are needed, prompt for them

You don't have to start over. Common revision prompts:

  • "Simplify the text on the chart — keep only the key points."
  • "Add icons next to each category to make it more scannable."
  • "Fix the typo on '[label]' and regenerate."
  • "Make a black-and-white version that prints clearly."
  • "Try a more diverse set of example businesses."
How To · Step 4

Download, Share, and Use

4

Save in the right format for the right place

  • PNG — Canvas pages, slide decks, anywhere on screen. Sharp at any zoom.
  • PDF — Print handouts, formal documents.
  • SVG (from diagram tools) — Editable later; scales without losing quality.
  • HTML (from interactive tools) — Drop into Canvas as a file or iframe.

Where to put it

  • Google Docs / Word handouts and worksheets
  • Google Slides / PowerPoint / Keynote
  • Canvas pages, modules, and assignments (PNG or iframe for interactive)
  • Printed for in-class distribution or posters
How To · Step 5

Generate Variations

One prompt can spawn an entire instructional kit. Squeeze every drop out of the work you already did.

5

Follow-up prompts that multiply your output

  • "Now make a poster-sized version of this handout."
  • "Create a worksheet version with fill-in-the-blank definitions."
  • "Make a black-and-white version for easy printing."
  • "Generate quiz questions based on the content of this handout."
  • "Now turn this into a 6-slide deck with speaker notes."
  • "Create a 60-second script summarizing this for a video."
  • "Translate the labels to Spanish, keeping the same layout."
Bonus Tips

For Faculty

Always fact-check

AI visuals are confidently wrong about as often as they're confidently right. Verify every claim before students see it.

Make students interpret

Use AI graphics as warm-up activities. "What does this infographic get right? What's missing?" turns consumption into critical analysis.

Cross-curricular reuse

The same prompt template works for science diagrams, history timelines, math visualizations, and ELA character maps.

Be transparent

Tell students when a visual was AI-generated, and model the review process you ran. They'll learn the same skill.

Build a prompt library

The third time you write a similar prompt, save it. A small collection of refined templates dramatically speeds up future creation.

Match tool to task

Don't force one tool to do everything. A diagram tool for diagrams, a chart tool for charts, an image tool for images. Use the gallery and decision table above.
