🔑 Authentication & Account Management

Security+ SY0-701 | Domains 1.2 · 2.4 · 4.6 · 5.6

Why Authentication Matters

AuthenticationAuthenticationThe process of verifying the identity of a user, device, or system. Authentication answers "Who are you?" Access decisions cannot be made without first confirming identity. Distinguished from authorization (what you can do) and accounting (what you did). is the foundation of every security model. Before a system can authorize access or hold users accountable for actions, it must first confirm identity. Weak or bypassed authentication is consistently ranked among the most exploited attack vectors in breach reports, including OWASP, Verizon DBIR, and CISA advisories.

This lesson covers the full authentication and account management lifecycle — from the theoretical concepts of identification and authentication to the practical controls that protect enterprise environments: multi-factor authentication, identity federation, privileged access management, and account lifecycle governance.

🔑 Authentication (AuthN)

Verifying who a user or device claims to be. Relies on factors: something you know, have, are, where you are, or what you do.

🛡️ Authorization (AuthZ)

Determining what an authenticated identity is permitted to do. Implemented through access control models: DAC, MAC, RBAC, ABAC, Rule-based.

📋 Accounting (Audit)

Tracking what authenticated users actually did. Enables forensics, compliance, and anomaly detection. Part of AAA framework.

⚠️ The Stakes

80%+ of breaches involve compromised credentials. Credential theft, password spraying, and MFA bypass are top attack vectors. Account management failures create orphaned, over-privileged accounts.

🔐 Authentication Factors & Methods

SY0-701 Objective 4.6 · 1.2

The Five Authentication Factors

Authentication factors are categories of evidence used to verify identity. Combining multiple factor categories creates multi-factor authenticationMulti-Factor Authentication (MFA)Requiring two or more authentication factors from different categories. The critical requirement is that factors must be from DIFFERENT categories — two passwords is NOT MFA. Defeating one factor must not automatically compromise others.. The exam tests both the factor categories and correct identification of what belongs in each.

🧠 Something You Know

Knowledge factors: Passwords, PINs, passphrases, security questions, cognitive passwords. Most common but also most attackable — susceptible to phishing, keyloggers, brute force, and credential stuffing.

📱 Something You Have

Possession factors: Hardware tokens (RSA SecurID), TOTP apps (Google Authenticator), SMS OTP, smart cards, PIV/CAC cards, USB security keys (FIDO2/YubiKey), push notifications.

👁️ Something You Are

Inherence factors: Biometrics — fingerprint, retina scan, iris scan, facial recognition, voice print, vein pattern, hand geometry. Tied to physical characteristics; cannot be easily changed if compromised.

📍 Somewhere You Are

Location factors: IP geolocation, GPS coordinates, network segment, geofencing. Often used as a contextual factor in adaptive authentication rather than a standalone authenticator.

🎯 Something You Do

Behavioral factors: Typing rhythm (keystroke dynamics), mouse movement patterns, gait analysis, behavioral biometrics. Often used for continuous authentication after initial login.

Password Security

🔨 Brute Force

Tries every possible combination. Mitigated by account lockout, long passwords, and rate limiting.

📋 Dictionary Attack

Uses wordlists of common passwords. Mitigated by complexity requirements and checking against breach databases (Have I Been Pwned).

🌊 Password Spraying

One common password against many accounts to avoid lockout. Mitigated by MFA and unusual login detection.

📦 Credential Stuffing

Uses credentials leaked from other breaches. Mitigated by MFA and breach detection services.

🌈 Rainbow Table

Pre-computed hash lookups. Mitigated completely by salted hashes (bcrypt, Argon2, PBKDF2).

🎣 Phishing

Tricks users into revealing credentials. Mitigated by FIDO2 passkeys (phishing-resistant), security training.

Key Concept

Salting adds a unique random value to each password before hashing, making rainbow table attacks infeasible. Even two users with the same password will have different hashes. Peppering adds a secret server-side value known only to the application (not stored in the DB).

NIST SP 800-63B Password Guidance (Current Best Practice)

NIST recommends minimum 8 characters for user-chosen passwords, with 64+ characters allowed. Passphrases (four random words: "correct-horse-battery-staple") provide more entropy than "P@ssw0rd!" style complexity. Complexity rules lead users to predictable patterns.

NIST 800-63B discourages forced periodic password changes unless compromise is suspected. Forced rotation leads to weak incremental changes (Password1 → Password2). Change on suspected compromise only.

NIST recommends checking new passwords against lists of known-breached passwords (e.g., Have I Been Pwned API). Block commonly used passwords and known-compromised credentials regardless of complexity compliance.

Avoid requiring specific character types (uppercase, symbol, number) as they lead to predictable patterns. Never use password hints — they weaken security. Allow all printable ASCII and Unicode (including spaces).

Biometric Authentication Metrics

FAR — False Acceptance Rate

Rate at which the system incorrectly accepts an unauthorized user. High FAR = insecure system. Type I from the system's perspective but Type II error statistically.

FRR — False Rejection Rate

Rate at which the system incorrectly rejects an authorized user. High FRR = poor usability. Also called False Non-Match Rate (FNMR).

CER / EER — Crossover Error Rate

The point where FAR equals FRR. The lower the CER, the more accurate the biometric system. Used to compare biometric technologies objectively.

⚠️ Biometric Concerns

Biometrics cannot be changed if compromised. Privacy concerns around data collection. Spoofing attacks (deepfakes, fingerprint cloning). Cultural and accessibility considerations.

Single Sign-On (SSO) & Federation

Single Sign-OnSingle Sign-On (SSO)An authentication scheme that allows a user to log in once and gain access to multiple systems without re-authenticating. Reduces password fatigue and the attack surface of multiple credential stores. Implemented via Kerberos (enterprise), SAML (web federation), OAuth/OIDC (modern APIs). enables users to authenticate once and access multiple resources. Federation extends SSO across organizational boundaries. Understanding the protocols is critical for the exam.

🎟️ Kerberos

Windows Active Directory SSO. Uses a KDC (Key Distribution Center) with Authentication Server (AS) and Ticket Granting Server (TGS). Client receives TGT, then service tickets (ST). Relies on time synchronization (5-min clock skew max). Port 88.

📜 SAML 2.0

Security Assertion Markup Language. XML-based standard for exchanging authentication and authorization data between an Identity Provider (IdP) and Service Provider (SP). Dominant in enterprise SSO and cloud applications. Browser-based.

🔑 OAuth 2.0

Authorization framework (NOT an authentication protocol). Allows apps to access resources on behalf of users without sharing credentials. Uses access tokens. Commonly used for API authorization. OIDC (OpenID Connect) adds authentication on top.

🪪 OpenID Connect (OIDC)

Authentication layer built on OAuth 2.0. Adds ID tokens (JWT format) containing identity claims. The modern standard for "Sign in with Google/Apple/Microsoft." Combines authentication + authorization in one flow.

📱 Multi-Factor Authentication

SY0-701 Objective 4.6

MFA Implementation Methods

MFA requires two or more factors from different categories. Combining a password with another factor from the same category (e.g., two passwords, two PINs) is NOT MFA — it's multi-step authentication. The exam distinguishes these concepts.

Time-Based OTP (TOTP) — RFC 6238

TOTPTOTP (Time-Based One-Time Password)A temporary 6–8 digit code generated by combining a shared secret with the current time (usually 30-second windows). Algorithm: HOTP(K, T) where T = floor(Unix_time / 30). Both client and server compute the same value independently — no network round-trip needed. RFC 6238. Used in Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator, Authy. generates codes using: TOTP = HOTP(SharedSecret, floor(CurrentTime / TimeStep)). Standard time step is 30 seconds. Common apps: Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator, Authy.

1
Enrollment: Server generates shared secret, encodes as QR code
2
Storage: Secret stored in authenticator app (and server)
3
Code Generation: App computes HMAC-SHA1(secret, time_counter) → 6-digit code
4
Verification: Server computes the same value and compares (allows ±1 window for clock skew)

HOTP — HMAC-Based OTP (RFC 4226)

HOTPHOTP (HMAC-Based One-Time Password)A counter-based OTP where codes change with each use rather than with time. Both client and server maintain a synchronized counter. Codes remain valid until used — less time-sensitive than TOTP. Used in hardware tokens where clock drift is a concern. uses a counter rather than time. Codes remain valid until used. Physical RSA SecurID tokens traditionally use this approach. Counter synchronization can become an issue if the token is pressed without submitting.

Push Authentication

Server sends a push notification to a registered mobile app (Duo, Microsoft Authenticator, Okta Verify). User approves or denies directly from the notification. More convenient than TOTP — no code to type. Can include context: location, application, device.

✅ Push Advantages

  • No code to read/type — reduces errors
  • Context-rich: shows what's being requested
  • Number matching prevents blind approvals
  • Phishing-resistant when number matching enforced

⚠️ SMS OTP Weaknesses

  • SIM Swapping: Attacker ports number to new SIM
  • SS7 Attack: Intercept SMS via telecom protocol flaws
  • Phishing: Real-time phishing proxies relay codes
  • NIST 800-63B restricts SMS as authenticator (RESTRICTED status)
MFA Fatigue Attack

Attackers send repeated push notification requests hoping the user will approve one to stop the notifications. Mitigation: Number matching (show a code on login page that user must confirm in the push app), additional context display, limit push attempts, use FIDO2 instead.

Hardware Security Keys — FIDO2 / WebAuthn

FIDO2 (WebAuthn + CTAP2) is the most phishing-resistant MFA method available. Uses asymmetric cryptography — a unique key pair is created per website. The private key never leaves the device; the server stores only the public key.

1
Registration: Device generates key pair; public key registered with the site
2
Challenge: Server sends a cryptographic challenge (nonce) + relying party ID (domain)
3
Signing: Key signs the challenge WITH the domain name embedded — only valid for that exact domain
4
Verification: Server verifies signature with stored public key — phishing domains cannot forge it

🗝️ Passkeys

FIDO2 credentials stored on platform authenticators (iPhone, Android, Windows Hello). Sync across devices via cloud. Replaces passwords entirely — passwordless authentication. Apple, Google, and Microsoft support passkeys natively.

🪪 Smart Cards / PIV / CAC

Physical cards with embedded X.509 certificates and private keys. Government PIV (Personal Identity Verification) and military CAC (Common Access Card) are examples. Require card + PIN = two factors.

🔑 Hardware OTP Tokens

Dedicated devices (RSA SecurID) generating TOTP/HOTP codes. No smartphone required. Used in high-security environments. Can be seeded tokens (compromise of seed compromises all tokens with same seed).

MFA Bypass & Attack Techniques

🤜 MFA Fatigue (Prompt Bombing)

Flood user with push requests until one is approved accidentally. Mitigated by number matching and push limits.

🕷️ AiTM Phishing

Adversary-in-the-Middle proxy captures session cookie after MFA. Bypasses MFA entirely. Mitigated by FIDO2 (domain-bound).

📱 SIM Swapping

Social engineer telecom to port number. Receives SMS OTPs. Mitigated by avoiding SMS MFA, using app-based authenticators.

🔄 Real-Time Phishing

Proxy relays credentials + OTP in real time. Valid only for one session. FIDO2 is the definitive countermeasure.

📋 SS7 Attack

Exploits telecom protocol vulnerabilities to intercept SMS. Nation-state level threat. Eliminated by using TOTP apps or hardware tokens.

🗑️ Recovery Code Theft

Compromise of stored backup codes bypasses MFA. Mitigation: store codes securely, limit use count, require re-enrollment if used.

Federation & Trust

Identity federationFederationA system where multiple organizations agree to share identity information across domain boundaries. Users authenticate with their home IdP (Identity Provider) and receive access to resources at the Service Provider (SP) without creating new accounts. Implemented via SAML, OAuth, WS-Federation. allows users from one organization (Identity Provider) to access resources at another organization (Service Provider) using their existing credentials. This is fundamental to cloud computing and business partnerships.

🏠 Identity Provider (IdP)

The authoritative source that authenticates users and issues identity assertions. Examples: Microsoft Entra ID (formerly Azure AD), Okta, Google Workspace, Active Directory Federation Services (AD FS). Holds the canonical identity store.

🏢 Service Provider (SP)

The application or service that relies on the IdP for authentication. SP trusts the IdP's assertions without independently verifying credentials. Examples: Salesforce, ServiceNow, AWS applications consuming SAML assertions.

📜 Trust Anchor

The established trust relationship between IdP and SP, typically implemented via certificate exchange. SP must trust the IdP's signing certificate. Pre-established federation metadata describes the relationship.

⚠️ Federation Risks

A compromised IdP can access all federated SPs (blast radius). Token replay attacks if assertion lifetime not enforced. Attribute mapping errors can grant excessive access. Monitor federation log activity.

Directory Services

Microsoft Active Directory uses a hierarchical structure: Forest → Domain → Organizational Unit (OU) → Object. Authentication uses Kerberos v5. Authorization managed via Group Policy Objects (GPOs) and group membership. Domain Controllers store the AD database (NTDS.dit) and must be highly protected — compromise gives attacker complete forest control.

AD Attack Targets: DCSync (dump all hashes), Pass-the-Ticket, Kerberoasting (crack service account tickets), Golden Ticket (forge TGTs), Silver Ticket (forge service tickets), AS-REP Roasting.

LDAP (Lightweight Directory Access Protocol) is the query protocol for directory services. Distinguished Names (DN) identify objects: CN=John Smith,OU=Finance,DC=corp,DC=example,DC=com. Always use LDAPS (port 636) or LDAP with StartTLS — plain LDAP transmits credentials in cleartext. LDAP injection attacks target applications that build LDAP queries from user input.

Microsoft Entra ID (formerly Azure Active Directory) is a cloud-native identity platform supporting OAuth 2.0, SAML 2.0, and OIDC. Key features: Conditional Access (risk-based policies), Privileged Identity Management (PIM), Identity Protection (sign-in risk scoring), and B2B/B2C identity federation. Does not use Kerberos — uses token-based protocols instead.

System for Cross-domain Identity Management (SCIM) is a REST API standard for automating user provisioning and deprovisioning between identity providers and SaaS applications. When a user is created in the IdP, SCIM automatically creates the corresponding account in all connected applications. Reduces orphaned accounts and eliminates manual provisioning delays.

Privileged Access Management (PAM)

PAMPrivileged Access Management (PAM)Security controls specifically for managing privileged (administrative) accounts. PAM solutions vault privileged credentials, require check-out/check-in workflows, rotate passwords automatically, and record all privileged sessions. Goal: ensure privileged access is used only when needed, by authorized people, for legitimate purposes, with full accountability. is distinct from general IAM — it specifically addresses the highest-risk accounts: local administrator accounts, domain administrator accounts, service accounts with elevated permissions, and root accounts.

🏦 Credential Vaulting

PAM solutions store privileged credentials in an encrypted vault. Users check out credentials for specific sessions — the actual password may never be revealed (session proxying). Automatic password rotation after check-in eliminates persistent credential exposure.

📹 Session Recording

All privileged sessions are recorded (keystrokes, screen, commands). Enables forensic reconstruction of administrator activity. Provides accountability — administrators know their actions are logged. Required for PCI DSS, HIPAA, and SOX compliance.

⏱️ Just-In-Time (JIT) Access

Privileged access is granted only when needed and automatically revoked after a time period. No standing privileged access. Reduces the window of opportunity for attackers who have compromised credentials.

🚨 Privileged Account Risks

Local Administrator accounts with the same password across systems enable lateral movement (Pass-the-Hash). Service accounts often have excessive permissions and never-expiring passwords. Domain Admin accounts used for daily work massively expand blast radius.

🛡️ Access Control Models

SY0-701 Objective 4.6 · 5.1

Access Control Frameworks

Access control models define the rules that determine who can access what resources under what conditions. The exam tests the ability to identify each model, its characteristics, and its appropriate use cases.

Discretionary Access Control (DAC)

✅ Characteristics

  • Resource owner decides who gets access
  • Most flexible model — easy to delegate
  • Standard for most OS file systems (NTFS, Linux permissions)
  • Access Control Lists (ACLs) implement DAC
  • Windows NTFS and Linux rwxr-xr-x are DAC

⚠️ Weaknesses

  • Relies on owner making correct decisions
  • Owners can share excessively — "share everything" tendency
  • Malware running as user inherits all user permissions
  • No central policy enforcement
  • Difficult to audit and manage at scale

Mandatory Access Control (MAC)

MAC uses security labels assigned by a central authority. Subjects have clearances; objects have classification levels. Bell-LaPadula Model (confidentiality): "No Read Up, No Write Down." Biba Model (integrity): "No Write Up, No Read Down."

Classification LevelAccess RuleExample System
Top SecretTS clearance requiredGovernment / Military systems
SecretSecret clearance requiredSELinux (enforcing mode)
ConfidentialConfidential clearanceTrusted OS (Trusted Solaris)
UnclassifiedGeneral accessAppArmor (Linux)
Exam Note

SELinux (Security-Enhanced Linux) and AppArmor implement MAC on Linux systems. Windows Integrity Levels (mandatory integrity control) provide a limited MAC-like system. MAC is most restrictive — users cannot override central policy.

Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)

Access rights are assigned to roles, not individual users. Users are assigned to roles. This significantly simplifies management — change a role definition and all role members are affected. Standard approach in most enterprise environments.

1
Define Roles: HR-Manager, Finance-User, IT-Admin, Developer-Prod
2
Assign Permissions to Roles: HR-Manager → read/write HR database, Finance-User → read-only GL
3
Assign Users to Roles: Alice → HR-Manager, Bob → Finance-User
4
Access Determined: By role membership, not individual grants
Privilege Creep

As employees change roles within an organization, they accumulate access rights from previous roles without old access being revoked. Over time, users have far more access than their current job requires. Mitigation: Periodic access reviews/recertification, automated de-provisioning on role change.

Attribute-Based Access Control (ABAC)

ABAC evaluates multiple attributes — user attributes (department, clearance), resource attributes (classification, owner), and environmental attributes (time, location, device) — to make dynamic access decisions. Enables very fine-grained, context-aware access control.

ABAC Policy Example

ALLOW access IF user.department = "Finance" AND user.clearance ≥ "Confidential" AND resource.type = "Financial_Report" AND environment.time BETWEEN 08:00–18:00 AND environment.location = "Corporate_Network"

Rule-Based Access Control

Distinct from RBAC — rules are applied globally, not tied to user roles. Firewall ACLs are rule-based: "Deny all traffic from IP range 10.0.0.0/8 to port 22." Time-of-day access restrictions are rule-based. Rules apply to everyone — not specific to individuals or roles.

📋 Rule-Based Examples

  • No access outside business hours
  • No access from foreign countries
  • Firewall permit/deny rules
  • NAC posture-based rules

🌐 ABAC in Practice

  • Zero Trust implementations
  • AWS IAM policies (condition keys)
  • Azure Conditional Access
  • XACML policy enforcement

Least Privilege & Need to Know

🔒 Principle of Least Privilege

Users, processes, and systems should have only the minimum permissions necessary to perform their required functions. Applies to user accounts, service accounts, processes, and applications. Reduces blast radius of compromised credentials.

🗂️ Need-to-Know

Even users with appropriate clearance/classification may only access information they have a demonstrated operational need for. Clearance is necessary but not sufficient — need-to-know is a separate gate.

⚠️ Separation of Duties

No single individual should have sufficient access to commit fraud or cause major harm alone. Two-person integrity, dual control. Example: the person who creates vendor records cannot also approve payments.

🔄 Job Rotation

Periodically rotating employees through different roles prevents monopolization of knowledge, detects collusion, and uncovers fraud. Mandatory vacation enforces temporary rotation and can surface hidden fraud.

👤 Account Management & Security

SY0-701 Objective 4.6 · 5.6

Account Types & Risks

👤 Standard User Accounts

Lowest privilege accounts for daily operations. Should NOT have local administrator rights. Implement least privilege — no admin rights on workstations unless operationally required.

⚡ Privileged / Admin Accounts

Domain Admin, Enterprise Admin, local Administrator. Highest risk — compromise gives complete control. Should have separate accounts (daily account + admin account), MFA required, PAM-managed, session recorded.

⚙️ Service Accounts

Accounts used by applications and services. Often over-privileged, rarely monitored, and have passwords that never expire. Kerberoasting targets service accounts. Use Managed Service Accounts (gMSA) or service principals instead.

👥 Shared / Generic Accounts

Multiple users sharing one account destroys accountability. Cannot trace actions to individuals. Strongly discouraged — use shared mailboxes/resources instead of shared accounts.

👻 Orphaned / Dormant Accounts

Accounts belonging to former employees or unused service accounts. Attack vectors — attacker uses dormant account to avoid detection (no login baseline anomaly). Automated deprovisioning is critical.

👤 Guest Accounts

Temporary access for non-employees. Minimum permissions, time-limited. Built-in Guest account on Windows should be disabled. Use dedicated guest Wi-Fi networks isolated from corporate resources.

DoS via Lockout

Account lockout policies can be weaponized — attackers intentionally trigger lockouts on all accounts during business hours to deny access. Mitigation: Implement CAPTCHA, adaptive lockout that increases delay rather than locking permanently, and alert on mass lockout events.

Time-of-Day and Location Restrictions

Restrict access to hours when users legitimately need it. A warehouse worker should not be accessing HR systems at 3 AM from a foreign country. GeofencingGeofencingDefines a virtual geographic perimeter and enforces policies when a device or user enters or exits it. Common in mobile device management (MDM) — can trigger alerts, disable camera, or require additional authentication when a device leaves the corporate campus. and impossible travel detection are key controls.

🕐 Time Restrictions

Logon hours policy limits when accounts can authenticate. Accounts with time restrictions automatically log off at end of allowed period. Applied via Group Policy in AD or IdP Conditional Access policies.

🌍 Geographic Controls

Block authentication from countries not part of normal operations. Detect "impossible travel" — authentication from two geographically distant locations within an impossible time window (2 hours apart, different continents).

Access Reviews & Recertification

Periodic formal reviews where managers attest that all access assigned to their team members is still appropriate and necessary. Required by SOX, PCI DSS, HIPAA, and ISO 27001.

  • Review all privileged account holders quarterly
  • Identify accounts not used in 90+ days — disable or remove
  • Verify role-to-access mapping for all groups
  • Confirm all service accounts have documented owners
  • Remove access inherited from previous roles (privilege creep)
  • Verify third-party/vendor accounts are still needed
  • Audit shared accounts — document all authorized users

    • Employee Offboarding — Account Deprovisioning

    Departing employee accounts must be promptly disabled to prevent unauthorized access. Speed is critical — many insider threats occur from terminated employees still retaining access.

    1
    Immediate: Disable AD account, revoke active sessions (invalidate tokens), change shared passwords
    2
    Within 24 hours: Remove from all security groups, revoke VPN certificates, disable email
    3
    Within 1 week: Transfer data ownership, archive mailbox, document service account changes
    4
    Within 30 days: Revoke cloud app access (SaaS that may not sync with AD), remove from distribution lists
    5
    Ongoing: Monitor for any residual access or unusual activity on former accounts
    Insider Threat Window

    The period between HR notification of termination and actual account deprovisioning is a critical risk window. Automated integration between HR systems and IAM/AD provisioning (via SCIM or HR connectors) minimizes this window. Manual processes create gaps measured in days or weeks.
