Module 01 · Setting the stage

Academic Integrity in the Age of AI

Generative AI didn't gradually arrive in higher education — it kicked the door in. Three years into widespread classroom impact, the consensus has shifted from "stop students from using it" to "teach them to use it well." This workshop is a working session to help you do that.

86%
of college students used AI for academic work in 2025
9%
false-positive rate for OpenAI's own (now shuttered) detector
24%
accuracy when humans manually try to detect AI writing
~50%
of US institutions have a formal AI policy as of late 2025
The most reliable AI detector is a well-designed assignment. Common refrain across 2025 EDUCAUSE and AAC&U guidance

What's changed since 2023

  • Detection has gotten worse, not better. Current models produce more diverse output; signatures detectors looked for have faded.
  • Multimodal AI is here. Students can generate code, images, audio, video, and structured data — not just text.
  • The "ban it" approach has largely failed. Most institutions that tried it have rolled back to permissive-with-disclosure policies.
  • Disclosure frameworks have matured. The AI Assessment Scale (AIAS) and AID Framework are emerging as standards.

The paradigm shift

  • From prohibition → integration with clear bounds
  • From detection → process-aware assessment
  • From individual policy → assignment-level granularity
  • From fear → curricular AI literacy
  • From "did they use AI?" → "did they learn?"
Workshop goal: You'll leave with a concrete AI policy draft for one of your courses, a redesigned assignment, a disclosure framework students can use, and scripts for the hard conversations you may need to have this semester.

Module 02 · What's actually at stake

What's at Stake — and What Isn't

Some concerns about AI in classrooms are well-founded. Others have been amplified beyond their actual risk profile. Separating them matters because faculty time is finite and student trust is fragile.

Concerns with strong evidence

🎯 Bypassed learning objectives

The clearest harm: students using AI to skip the cognitive work that an assignment was designed to develop. A student who has a chatbot write their argumentative essay hasn't practiced argumentation.

⚖️ Equity gaps in access & literacy

Paid tiers (GPT-5, Claude Opus, Gemini Advanced) outperform free versions substantially. Students with money or institutional access have a real advantage. Detection bias against non-native English speakers compounds this.

📚 Atrophied foundational skills

Emerging evidence (still early) suggests heavy AI reliance during early skill acquisition may impair retention. Less clear for advanced learners using AI to extend their reach.

🔒 Privacy & FERPA exposure

Faculty pasting student work into consumer AI tools may violate FERPA. Students pasting peers' work or their own personally identifiable info into chatbots adds further risk.

Concerns that are mostly overblown

"Detection will solve this"

It won't, and trying to make it solve this has caused real harm to innocent students. See Module 05.

"AI will replace writing instruction"

Writing instruction matters more now, not less. Students still need to read, evaluate, and revise — including AI output.

"This is just like calculators"

It's not — calculators do bounded math; generative AI produces open-ended content that can be plausibly wrong. But the metaphor isn't wrong about policy direction: integrate, teach the limits, design assessments accordingly.

The biggest risk in the room right now: a faculty member making an unfounded AI-use accusation against a student based on a detector flag or gut feeling. This causes documented harm, often to the most marginalized students, and is increasingly the source of lawsuits and institutional grievances.

Module 03 · Actionable ethics

Ethics Frameworks You Can Actually Use

"Be ethical" is not a policy. Faculty and students both need concrete frameworks that translate principles into decisions. These are the four most useful frameworks for classroom AI ethics — each grounded in established ethical theory, each producing different actionable guidance.

The Transparency Principle

If you wouldn't say it out loud, don't do it. Any AI use that a student would hide from their instructor probably crosses an ethics line — not because AI use is bad, but because the hiding signals the student believes the work misrepresents their effort.

Actionable test:

  1. The Disclosure Test: Could you describe exactly how AI was used in a 2-sentence footnote at the end of the assignment?
  2. The Conversation Test: If your instructor asked "walk me through how you wrote this," could you do it honestly?
  3. The Process Test: Could you produce the drafts, prompts, and revisions that led to this final work?
What this looks like in practice: A required AI Disclosure Statement on every assignment, where students name the tool, describe the use, and reflect on what they learned. Not surveillance — it's the same standard we apply to citing sources.
Transparency reframes "Did you use AI?" from a gotcha question into a routine part of describing your process.

The Attribution Principle

Intellectual honesty has always required acknowledging help. AI is help. The major citation styles now have formal AI citation formats — APA 7, MLA 9, and Chicago 17 all updated their guidance in 2024–2025.

The three layers of attribution:

  1. Substantive citation — when AI generated specific content (a passage, an argument, a code block), cite it like any other source.
  2. Process disclosure — when AI shaped how the work was produced (brainstorming, outlining, editing), disclose the role even if no specific output is in the final work.
  3. Prompt archiving — for substantial AI use, save and submit the prompt chain so the process is reviewable.

Standard AI citation (APA 7, 2024 update):

OpenAI. (2025). ChatGPT (GPT-5 version, October 2025) [Large language model]. https://chat.openai.com

In-text: (OpenAI, 2025)

Important: AI output is not a stable source. Two students using "the same prompt" get different outputs. Citation alone isn't enough — substantial AI use needs a process disclosure too.

The Purpose-Fit Principle

Every assignment has a learning purpose. AI use is ethical when it serves that purpose and unethical when it bypasses it. This is the most-discipline specific framework — what counts as "the purpose" varies enormously.

Three diagnostic questions:

  1. What skill is this assignment trying to develop? (Not what topic — what skill.)
  2. Does AI use here practice that skill, substitute for it, or amplify it?
  3. Could a student honestly say "I got better at X by doing this with AI?"

✅ Purpose-fit example

Assignment: Write a literature review.

Goal: Practice synthesizing competing scholarly positions.

Ethical AI use: Asking Claude to summarize an article you've read, then comparing the summary to your own reading. Practices the skill (synthesis) by adding a check on your interpretation.

❌ Purpose-mismatched example

Same assignment.

Same goal.

Unethical AI use: Asking Claude to write the synthesis section from a list of titles. Bypasses the skill entirely — student never did the synthesis.

This is why blanket policies are weak. "AI prohibited" or "AI allowed" misses the point — fitness depends on what each assignment is trying to teach. Use the AI Assessment Scale (Module 06) to set assignment-level policies.

The Equity Principle

An AI policy that disadvantages students based on cost, language, disability, or background is unethical regardless of academic-integrity rationale. This is where many well-intentioned policies fail.

Equity considerations to audit:

  1. Access: Are you requiring or permitting AI tools that have a paid tier students need? Is institutional access provided?
  2. Language: Non-native English speakers benefit most from AI editing assistance — does your policy let them use it for that without flagging them as "cheaters"?
  3. Disability: AI tools provide significant accommodation for students with dyslexia, ADHD, and processing differences. Restrictive policies may violate ADA.
  4. Detection bias: Detectors flag non-native English writing as AI at rates 2-3× higher than native writing. Using detector results disproportionately harms these students.
  5. Privacy cost: Free AI tools train on user input. Students who refuse to surrender their work to corporate training datasets shouldn't be penalized for it.
Legal note: Disparate impact in AI detection enforcement may create Title VI and ADA liability. UCLA and several UC campuses declined to adopt Turnitin AI detection specifically citing these concerns.

Module 04 · Decisions in the wild

Scenarios & Practice

Frameworks matter most when you have to apply them under pressure. Work through these scenarios alone or as a workshop group. Each reveals an analysis after you've formed your own answer.

Scenario 1 · Writing course

A student in your composition course submits a polished argumentative essay. The argument is sophisticated, the prose clean — much better than her in-class writing. She tells you she "just had ChatGPT clean up my grammar." Your AI policy permits grammar/style assistance.

What the frameworks tell you

Transparency: She told you. That matters — she didn't try to hide it.

Purpose-fit: The harder question. If the assignment's purpose was argumentation, "cleaning up grammar" is permitted. But the gap between her in-class voice and submitted voice suggests more than grammar happened.

Equity: If she's a non-native English speaker, grammar assistance is exactly what AI is best at — penalizing this disadvantages her.

Action: Don't accuse. Schedule a brief conference. Ask her to walk you through her draft history and her prompt to ChatGPT. The conversation is the assessment.

Lesson learned for next time: "Grammar assistance" is fuzzy. Tighten policy to specify what level of revision is in-bounds, and require submission of the original draft alongside the polished version.

Scenario 2 · STEM lab

Two students in your networking lab submit nearly identical Python scripts for analyzing packet captures. The code works, has unusual stylistic similarities (same variable names, same comments in the same places), and includes a function neither student can explain when asked. Your policy permits AI assistance for "debugging and explanation, not initial code generation."

What the frameworks tell you

Purpose-fit: The function they can't explain is the key signal — not the similarity. If they can't explain what their code does, they haven't learned what the assignment was teaching.

Transparency: Was AI use disclosed? Your policy required disclosure for permitted use. Lack of disclosure plus inability to explain = academic integrity issue.

Action: Don't lead with "did you use AI." Lead with "explain this function to me." Their answer will be diagnostic. If they can't, you have legitimate grounds — not for AI use, but for not meeting the learning objective. The remedy is rework, not punishment.

Process check: This is why "oral verification" components on technical assignments work so well — they're robust to both AI use and traditional copying.

Scenario 3 · Research paper

A student's final paper cites three sources you can't find. The titles, authors, and journal names sound plausible. Web searches turn up nothing. The student says they got the citations from "a research helper AI" they used to find sources.

What the frameworks tell you

The diagnosis: AI hallucinated the citations. This is one of the most common, well-documented failure modes of LLMs. The student didn't verify the sources existed.

Not primarily an integrity issue — primarily a literacy issue. The student didn't deceive; they trusted output they should have verified. This is exactly the AI literacy gap your course should be addressing.

Action: Show the student that the citations don't exist. Treat as a teachable moment about verification. Require resubmission with verified sources. Use this as a class-wide lesson on AI hallucination.

Policy update: Add an explicit "you are responsible for verifying any source AI provides" clause to your policy. Make verification a graded process step.

Scenario 4 · Discussion forum

In your online course, a student's discussion posts are dramatically more polished than their in-class participation when you do synchronous sessions. Their posts read like they could have been written by AI. A detection tool flags them at 87% likely AI. You have no other evidence.

What the frameworks tell you

The detector score is not evidence. 13% false-positive rates exist. Acting on the detector alone is the most legally and ethically dangerous move available to you.

The "polished posts vs. messy speech" gap is normal. Students write differently than they speak. Many students who struggle in synchronous sessions write well asynchronously — neurodivergent students especially.

Action: Do not accuse. Do nothing punitive based on this evidence. If you want to assess authenticity, redesign your discussion structure: add a brief synchronous response component, or a follow-up "describe a question your post raised" prompt.

Hard truth: Some students will use AI for discussion posts. The right response is structural (better assignment design) not investigative (detection-based enforcement).

Scenario 5 · Group project

A student approaches you privately: "My group is using ChatGPT to write our project, and I don't think that's allowed, but I don't want to get them in trouble." Your policy on AI use for this assignment is "limited brainstorming only."

What the frameworks tell you

The student is asking for help, not reporting a violation. Lead with that.

Equity: They're being asked to participate in something that violates policy. They lose either way — submit work that violates the policy, or refuse and damage their team.

Action: Thank them for raising it. Don't ask them to surveil. Instead, address the whole class: "I've heard about some confusion regarding group projects and AI use. Let me clarify expectations and provide an opportunity for any group to revise their approach before submission." This protects the reporting student and gives the group a face-saving exit.

For policy: Group assignments need especially explicit AI policies because diffusion of responsibility creates more violations.

Pattern across scenarios: In every case, the strongest move is conversation, process evidence, and structural redesign — not detection or punishment. Investigative posture damages trust; structural posture builds learning culture.

  2. What does your current policy say about each scenario type — and is it specific enough?

Module 05 · Hard reality check

AI Detection: What the 2025–2026 Research Actually Shows

If you take only one thing from this workshop, take this: AI detection tools are not reliable enough to base academic-integrity decisions on. The research is unambiguous, the institutional consensus is shifting fast, and continuing to use these tools as evidence is increasingly a liability.

The bottom line: OpenAI shut down its own AI detector in 2023 because it correctly identified only 26% of AI text while falsely flagging 9% of human writing. Independent research published in 2025 shows the situation has not meaningfully improved — in some respects it has worsened as models have improved.

What 2025 research found

Detection accuracy is variable and often poor

A 2025 evaluation of mainstream detectors (Turnitin, GPTZero, Copyleaks, Originality.AI, ZeroGPT) found that simple paraphrasing defeats most tools. Performance varies wildly by content type — formal lab reports and structured business writing get false-flagged most.

Bias is systematic, not incidental

Multiple peer-reviewed studies (2024–2025) confirm non-native English speakers are flagged as AI at substantially elevated rates. The patterns the detectors associate with AI — simpler vocabulary, predictable syntax — are also features of writing by anyone still acquiring English.

Humans aren't better than detectors

A 2025 study found human evaluators distinguish AI from human writing only slightly better than chance. Faculty "gut feelings" about AI use are unreliable evidence and disproportionately target students who write in atypical styles.

Major institutions are backing away

UCLA, the broader UC system, and many institutions nationwide have declined to enable Turnitin's AI detection. The trend is toward process-aware assessment and conversation-based verification rather than algorithmic scoring.

AI-generated text cannot be reliably detected, and simple paraphrasing is sufficient to evade detection. University of Maryland Computer Science research, replicated multiple times 2023–2025

What the tools claim vs. what's true

Tool Marketing claim Independent finding
Turnitin AI "<1% false positives" Independent testing shows 4–15% false positives depending on text type and length
GPTZero High accuracy across content types Inconsistent. Famously flagged the US Constitution and the Bible as AI-generated
Originality.AI "99% accuracy" Independent testing showed much lower accuracy on edited or paraphrased AI text
Copyleaks, ZeroGPT, others Various accuracy claims Same fundamental limitations; performance degrades as AI models improve

What to do instead

STEP 1
Design assessments that don't need detection. Process-aware, oral-component, personal-context assignments make detection irrelevant.
↓
STEP 2
Require disclosure. If AI use is permitted (per your policy), require students to state how they used it. Skip-the-disclosure becomes the integrity issue, not the AI use.
↓
STEP 3
Use conversation to verify. Brief conferences, oral defenses, or follow-up questions can verify learning without algorithmic accusations.
↓
STEP 4
If you must use a detector — never as sole evidence. Treat a detector flag as a prompt for conversation, never as proof. Document this in your policy.
Before you escalate: Before initiating any academic integrity case based partly on AI detection, consult your Dean's office, academic integrity officer, or teaching and learning center. A detector score alone has not held up well in formal appeals at peer institutions.
Module 06 · The emerging standard

The AI Assessment Scale (AIAS)

The AI Assessment Scale, developed by Perkins, Furze, Roe and MacVaugh, is becoming the de facto standard for communicating AI-use expectations at the assignment level. Rather than a single course-wide policy, AIAS lets you set different expectations for different assessments — matching policy to learning purpose.

From Autumn 2025, AIAS is embedded as a default in Canvas modules at many universities including the University of Stirling, and is being adopted at the individual-course and program level across community colleges and four-year institutions in the US.

1
No AI
AI use is not permitted for any aspect of this assessment. Work must be entirely the student's own.
2
AI Planning
AI may be used for brainstorming, outlining, and structuring ideas — but not for generating final content.
3
AI Collaboration
AI may be used throughout the process for editing, refinement, and feedback. Student remains author.
4
Full AI
AI may generate substantial content. Student curates, verifies, and adds critical analysis.
5
AI Exploration
AI use is required. Assessment focuses on prompt engineering, output evaluation, and metacognition about AI.

How to use AIAS in your syllabus

Label each major assessment with its AIAS level in your syllabus and on the assignment instructions:

Assignment 3: Annotated Bibliography

AIAS Level: 2 (AI Planning)

You may use AI tools to help identify potential sources and develop search strategies. You must read, summarize, and evaluate each source yourself. AI may not write any annotation text.

The AID Framework: a complement to AIAS

The Artificial Intelligence Disclosure (AID) Framework, developed by Weaver et al. and adopted by the ACRL, provides a structured way for students to disclose AI use:

AID Statement template

"I acknowledge the use of [AI system name and version] (link) to [specific purpose]. The prompts used include [list of prompts]. The output from these prompts was used to [explain final use]."

Example completed AID statement:

"I acknowledge the use of Claude (Anthropic, accessed November 2025, https://claude.ai) to brainstorm counterarguments to my main thesis. The prompts I used: 'What are the three strongest objections to the claim that...' The output was used as a checklist while I drafted Section 3 of my essay. All argument text is my own."

Why AIAS + AID works: AIAS sets the expectation (what's permitted), AID provides the disclosure mechanism (how to communicate what was done). Together they make the conversation about AI use routine and structured instead of suspicion-based.

What every AI policy should include

1. The "why"

Explain the rationale. Students who understand the reasoning are more likely to comply. "Because I said so" doesn't work.

2. Specific examples

"AI for brainstorming is fine" is too abstract. Give 2-3 concrete examples of permitted use and 2-3 examples of impermissible use.

3. Disclosure mechanism

Where, when, and how should students disclose AI use? Without this specified, "disclosure required" is unenforceable.

4. What happens if

What's the consequence of undisclosed AI use? What's the consequence of policy questions raised in good faith? Process matters.

Module 08 · Design out the problem

Designing AI-Resilient Authentic Assessment

The most durable response to generative AI isn't policy or detection — it's assessment design. Authentic assessment was good pedagogy before AI existed. It happens to also be AI-resilient.

Core principle: An AI-resilient assessment is one where AI use either (a) doesn't bypass the learning goal, or (b) becomes evident through the assessment's own structure — not through detection technology.

Five design moves that work

1. Make process visible

Require students to submit drafts, notes, outlines, prompt logs, and revision histories alongside the final product. Use Google Docs version history, Microsoft Word's track changes, or a structured process portfolio.

Why it works: AI-only work has no draft history. Real learning leaves traces.

2. Add an oral component

A 5-minute discussion of the work after submission. Could be a brief office-hours conference, an in-class lightning presentation, or a recorded Loom video answering 2-3 questions about choices the student made.

Why it works: Students who didn't do the thinking can't fluently explain the thinking. This is the single most powerful AI-resilience technique.

3. Anchor in personal or local context

"Analyze a network in your home or workplace." "Apply this framework to a current event you care about." "Interview someone in your intended profession." Tasks that draw on specific situated knowledge.

Why it works: AI can produce generic analysis. It can't produce your student's specific analysis of their specific context.

4. Build in iterative critique

Peer review, instructor feedback rounds, revision cycles. The final grade reflects growth across iterations, not the polish of a single submission.

Why it works: Iteration practices the actual cognitive skills your course is teaching, and AI-only work tends not to incorporate prior feedback meaningfully.

5. Assess the AI use itself

At AIAS levels 4-5, the assignment is partly about how well the student uses AI: prompt quality, output evaluation, verification, integration. The skill is no longer hidden — it's the explicit subject.

Why it works: Treats AI literacy as a teachable competency rather than a cheating risk.

Examples by discipline

Engineering & Technology

  • Lab notebooks with photographed in-process work and reflection on troubleshooting decisions
  • Configuration challenges with oral defense of design choices
  • Real-world problem identification in students' workplaces or local environments
  • Critique tasks: "Here's AI-generated code that has a subtle bug — find it and explain it"

Writing & Humanities

  • Portfolios with reflective metacommentary on each piece
  • Op-eds on local/personal issues with stakeholder feedback
  • Revision-based assessment where the grade comes from how the student responded to feedback
  • "Steelman" arguments where students must argue positions they disagree with

STEM (math, science)

  • Whiteboard problem-solving sessions, recorded or in-person
  • Original problem creation: "Write a problem that requires using both X and Y techniques, and solve it"
  • Error analysis on AI-generated solutions
  • Lab investigations with data the student collected themselves

Cybersecurity / IT

  • Capture-the-flag and hands-on lab demonstrations
  • Incident response simulations requiring real-time decision-making
  • Threat modeling for actual systems the student designed
  • Code review of AI-generated code, with required identification of security issues

Module 09 · The hard conversations

Conversation Scripts: When You Need Words

The single most fraught moment in AI-and-academic-integrity work is the conversation with a student you suspect — or know — has misused AI. Here are scripts. Adapt the wording; keep the structure.

Universal principle: Lead with curiosity, not accusation. Ask the student to walk you through their process. Their answer is your evidence — not the detector score, not your gut feeling.

Conversation 1: Inviting disclosure (proactive)

Best version: this happens before any specific case arises. Have it with the whole class at the start of every term.

You — opening class statement
"I want to be direct with you about AI tools in this course. They exist, you have access to them, and you're going to use them somewhere in your life. My goal isn't to catch you using AI — it's to make sure that when you graduate from this course, you've actually learned the things this course is designed to teach. Some of those things require you to do the work without AI help. Others might actually be better with AI help. The syllabus spells out which is which for each assignment. If you're ever unsure, ask. If you use AI in a way that's not permitted and you tell me — even after the fact — we'll talk about it as a learning issue, not a misconduct issue. The thing I won't accept is undisclosed use."

Conversation 2: When a student's work raises questions

Don't open with "did you use AI?" That's adversarial and produces lies. Open with the work.

You — opening the meeting
"Thanks for coming in. I want to talk about your paper because there's something interesting about it I want to understand better. Can you walk me through how you developed the argument in your second section? I'm curious about how you got from the source material to your specific conclusion."
If their answer is fluent and detailed
"That makes sense. Thanks for explaining. I had some questions about the writing style but you've clarified that for me. The piece is well done."
If they can't explain their own work
"I'm noticing you're having some difficulty walking me through this. I want to be straightforward: the work you submitted reads as though it was written by someone who has a clear analysis, but you're having trouble describing that analysis to me right now. That gap is something we need to address — not because I'm trying to accuse you of anything, but because something isn't lining up between the work and what you can explain. Tell me what happened with this assignment."

This phrasing creates space for the student to disclose without forcing a confession. It also focuses on the verifiable issue (can't explain own work) rather than the unverifiable one (used AI).

Conversation 3: When a detector flags a paper

Do not lead with the detector score. The detector is not evidence; it's an unreliable prompt for further investigation.

You — what NOT to say
"Turnitin says your paper is 87% AI-generated. Did you use AI to write this?"

Why this is wrong: You've staked your authority on a tool with documented bias and a high false-positive rate. If the student didn't use AI, you've now insulted them based on bad evidence. If they did, you've given them an easy out ("the detector is wrong").

You — better version
"I want to spend a few minutes on your paper. Can you walk me through how you wrote the introduction? I'd love to hear about your drafting process and what changed between your first version and what you turned in."

You're never actually citing the detector to the student. You're using its flag as a prompt to do what you should be doing on a sample basis anyway: asking students about their process.

Conversation 4: A student admits to undisclosed AI use

This is the moment to model the response you'd want. Punitive responses to disclosure teach future students to hide better.

Student
"Okay. I did use ChatGPT to write most of it. I didn't think it would matter."
You
"Thank you for telling me directly. I appreciate that, and I want to be clear that telling me makes this conversation different from the one we'd have had if I'd discovered it on my own. Let me explain what I see as the issue. The point of this assignment was to practice [specific skill]. AI doing it for you means you didn't practice that skill — that's the problem, not the AI use itself. Here's what I'd like to do: I'd like you to redo the assignment with [specific structural constraint that ensures the practice happens], and we'll grade it on that. The grade on the AI version doesn't count. Does that work for you?"

This response acknowledges the disclosure as meaningful, names the actual problem (skill not practiced), and provides a path back that's about learning. It doesn't pretend nothing happened, but it doesn't escalate to misconduct procedures either.

Conversation 5: A student tells you a peer is using AI inappropriately

You
"Thank you for bringing this to me. I want to ask first — are you in a difficult position with this group? My priority right now is making sure you're okay with how the project is going. I'm not going to ask you to gather evidence on anyone or report further. If there's a policy issue with the group, I'll address it with the whole class, and your name won't come up."

Then address the class, not the individual group, with revised expectations and a chance to reset.

What to avoid in every conversation

❌ Don't lead with the detector

Algorithmic accusations damage trust and rarely hold up under appeal.

❌ Don't bluff

"I know you used AI" without evidence creates lawsuits, not learning.

❌ Don't make it about cheating

Make it about whether learning happened. That's the actually-important question, and it's the question process evidence can answer.

❌ Don't punish disclosure

A student who admits to AI use is doing something honest. Match that with proportional response or you'll teach the next class to hide.

Where we end

The goal isn't to keep AI out of higher education — that ship has sailed.
