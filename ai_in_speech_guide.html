The Landscape: AI & Student Presentations

AI tools can now generate entire slide decks ‚Äî content, design, and images ‚Äî from a single prompt. For speech faculty, this raises an important question: how do we ensure students are still doing the intellectual and rhetorical work that speech courses are designed to teach?

What AI Presentation Tools Can Do Today

Current AI presentation tools operate across a spectrum. At the basic level, they can suggest layouts and design elements for content you provide. At the most advanced level, tools like Beautiful.ai, Gamma, and Canva Magic Design can take a single topic prompt and produce a complete slide deck with original content, generated images, and professionally designed layouts ‚Äî in under two minutes.

Beyond the dedicated tools, the major LLMs? Large Language Models ‚Äî AI systems like Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini that can generate text, code, and now create files like PowerPoint presentations directly. ‚Äî Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini ‚Äî can now generate downloadable .pptx files from within a chat conversation. This means a student could paste their assignment prompt into a chatbot and receive a finished presentation file.

Why This Matters for Speech Courses

Speech courses are fundamentally different from courses that assign presentations as a way to display information. In speech, the process matters as much as the product. Students must learn to analyze an audience, organize arguments, select supporting evidence, craft transitions, and make deliberate visual choices that support ‚Äî not replace ‚Äî their spoken delivery.

When AI generates the entire presentation, students bypass the rhetorical thinking that is the core learning outcome. However, when AI is used strategically, it can be a powerful tool that enhances learning rather than replacing it.

üí° Key Principle
The question is not "should students use AI?" but rather "at what stage of the speech preparation process should AI be permitted, and what must the student still do themselves?" This guide helps you answer that question for your specific courses.

The Two Big Risks

1. Content Integrity

If AI generates the speech content itself ‚Äî the arguments, the evidence, the analysis ‚Äî the student has not practiced the research and critical thinking skills that speech courses develop. AI-generated content also frequently includes inaccurate claims, fabricated statistics, and generic reasoning that would not pass disciplinary scrutiny.

2. Visual Dependency

When AI creates polished, image-heavy slides, students may lean on the slides as a crutch rather than developing strong delivery skills. The slide deck becomes the presentation, rather than a visual aid that supports the presenter. In worst cases, students read AI-generated slide text verbatim.

The AI Use Spectrum for Speech Presentations

Not all AI use is equal. This spectrum helps you decide what level of AI involvement aligns with your course learning outcomes. Your position on this spectrum may vary by assignment type ‚Äî a practice informative speech might allow more AI support than a final persuasive speech.

Student does all work AI does all work
Level 1 No AI Permitted
Student creates all content, design, and visuals
Level 2 AI as Brainstorm Partner
Student uses AI for idea generation only; all final content is original
Level 3 AI for Design Only
Student writes all content; AI handles layout and visual design
Level 4 AI Co-Creation
Student provides outline/key points; AI expands and designs
Level 5 Full AI Generation
AI creates content, design, and visuals from a topic prompt

Choosing the Right Level for Your Assignment

Persuasive & Argumentative Speeches

Recommended: Level 1‚Äì2. The core skill is constructing an original argument with evidence the student has evaluated. AI should not generate the persuasive reasoning. Students may use AI to brainstorm topics or counterarguments, but all claims and evidence should be student-sourced and verified.

Informative & Expository Speeches

Recommended: Level 2‚Äì3. Students must demonstrate research skills and content organization. AI can assist with brainstorming and slide design after the student has written the content. Some faculty may permit AI for polishing language on individual slides ‚Äî but not for generating the explanatory content itself.

Special Occasion & Ceremonial Speeches

Recommended: Level 1‚Äì2. These speeches are deeply personal (tributes, toasts, introductions). The authenticity of the student's voice is the primary assessment criterion. AI involvement undermines the fundamental purpose.

Group Presentations

Recommended: Level 2‚Äì3. AI can help unify visual design across slides created by different group members ‚Äî a legitimate use that mirrors professional practice. Content should still be student-generated and attributed to specific group members.

Practice / Low-Stakes Assignments

Recommended: Level 3‚Äì4. For early or practice speeches focused on delivery skills, you may permit more AI involvement with slide content so students can focus on vocal variety, eye contact, gestures, and timing. Make clear that this level of AI use will not be permitted on graded speeches.

‚úÖ A Useful Distinction
"Provide the content, let AI design the slides" is a reasonable middle ground for many assignments. The student still does the intellectual work ‚Äî researching, organizing, writing ‚Äî while AI handles the visual polish. This mirrors how professionals often work: they prepare the content and hand it to a designer (or a tool) for formatting. The key is requiring students to submit their written content separately from the final slides.

Assignment Design: Keeping Students in the Driver's Seat

The best defense against AI misuse is assignment design that makes AI-generated work either impossible or obviously inferior to authentic student work. These strategies work across speech types.

Strategy 1: Require a Process Portfolio

Instead of grading only the final presentation, require students to submit evidence of their process at multiple checkpoints. This makes it very difficult (and very time-consuming) to use AI for the entire workflow.

Suggested Checkpoints:

  • Topic proposal & audience analysis ‚Äî Student identifies their topic, states their thesis or purpose, and analyzes the specific audience they will address.
  • Research log with annotated sources ‚Äî Student submits a log of sources consulted, with 2‚Äì3 sentence annotations explaining how each source will be used.
  • Formal outline (student-written) ‚Äî Full-sentence outline showing the organizational pattern, main points, sub-points, transitions, and source citations.
  • Slide content draft (text only) ‚Äî The text that will appear on each slide, submitted as a plain document before any design tool is used.
  • Final presentation with reflection ‚Äî The delivered speech plus a written reflection on choices made during the process.

Strategy 2: Anchor to Personal Experience & Local Context

AI cannot speak from personal experience. Assignments that require students to draw on their own lives, local community, or real observations are inherently AI-resistant.

Examples:

Informative: "Explain a skill, process, or cultural practice that you have personal expertise in. Your presentation must include at least one personal anecdote and a demonstration component."

Persuasive: "Identify a problem in our campus community or local area. Interview at least two stakeholders, and propose a specific, actionable solution. Your evidence must include primary data you collected."

Special Occasion: "Deliver a tribute speech about someone who has personally influenced your life. Include specific memories, dialogue, and details that only someone with a personal connection would know."

Strategy 3: Live Justification & Q&A

After the speech, require students to answer follow-up questions about their content and process. A student who genuinely prepared their own presentation can easily explain their choices; a student who submitted AI-generated work will struggle.

Sample Follow-Up Questions:

"Why did you choose this organizational pattern over other options?"

"Walk me through how you found and evaluated your third source."

"What was the most challenging part of adapting your content for this audience?"

"If you used AI for any part of this presentation, describe what you asked for and how you modified the output."

Strategy 4: Require Delivery-Specific Elements

AI can build slides, but it cannot deliver a speech. Build assessment criteria that weight delivery and live interaction heavily.

Delivery-Centered Requirements:

  • Extemporaneous delivery from a keyword outline ‚Äî Not reading from slides or a script. The student must speak from notes, demonstrating command of the material.
  • Audience interaction ‚Äî Require at least one moment of direct audience engagement (a question, a poll, a demonstration volunteer).
  • Adaptation in real time ‚Äî During practice or peer review rounds, ask students to adjust one element based on peer feedback before final delivery.
  • Self-assessment video review ‚Äî Students watch a recording of their own speech and write a targeted self-critique with specific timestamps.
üí° The "Separation Principle"
Consider requiring that content creation and slide creation happen as separate, documented steps. When students must submit their written content before they create slides, you can verify the intellectual work happened first. If AI is then used for slide design only, the core learning outcomes are preserved.
