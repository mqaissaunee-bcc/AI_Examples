London artists, students, and other scribblers take up "plumbago drawing" as merchants hawk pieces of graphite on the street. The tools don't come with erasers, though. One unnamed 1612 writer recommends rubbing out the marks with "crums of wheate bred."

Con artists called "stumplers" boost the fake pencil trade by blackening the tips of wooden sticks, making it look like there's graphite inside. Frustrated customers soon discover they've been duped into buying expensive kindling.
