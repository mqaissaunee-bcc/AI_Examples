What Academic Pro Does

A tool for the professional side of working in higher education — not course content, but the documents, decisions, and communications that keep an institution running.

Most AI tools drop you in front of a blank text box and expect you to write your own prompt from scratch. Academic Pro takes a different approach: it assembles a structured, context-rich prompt for you by gathering your role, task type, audience, format preferences, and constraints — then hands the complete prompt off to your preferred AI platform.

The result is a much more precise prompt than most people write on their own, which means better, more useful AI output on the first try.

Who is this for?
Faculty & Instructors
Grant writing, letters of rec, tenure narratives, peer review, course proposals, scholarly writing
Administrators
Policy drafts, committee minutes, budget justifications, faculty senate motions, governance documents
Advisors & Counselors
AIPs, early alert emails, referral letters, degree plan communications, student-facing templates
Staff Specialists
HR documents, institutional communications, assessment narratives, accreditation materials, SLO reports
💡
Academic Pro vs. Prompt ArchitectBoth tools live in the Demystifying AI suite. Prompt Architect is for faculty building student-facing interactive learning tools. Academic Pro is for the professional and institutional work — grant writing, advising documentation, HR, accreditation, and more — that everyone at a college does.

Quick Start Mode

Build a usable prompt in about 30 seconds — then refine with the full builder below.

At the very top of the builder, above Panel 1, you'll find a collapsed ⚡ Quick Start banner. Click it to expand. Inside are just three fields — enough to generate a solid, usable prompt right away.

1
Your role — dropdown
The same 8 role options from Panel 2, in a compact dropdown. Pick the hat you're wearing for this task.
2
Task type + specific task — two linked dropdowns
Select a task category (e.g., Academic Writing & Documents). The second dropdown immediately populates with all the subtypes for that category — so you can get as specific as Letter of Recommendation or Grant Narrative without clicking through the card grid.
3
One sentence — what do you need?
A single textarea. Write one sentence describing your task. Even a brief description here makes a real difference in output quality.
What happens when you click "Build My Prompt"

Two things happen simultaneously:

✅ Prompt assembled instantly in the output panel ✅ Full builder panels pre-filled below

You get a working prompt right away — and the full builder is already loaded with your Quick Start choices, so you can drop in your audience, institution type, discipline, output format preferences, and anything else without starting from scratch.

💡
Quick Start is the fastest path for new users. For faculty or staff who are hesitant about prompting, having just three fields to fill removes the "where do I even start?" barrier. Once the prompt appears, curiosity usually takes over and they start exploring the full builder.

Panel 1 · Task Category

The starting point. Choose the broad category that best describes what you need to produce.

Academic Pro organizes work into 9 task categories, each with 14–26 specific subtypes. Clicking a category card immediately reveals those subtypes as clickable chips below the grid.

✍️ Academic Writing & DocumentsLetters of rec, grant proposals, tenure narratives, sabbatical proposals, OER adoption, course proposals
🔬 Research & ScholarshipLiterature reviews, IRB applications, methodology sections, dissemination strategy, CITI compliance correspondence

How subtypes work

After clicking a category, a row of chip buttons appears showing every specific task type in that category. Click a subtype chip to select it — this tells the AI exactly what kind of document you're producing, not just the broad area. You can change your selection at any time.

ℹ️
You don't have to select a subtype. If none of the chips exactly match your task, you can skip this step and describe your task in the "What do you need help with?" field in Panel 3. The category selection alone adds useful context to your prompt.

Panel 2 · Your Role

Tell the AI which professional hat you're wearing. This shapes the voice, register, and assumptions in the output.

The 8 role/persona buttons each inject a brief context sentence into your prompt that orients the AI. A faculty member writing a grant gets a different prompt framing than an HR specialist writing the same request. The role isn't locked — you can always click a different one to rebuild your prompt instantly.

📚 Faculty / Instructor
Full-time, adjunct, or lecturer roles. Teaching, scholarship, service.
🏢 Academic Administrator
Department chairs, deans, directors, program coordinators.
🗺 Advisor / Counselor
Academic advising, career counseling, personal counseling.
🔭 Researcher / Scholar
PIs, postdocs, research staff, institutional researchers.
📖 Librarian / Archivist
Reference, subject librarianship, special collections, archives.
🌐 Student Affairs
Housing, wellness, student life, DEI offices, student engagement.
💻 IT / Instructional Tech
LMS support, instructional design, educational technology.
🤝 HR / Staff Specialist
Human resources, finance, marketing, communications staff.
Contextual position dropdown + additional role context field

Once you click a role card, a contextual "Position / title" dropdown appears below the grid — automatically populated with specific titles relevant to your role. For example:

Faculty → Adjunct / Part-time · Full-time Tenure-track · Lecturer · Department Chair Admin → Dean · Director · Coordinator · Provost / VP Academic Affairs Advisor → Academic Advisor · Transfer Counselor · Veterans Services

Selecting from the dropdown auto-fills the additional role context text field below it — which you can still edit freely to add anything more specific: "chair of the Curriculum Committee," "nursing faculty at a community college doing pre-tenure review," or "co-PI on a Title III grant." This text gets woven directly into your prompt.

💡
The role field is optional but impactful. An AI that knows it's helping a community college advisor write a FERPA-compliant early alert email will produce something very different from a generic "write an email" prompt. Even a broad role selection is worth clicking.

Panel 3 · Task Context & Details

The most important panel. The more specific you are here, the better your AI output will be.

Panel 3 collects the contextual information that separates a generic AI output from something genuinely useful. There are six fields — some required, most optional — each adding precision to your assembled prompt. Two of them have been upgraded with smarter controls to make selection faster.

1
What do you need help with? (most important field)
Describe your task in plain language. Be specific: name the student, grant, program, or situation. Include relevant details — GPA, program type, institutional context, goals. A longer, richer description produces measurably better output than a short one. The character counter in the lower right tracks your input length.
2
Primary audience / recipient
This is a multi-select dropdown — you can pick more than one audience. Selecting "granting agency / foundation" and "DEI office" together tells the AI it needs to address both. See Section 6 for full details on the multi-select widget.
3
Institution type
Community college? R1 research university? HBCU? This context matters — a policy draft for a large public research university reads differently than one for a tribal college. Select the option that best matches your institution.
4
Discipline / field — grouped dropdown
This is now a grouped dropdown organized into seven discipline families: Health & Human Services, STEM, Business & Professional, Humanities & Arts, Social Sciences & Education, Trades & CTE, and Student & Academic Affairs. Select the closest match — or choose "Other — describe below" to reveal a free-text field for anything more specific or unusual. Field-specific vocabulary and framing matters a great deal for professional documents.
5
Key constraints, requirements, or context — with quick-insert chips
Word limits, template requirements, institutional style guides, specific formats, legal context, or anything else that shapes what the AI should produce. Above the textarea you'll find a row of quick-insert chips — click any to append it instantly: 500-word limit, Follow college template, Confidential, Must cite sources, Formal register, FERPA language, Action items, Plain language, ADA / accessibility. Chips append with proper comma spacing; the textarea stays fully editable.
6
Target length
A row of toggle buttons lets you set a length target: ~150 words, ~250 words, ~500 words, ~750 words, 1–2 pages, 2–3 pages, or 5+ pages. Clicking an active button deselects it. See Section 7 for details.
Supporting Materials

A collapsible violet section at the bottom of Panel 3 lets you paste or upload existing content for the AI to work with — a student draft, a rubric, a position description, a prior narrative, data tables, raw notes. Supported upload formats are .txt, .md, .csv, and .pdf (text-based). This material is appended to your prompt between clear delimiters. See Section 8 for full details.

Panel 4 · Output Preferences

Shape how the AI responds — format, tone, behavior, and any sensitive considerations.

📄 Format chips

Select one or more output formats. Each adds a specific formatting instruction to your prompt.

Flowing prose Bulleted draft Fill-in template Outline / structure Action checklist Email format Memo format Table / matrix Executive summary Annotated bibliography
🎚 Tone & Register slider

A 5-position slider ranging from Conversational & Warm through to Formal & Academic. For most professional institutional documents, aim for position 3 (Professional) or 4 (Formal). Grant narratives and accreditation documents typically warrant position 5 (Formal & Academic).

Pos. 1 — Conversational
Warm, approachable, colleague-to-colleague. Good for advising emails, mentoring notes, PLC agendas.
Pos. 3 — Professional
Clear, polished, institutional tone. Appropriate for most external-facing documents.
Pos. 5 — Formal & Academic
Rigorous, citation-aware framing. Best for accreditation materials, scholarly writing, grant narratives.
🔁 AI Behavior chips

These chips shape how the AI approaches its work — not what it produces, but how.

Draft only — I will edit Give me 2–3 versions Ask clarifying questions first Be ready to iterate Think step-by-step Flag any uncertainties Be concise Be thorough

"Flag any uncertainties" is particularly valuable for documents involving specific policies, regulations, or legal matters where hallucination risk is real. "Ask clarifying questions first" works well when your task description is intentionally brief and you want the AI to help you flesh it out.

⚠️ Sensitive Considerations chips

Each chip adds a specific compliance or sensitivity instruction to your prompt. These are designed for the real constraints of academic institutional work.

FERPA compliance HIPAA-aware language DEI-inclusive framing Union / collective bargaining Legally cautious language Treat as confidential Accessibility-first language Include AI disclosure note
⚠️
About the AI Disclosure chip When selected, this appends a suggested disclosure sentence at the bottom of the AI's output — something like "[Draft developed with AI assistance (Academic Pro / Claude) and reviewed by the author. Final content reflects the author's professional judgment.]" Useful as institutions develop AI use policies.
Additional instructions field

A free-text textarea at the bottom of Panel 4 for anything else you want included in the prompt. Examples: "Avoid the phrase 'pleasure to recommend'," "mirror the structure of the attached rubric," "use a warm but authoritative voice throughout."

Audience Multi-Select

Academic documents often serve multiple audiences at once. The audience widget lets you select as many as apply.

The Primary Audience / Recipient field is a custom multi-select widget — not a standard dropdown. It has three audience groups (Internal, Students, External), a search/filter box, and tag pills that display your selections below the trigger.

1
Click the trigger button to open the dropdown
The trigger shows either the placeholder text or a count of selected audiences ("3 audiences selected"). Clicking it opens a dropdown panel with grouped options and a search input.
2
Search to filter, or scroll through grouped options
Type any keyword in the search box to filter the list instantly. Empty group headers hide automatically when they have no matching options.
3
Click options to toggle them on and off
Each selected option gets a checkmark indicator and appears as a removable tag pill below the trigger. Clicking a selected option deselects it. Clicking the × on a tag pill also removes it.
4
Click outside or press Escape to close
The dropdown closes when you click anywhere outside it or press the Escape key. Your selections persist in the tag row below.
💡
When to select multiple audiences A grant proposal might go to both a granting agency and a board of trustees. An accreditation evidence summary might address both the accrediting body and faculty colleagues. Selecting both tells the AI it needs language that works across those audiences.

When multiple audiences are selected, the assembled prompt uses "PRIMARY AUDIENCES:" (plural) followed by a semicolon-separated list.

Target Length

Professional documents often have strict length requirements. Set a target and the AI will respect it.

The Target Length row in Panel 3 offers eight toggle buttons. Clicking a button activates it (gold highlight); clicking it again deactivates it, returning to "no limit."

OptionBest for
~150 wordsConference poster abstracts, email subject lines, short nomination blurbs
~250 wordsConference abstracts, one-page double-spaced, short exec summaries
~500 wordsStandard letters of recommendation, policy summaries, brief narratives
~750 wordsGrant sections, program review sections, three-page documents
1–2 pagesLetters, short memos, white paper sections, brief committee reports
2–3 pagesGrant narratives, tenure section drafts, detailed program proposals
5+ pagesFull program reviews, accreditation self-study sections, comprehensive reports
No limit (default)When length isn't constrained — let the AI determine appropriate scope
⚠️
Treat length targets as guidance, not hard stops. AI models work in approximate word counts rather than enforcing precise limits. If a grant narrative must be exactly 500 words, state that explicitly in the Constraints field as well as setting the length target here.
8

Supporting Materials

Hand the AI something real to work with — dramatically improving output quality for revision, review, and template-matching tasks. You can paste content directly or upload a file.

The 📎 Supporting Materials section is a collapsible violet accordion at the bottom of Panel 3. Click the header to expand it. You can add content two ways — paste it directly into the textarea, or upload a file — and both methods append the content to your prompt between clear delimiters.

Option 1 — Paste directly

Click the accordion header to expand it, then paste any text into the textarea. This works for anything you can copy from another application — a student's draft, a rubric, a data table, a prior narrative, or raw notes.

Option 2 — Upload a file

Click the 📄 Upload File button to open a file picker. Supported formats are .txt, .md, .csv, and .pdf (text-based PDFs only). You can upload multiple files at once, or upload them one at a time — each appears as a removable tag badge above the textarea.

1
Click "📄 Upload File" and select one or more files
A native file picker opens. Select .txt, .md, .csv, or .pdf files. You can hold Ctrl/Cmd to select multiple files at once.
2
The file content is read and appended automatically
Each uploaded file appears as a violet tag badge (showing filename and size) and its text content is appended to the materials textarea under an "UPLOADED FILES" section header. The assembled prompt updates immediately.
3
Remove individual files with the × badge button
Click the × on any file badge to remove that file. Its content is removed from the textarea and the prompt rebuilds. Manually pasted content above the uploaded section is preserved.
4
Combine paste and upload freely
You can paste notes or partial text directly into the textarea and also upload files — both appear in the prompt. Manually pasted content always stays above the uploaded file section.
What to paste or upload
Student drafts or submissions
Raw student writing for feedback, or context in advising documentation
Existing policy text
A prior policy version, a model policy, or an existing procedure to revise
Rubrics and criteria
The rubric you're applying in a review, or criteria documents to align with
Data tables or statistics
Enrollment numbers, SLO assessment results, demographic data for an equity narrative
Position descriptions
Job postings for matching evaluation criteria or writing search committee documents
Prior narrative drafts
An earlier version of a grant narrative, tenure statement, or accreditation section
⚠️
PDF limitation The file upload reads text from PDFs using the file's raw content stream. This works well for text-based PDFs (documents created digitally). It will not work for scanned/image-only PDFs. For scanned documents, copy-paste the text directly into the textarea instead.
💡
Privacy reminder Do not paste or upload personally identifiable student information (FERPA-protected data) into AI platforms unless your institution has approved that platform for use with student records. Refer to students generically and always use the FERPA compliance chip in Panel 4.

Sending to an AI Platform

Three ways to get your assembled prompt into the AI of your choice.

1
📋 Copy Prompt
Copies the assembled prompt to your clipboard. Use this to paste into any AI platform, your notes, a shared document, or anywhere else. The button briefly confirms with a green checkmark.
2
🚀 Open in LLM
First use the Send to: dropdown to choose your platform (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, Perplexity, Meta AI, Mistral, or Other). Then click Open in LLM — it both copies the prompt to your clipboard and opens the selected platform in a new tab. Paste with Ctrl+V or Cmd+V once the platform loads.
3
💾 Download .txt
Downloads the assembled prompt as a plain-text file with a descriptive filename (e.g., academic_pro_letter_of_recommendation_[timestamp].txt). Useful for saving prompts for reuse, sharing with colleagues, or building a personal prompt library.
ℹ️
The "Other" option When you select "Other (copy & paste)" in the dropdown and click Open in LLM, the prompt is copied to your clipboard with a confirmation message. No new tab opens — just navigate to whatever AI platform you use and paste.
After you send your prompt
✦
Treat the first output as a draft, not a finished product
Even an excellent prompt produces something that needs your professional review, editing, and judgment. AI doesn't know your institution's specific policies, your relationships with colleagues, or the particular history of a student you've advised for a year.
✦
Ask for revisions in plain language
Within the same AI conversation, you can ask: "Make the opening more direct," "Add a paragraph about equity outcomes," "Shorten this to 500 words," or "Give me two alternative versions of the second paragraph." You don't need to rebuild a new prompt for every tweak.

Prompt History

Session-based history so you can revisit, compare, and reuse prompts built during the same working session.

The 🕐 Prompt History panel at the bottom of the output column automatically saves up to 8 prompts per session. It updates after a brief pause in your editing (about 1.5 seconds), so it captures snapshots as your prompt evolves rather than every keystroke.

Click a history item
Restores that version to the output box so you can copy, review, or open it in an LLM platform.
Copy button per item
Each history entry has its own "Copy" button so you can grab earlier versions without disrupting your current build.
Labeled by task type
Each entry is labeled with the selected task subtype (or category) and a timestamp, making it easy to find what you built 20 minutes ago.
Survives Clear All
Clicking "Clear All" resets all form fields but preserves your prompt history — so you can start fresh without losing earlier work.
⭐
Best use case: iterative document sessions If you're building a grant proposal and want to try several different framings for the same narrative, use prompt history to compare approaches. Build one version, let it save, tweak your inputs, build another. Compare both in the history panel without needing to rebuild from scratch.
⚠️
Session-only storage. Prompt history lives in-memory and disappears when you close or refresh the page. If you want to keep a prompt for future sessions, use the Download .txt button or copy it to a document before navigating away.

Complete Walkthrough Example

Step-by-step example: building a prompt for an NSF-style grant proposal narrative.

1
Panel 1 — Select category and subtype
Click Academic Writing & Documents. From the subtype chips, click Grant Proposal (narrative).
2
Panel 2 — Select role
Click Faculty / Instructor. In the additional context field, type: "STEM faculty, community college, co-PI on Title III application."
3
Panel 3 — Fill in context
In the main description: "Draft a 2-page project narrative for an NSF-HBCU-UP style grant. Our data shows a 40% drop in STEM persistence between year 1 and year 2. The proposed program pairs STEM juniors with first-year students for a one-year mentoring experience."

Audience: Granting agency / foundation
Institution: Community college (2-year)
Discipline: STEM / Higher Education
Constraints: "2 pages, NSF format, Evidence Base section required"
Length target: 2–3 pages
4
Panel 4 — Set preferences
Format: Flowing prose
Tone: Position 5 (Formal & Academic)
Behavior: Think step-by-step
Sensitive: DEI-inclusive framing
5
Review the assembled prompt, then send
The output panel now shows a complete, structured prompt. Select Claude from the Send to: dropdown and click Open in LLM. Paste in Claude.ai.
What the assembled prompt looks like
You are assisting a college faculty member (instructor/professor). More specifically: STEM faculty, community college, co-PI on Title III application. TASK: Grant Proposal (narrative) [Academic Writing & Documents] Draft a 2-page project narrative for an NSF-HBCU-UP style grant. Our data shows a 40% drop in STEM persistence between year 1 and year 2. The proposed program pairs STEM juniors with first-year students for a one-year mentoring experience. PRIMARY AUDIENCE: Granting agency / foundation. INSTITUTION TYPE: Community college (2-year). DISCIPLINE / FIELD: STEM / Higher Education. KEY CONSTRAINTS & CONTEXT: 2 pages, NSF format, Evidence Base section required LENGTH TARGET: Target two to three pages (approximately 750–1,000 words). Include sufficient detail and context. FORMAT: Present the output as flowing, polished prose paragraphs. TONE: Use a formal academic register — rigorous, citation-aware in framing, suitable for scholarly or official accreditation documents. AI BEHAVIOR: Think through this step by step before producing the final output. SENSITIVITY & COMPLIANCE: Use inclusive, equity-centered language throughout. Reflect awareness of diverse identities, backgrounds, and access needs.
