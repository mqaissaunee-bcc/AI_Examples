Module 1 of 6

// What Is Zero Trust?

For decades, network security operated on a simple idea: build a strong wall around everything, and trust whatever is inside it. This model — called perimeter-based security — worked reasonably well when all employees sat in an office, all data lived in on-premises data centers, and the only threats came from outside the corporate network. Think of it like a castle: once you got past the moat and the drawbridge, you were essentially trusted to wander the halls freely.

But that world no longer exists. Today, employees work from coffee shops, home offices, and airports. Applications run in public clouds like AWS and Azure. Partners and contractors access corporate systems. And attackers have learned that once they crack a single endpoint inside the perimeter — through phishing, a stolen password, or a vulnerable application — they can roam the network almost unchallenged. The castle walls have holes everywhere, and the "trusted" insiders can be attackers in disguise.

⚠️ The Perimeter Is Dead
The 2020 SolarWinds attack is a chilling example. Attackers compromised a software update mechanism and gained access to thousands of organizations' internal networks — having already breached the "perimeter." Once inside, they moved laterally for months without detection. Traditional security models had no answer.

The Zero Trust Answer

Zero Trust is a security philosophy — and increasingly a set of concrete technical practices — built on a radically different assumption: trust nothing and no one by default, regardless of whether they are inside or outside the network. The term was coined by John Kindervag at Forrester Research in 2010, though the underlying ideas had been percolating in security research for years. Google's internal implementation, called BeyondCorp, launched around the same time and became one of the most influential real-world examples of Zero Trust at scale.

The core mantra of Zero Trust is simple: "Never Trust, Always Verify." Every request to access any resource — whether it comes from an employee laptop on the corporate network, a mobile phone on public Wi-Fi, or an internal server making an API call — must be authenticated, authorized, and continuously validated. Location on the network (inside or outside) is irrelevant. Identity, device health, and context are everything.

A Quick History

1994
Stephen Paul Marsh coins "Zero Trust"
The phrase appears in a doctoral thesis at the University of Stirling, though it referred to computational trust models, not network security.
2010
John Kindervag at Forrester popularizes Zero Trust for networking
Kindervag publishes research explicitly applying "Zero Trust" to network security architecture, arguing that internal network traffic should not be trusted by default.
2011–2014
Google's BeyondCorp initiative launches
Following the Operation Aurora attack (which targeted Google through a browser zero-day), Google redesigns its internal network using Zero Trust principles. Employees eventually access corporate apps from any device, anywhere, without a VPN.
2018–2020
Cloud adoption accelerates Zero Trust adoption
As Microsoft Azure, AWS, and SaaS platforms become dominant, the traditional perimeter becomes meaningless for many organizations. Zero Trust frameworks go mainstream.
2021
U.S. Executive Order on Cybersecurity mandates Zero Trust
President Biden's Executive Order 14028 requires federal agencies to implement Zero Trust architecture, cementing it as the official U.S. government security framework. NIST SP 800-207 becomes the reference standard.

// The Five Pillars of Zero Trust

Zero Trust isn't a single product you can buy. It's an architectural philosophy — a set of principles that you apply across your entire IT environment. The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) and NIST SP 800-207 both organize Zero Trust into distinct capability areas, sometimes called pillars. Understanding these pillars gives you a mental map for how Zero Trust works across a real organization.

Pillar 1 — Identity

In Zero Trust, identity is the new perimeter. Instead of relying on network location to determine trust, you rely on who (or what) is making the request. This means every user, service account, and application must have a verifiable identity, and that identity must be authenticated before access is granted. Identity is verified using strong authentication mechanisms — ideally multi-factor authentication (MFA)MFA requires a user to provide two or more verification factors: something you know (password), something you have (phone/hardware token), or something you are (biometric). — and the credentials themselves must be protected by a modern Identity Provider (IdP) like Azure AD, Okta, or Google Workspace.

An important concept here is continuous authentication — verifying identity not just at login, but throughout the session. If a user's behavior suddenly changes (e.g., they start downloading huge amounts of data they normally don't touch), the system can trigger re-authentication or revoke access automatically. This is called risk-based or adaptive authentication.

Pillar 2 — Device / Endpoint

Your identity being valid doesn't mean your device is safe. Zero Trust requires that every device accessing corporate resources be known, registered, and assessed for health. A device health assessment might check whether the operating system is up to date, whether antivirus is running, whether the disk is encrypted, and whether the device is enrolled in Mobile Device Management (MDM)MDM solutions (like Microsoft Intune or Jamf) allow organizations to remotely manage, configure, and enforce security policies on employee devices.. Unmanaged or unhealthy devices are denied access or granted only limited access to low-sensitivity resources.

Pillar 3 — Network

The network pillar addresses how traffic flows between users, applications, and data. Under Zero Trust, network access is least-privileged: users only get network connectivity to the specific resources they need, not broad access to entire network segments. Techniques like micro-segmentation (dividing the network into tiny isolated zones) and Software-Defined Perimeters (SDP) enforce this. We'll dig deeply into micro-segmentation in Module 4.

Pillar 4 — Application / Workload

Applications themselves must be hardened and access-controlled. This means authorizing users at the application level (not just the network level), securing APIs with authentication tokens, continuously scanning workloads for vulnerabilities, and treating every application interaction as potentially untrusted. This pillar has become especially critical as organizations move to microservices and containers, where dozens of application components communicate with each other over APIs.

Pillar 5 — Data

Ultimately, everything in security is about protecting data — customer records, intellectual property, financial transactions. The data pillar focuses on classifying data by sensitivity, encrypting it at rest and in transit, and ensuring that only authorized identities can access specific data sets. Modern Data Loss Prevention (DLP)DLP tools monitor and control data movement to prevent sensitive data from being sent outside the organization — for example, blocking an employee from emailing a spreadsheet of customer credit card numbers. tools integrate with Zero Trust frameworks to enforce data access policies automatically.

✅ Key Takeaway
No single pillar is sufficient on its own. Verifying identity but ignoring device health, or securing the network but ignoring data encryption, still leaves major gaps. Zero Trust is a defense-in-depth strategy that stacks multiple layers of protection, so that compromising one layer doesn't give an attacker the keys to the kingdom.

Module 3 of 6

// Identity & Multi-Factor Authentication

If Zero Trust has a single most important pillar, it is identity. Everything else in the framework — network controls, device policies, data encryption — depends on knowing with high confidence who or what is making a request. This module dives deep into how modern organizations establish and verify identity, and why basic username/password authentication is woefully insufficient for a Zero Trust world.

Why Passwords Alone Are Not Enough

Passwords have been the primary authentication mechanism since the dawn of computing. The problem? Humans are terrible at creating and managing them. Research from Verizon's Data Breach Investigations Report has consistently shown that roughly 80% of hacking-related breaches involve stolen or weak credentials. Attackers use techniques like phishing (tricking users into entering credentials on fake sites), credential stuffing (reusing breached username/password pairs from data leaks), and brute force attacks (automated guessing). A password alone, no matter how complex, can be stolen without the user even knowing.

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) Explained

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) adds one or more additional verification steps beyond the password. The idea is based on three classic authentication factors:

🧠
Something You Know
A password, PIN, or the answer to a security question. This factor alone is vulnerable to phishing and data breaches.
📱
Something You Have
A phone (for SMS codes or authenticator apps like Google Authenticator), a hardware security key (like a YubiKey), or a smart card. Much harder for attackers to steal remotely.
👁️
Something You Are
A biometric: fingerprint, face recognition, retina scan, or voice pattern. Built into most modern smartphones and laptops. Convenient and strong, but not infallible.

True MFA requires at least two different types of factors. Requiring a password plus a PIN is not MFA — both are "something you know." True MFA would be a password plus a code from an authenticator app (something you know + something you have).

MFA Methods: A Spectrum of Strength

Not all MFA is created equal. Here's how common methods stack up, from weakest to strongest:

A one-time code is sent via text message. This is the most widely deployed form of MFA and is significantly better than passwords alone. However, it has known vulnerabilities: SIM swapping (an attacker convinces a carrier to transfer your phone number to their SIM card) and SS7 attacks (exploiting weaknesses in the telephony protocol). Real-time phishing sites can also trick users into entering their SMS code on a fake login page. NIST now discourages SMS OTP for high-security environments but still considers it acceptable for general use.

Apps like Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator, and Authy generate Time-based One-Time Passwords (TOTP) — six-digit codes that change every 30 seconds. These codes are generated locally on the device, without needing network access, using a shared secret established during setup. They are immune to SIM swapping and SS7 attacks. However, they are still vulnerable to real-time phishing (the attacker can relay the code before it expires) and to malware that can read codes from a compromised device. Widely recommended for most enterprise environments.

Apps like Duo Security or Microsoft Authenticator send a push notification to the user's phone when a login is attempted. The user taps "Approve" or "Deny." This is convenient and broadly deployed in enterprise settings. However, attackers have successfully used MFA fatigue attacks (also called push bombing) — flooding users with approval requests until they accidentally or frustratedly tap "Approve." Modern implementations counter this with number matching: the app displays a number from the login screen, and the user must enter that same number in the app, making accidental approval nearly impossible.

Physical hardware security keys (like YubiKey) and the software-based passkeys standard are the strongest and most phishing-resistant form of MFA available today. They use the FIDO2 / WebAuthn standard: during authentication, the device cryptographically proves it possesses a private key without ever sending that key over the network. Critically, the authentication is bound to the specific website's domain — a phishing site cannot intercept the response because the device detects the domain mismatch. Passkeys are now supported by Apple, Google, and Microsoft, and are considered the future of authentication. For high-security Zero Trust environments, FIDO2 is the gold standard.

Adaptive / Risk-Based Authentication

Zero Trust doesn't just check identity once — it uses adaptive authentication to continuously evaluate risk throughout a session. The system considers signals like: Is this the user's normal location? Is this a registered device? Is the login happening at an unusual time? Has the user's behavior changed since login? If any signal looks suspicious, the system can require step-up authentication (additional MFA factor), limit the user's access, or terminate the session. This is why Zero Trust can often provide a better user experience than traditional VPNs — trusted users on trusted devices in trusted locations may not need to re-authenticate constantly, while anomalous sessions get flagged immediately.

💡 Real-World Example: Lapsus$ Attacks (2021–2022)
The Lapsus$ hacking group successfully breached Microsoft, NVIDIA, Samsung, and Okta by targeting employees with social engineering and SIM swapping. In many cases, they bypassed MFA by either convincing carriers to transfer the victim's phone number, or by using real-time phishing proxies to relay authenticator app codes. Their success highlighted the importance of phishing-resistant MFA (FIDO2) and the need for Zero Trust to check device health and behavioral signals, not just authentication codes.

// Micro-Segmentation & Least Privilege

You've verified the user's identity and confirmed their device is healthy. Now what? In a traditional network, the answer is: let them in and give them broad access to the internal network. In Zero Trust, the answer is: grant them access to only the specific resource they need, for the duration they need it, and nothing more. This principle is called least-privilege access, and the primary technical mechanism for enforcing it at the network level is micro-segmentation.

What Is Micro-Segmentation?

Traditional network security divides the network into large zones — often just "inside" and "outside," or maybe a DMZ for web-facing servers. Everything inside a zone can freely communicate with everything else inside that zone. Micro-segmentation takes this concept and shrinks the zones down to the level of individual workloads, applications, or even individual processes. Instead of one big "internal network" where everything trusts everything, you get hundreds of tiny isolated segments, each with its own access rules.

Imagine a hospital's network as a city. Traditional segmentation is like having a perimeter wall around the city — once you're inside, you can walk anywhere. Micro-segmentation is like every building, every floor, and every room having its own locked door and requiring credentials to enter. A thief who breaks into the pharmacy can't automatically access the patient records server on the sixth floor of the same building.

🚨 The NotPetya Lesson
In 2017, the NotPetya ransomware devastated shipping giant Maersk — causing an estimated $300 million in damage — largely because the company's internal network allowed unrestricted lateral movement. Once a single machine was infected, the malware spread to virtually every Windows machine in the company. Micro-segmentation would have contained the blast radius dramatically.

How Micro-Segmentation Is Implemented

The most straightforward approach: deploy a firewall or agent on each server or workload that enforces rules about what traffic is allowed in and out. Tools like Windows Defender Firewall, iptables on Linux, or dedicated agents from vendors like Illumio or Guardicore sit on each host and enforce policy. The advantage is that this approach works with any underlying network — you don't need special hardware. The disadvantage is that it requires managing policies across potentially thousands of individual hosts.

Software-Defined Networking (SDN) allows network behavior to be controlled programmatically through software. In a data center or cloud environment, SDN controllers can dynamically create isolated network overlays — virtual networks that sit on top of the physical infrastructure. Traffic between segments is controlled and inspected centrally. Cloud platforms like AWS (using Security Groups and VPC peering rules) and Azure (using Network Security Groups) implement this model natively. You define traffic rules in code, and the platform enforces them automatically.

In modern cloud-native environments built on containers and microservices, a service mesh (like Istio or Linkerd) handles inter-service communication. Each microservice gets a sidecar proxy that enforces mutual TLS (mTLS) authentication — every service must cryptographically prove its identity to every other service before communication is allowed. Policy engines within the mesh define which services are allowed to talk to which, with fine-grained rules. This is Zero Trust applied at the application/workload level in a Kubernetes environment.

Least Privilege Access

Least-privilege access means that users, processes, and systems should only have the minimum permissions necessary to perform their current task — nothing more, nothing less. This principle applies at multiple levels: a database user account should only be able to read the tables it needs (not modify or delete them); an employee should only have access to the business systems relevant to their job; a container process should only run with the privileges required for that specific workload.

A related concept is Just-in-Time (JIT) accessJIT access means privileged access is granted only when needed, for a specific time window, and then automatically revoked. This reduces the risk of standing privileged accounts being compromised.. Instead of giving administrators permanent administrative accounts (which become high-value targets), JIT access grants elevated privileges only when requested and for a limited time window — say, 30 minutes to perform a specific maintenance task — and then revokes them automatically. Microsoft's Azure Active Directory Privileged Identity Management (PIM) and similar tools implement this model.

🗂️
Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)
Permissions are assigned to roles (e.g., "developer," "admin," "read-only analyst"), and users are assigned to roles. Users inherit the permissions of their role. Easy to manage at scale, but can lead to "role explosion" if too many roles are created.
📋
Attribute-Based Access Control (ABAC)
Access decisions are based on attributes — user attributes (department, clearance level), resource attributes (data classification), and context attributes (time of day, device health). More flexible and powerful than RBAC, especially in complex Zero Trust environments.

// Policy Engine, Continuous Monitoring & SASE

Everything we've covered so far — identity verification, device health checks, micro-segmentation, least privilege — needs a central brain that decides whether to allow or deny each access request. In Zero Trust architecture, this brain is called the Policy Engine (PE) and Policy Administrator (PA). And delivering all these capabilities to distributed users and cloud environments is increasingly done through a modern architecture called SASE (Secure Access Service Edge).

The Policy Engine: Zero Trust's Decision Maker

According to NIST SP 800-207 (the U.S. government's Zero Trust reference framework), every access request flows through the following logic:

🔍
Policy Engine (PE)
The component that evaluates access requests against policy rules. It consumes signals from identity providers, device health systems, threat intelligence, and behavioral analytics to make an allow/deny decision.
⚙️
Policy Administrator (PA)
Executes the Policy Engine's decision. If access is allowed, the PA generates credentials and communicates the decision to the Policy Enforcement Point. If denied, it sends a termination signal.
🚧
Policy Enforcement Point (PEP)
The gateway or proxy that actually allows or blocks the traffic. It sits between the subject (user/device) and the resource (application/data). All access flows through the PEP.

The Policy Engine doesn't just check rules statically. It continuously evaluates contextual signals: Is the user's device compliant at this moment? Has the user's location changed suspiciously? Has the IP address been flagged in threat intelligence feeds? Are there any anomalous behavioral patterns? This constant evaluation is what makes Zero Trust dynamic rather than a one-time check-box.

Continuous Monitoring & Logging

Zero Trust requires that you can't just grant access and forget about it. All access must be continuously monitored and logged. This generates an enormous amount of data — every login, every API call, every file download — which is fed into a SIEM (Security Information and Event Management)A SIEM platform aggregates logs from across the environment, correlates events to identify patterns indicative of attacks, and generates alerts. Examples: Microsoft Sentinel, Splunk, IBM QRadar. system and increasingly into UEBA (User and Entity Behavior Analytics)UEBA tools use machine learning to build baselines of normal user and device behavior, then alert on deviations that may indicate compromise or insider threat. platforms that use AI to detect anomalies. The goal is to shrink the time from intrusion to detection — the infamous "dwell time" that averaged over 200 days in traditional environments.

SASE: Delivering Zero Trust at the Edge

As workforces went remote and applications moved to the cloud, a new challenge emerged: how do you deliver consistent Zero Trust security to users who are everywhere, accessing applications that are also everywhere? The answer from Gartner (who coined the term in 2019) is SASE — Secure Access Service Edge. SASE converges networking and security into a single cloud-delivered service, delivered close to where users are (at the "edge").

🔐
Security Components of SASE
CASB (Cloud Access Security Broker) — controls SaaS app access
SWG (Secure Web Gateway) — filters web traffic
ZTNA (Zero Trust Network Access) — replaces VPN
FWaaS (Firewall as a Service) — cloud-based next-gen firewall
DLP — enforces data loss prevention policies
🌐
Networking Components of SASE
SD-WAN (Software-Defined WAN) — intelligent traffic routing
CDN/Edge PoPs — deliver security inspection close to users
QoS / Optimization — prioritize critical traffic
Cloud-native delivery — scale up/down on demand

A key component of SASE is ZTNA (Zero Trust Network Access), which replaces traditional VPNs. With a VPN, users connect to a corporate network endpoint and then have broad access to internal resources. ZTNA instead creates a secure, application-specific tunnel — a user can only access the specific application they're authorized for, with no visibility into or access to the rest of the network. Vendors like Zscaler, Palo Alto Networks (Prisma Access), and Cloudflare Access implement ZTNA as part of their SASE offerings.

💡 SASE in Practice: A Day in the Life
Priya is a contractor working from a café in Austin. She opens her laptop and tries to access a financial reporting app. The SASE platform's ZTNA checks her identity (she authenticates with her FIDO2 key), verifies her device health (her managed laptop passes compliance checks), evaluates her risk score (low — normal location, normal time), and applies data policies (she can view reports but not download them as CSV). Access is granted — to that specific app only. Priya never connects to the corporate "network." The whole process is invisible to her.
