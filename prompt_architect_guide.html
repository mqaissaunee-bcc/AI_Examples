What is Prompt Architect?

Prompt Architect is a form-based tool that helps you write detailed, structured prompts for AI tools like Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini. Instead of writing a prompt from scratch, you fill in a few fields — topic, audience, interactive type — and the tool assembles a prompt that instructs the AI to build a complete, deployable HTML learning activity for your students.

You don't need coding experience. You're describing what you want; the AI does the building. The result is a single HTML file you can embed directly in Canvas, share as a link, or use as a standalone activity.

Set Your Context

The first three fields are the foundation of your prompt. They tell the AI what you're teaching, where it fits in your curriculum, and who the students are. All three are required.

Topic / Subject Matter — Be specific. "The Water Cycle" generates a better activity than "Earth Science." Include a subtopic if you have one: "Cell Division — specifically mitosis vs. meiosis."

Discipline / Course Area — Start typing and select from the dropdown, or type your own. This helps the AI choose relevant examples, terminology, and framing.

Target Audience — Add one or more audience tags. You can combine them: "First-year nursing students" + "English language learners" will produce an activity tuned to both groups.

Choose Your Interactive Type

This is where you tell the AI what kind of activity to build. There are over 40 types organized into categories — quizzes, simulations, timelines, writing scaffolds, role-play scenarios, data narratives, and more.

You can select more than one type. The first selection becomes the primary — the core structure of the activity. Additional types become secondary enrichment layers. Click ★ Primary on any tag to reassign.

Quick Combos are preset bundles that pre-fill the type field with a sensible primary + secondary combination. They're the fastest way to get started — click one, and the type selector is already configured for a common faculty workflow.

Set Scope & Tone

Three sliders let you calibrate how the activity feels, how long it runs, and how text-heavy it is. All three are visible in Simple and Full mode.

Instructional Tone ranges from Formal to Conversational. A biology lecture might stay Balanced; an orientation activity might lean Conversational. The AI adjusts vocabulary, prompts, and feedback language to match.

Content Scope & Depth controls the breadth and estimated time-on-task. Use this to match your class period — a "10–15 min warmup" setting produces something meaningfully different from a "45–60 min full lesson."

Text Density controls how much explanatory prose appears in the generated activity. Minimal produces labels and bullets only — ideal for fast-paced quizzes or review games. Textbook-Dense generates multi-paragraph explanations per concept — appropriate for self-paced lessons that stand alone without an instructor. The default Balanced is a good starting point for most activities.

💡 Slider quick reference Scope: Micro (5 min) → Warmup (10–15 min) → Standard (15 min) → Comprehensive (25 min) → Deep Dive (30 min) → Extended Module (60 min) → Full Unit (2 hrs) → Full Lecture (3 hrs).

Text Density: Minimal (labels & bullets only) → Concise (1–2 sentences) → Balanced (short paragraphs + examples) → Rich (full paragraphs, context & nuance) → Textbook-Dense (extended prose, multiple paragraphs). Match density to how independently students will use the activity — higher density for fully self-paced lessons, lower for in-class activities with instructor support.

Hover over any active card's info icon to see exactly what chips it activates and what each one does. This is also a good way to learn what Full Mode offers before switching to it.

Fine-Tune with Chips (Full Mode)

Switching to Full Mode reveals five categories of individual chips — toggle-able settings that add specific instructions to your prompt. Chips let you go beyond what bundles offer, mixing and matching any combination.

Each chip is a focused instruction. Activating 🖨️ Print / Save as PDF adds exactly that requirement and nothing else. You can build a prompt with 2 chips or 15 — there's no limit, though more chips produce a more prescriptive (and longer) prompt.

🎨 Color Palette Picker Just below the sliders, the Color Palette section (collapsed by default — click to expand) lets you choose a named palette for the generated activity. Options include Brookdale, Ocean, Forest, Slate, Warm, High Contrast, and Custom. Selecting a palette injects exact CSS variable values into the prompt so the LLM applies your colors throughout the application — no guessing, no generic blue-on-white defaults. Leave it on None to let the AI choose its own palette.
❓ Ask Clarifying Questions First Below the Additional Requirements field, a toggle chip — ❓ Ask clarifying questions first — instructs the LLM to pause before generating any code and ask you questions about content, structure, audience, or technical constraints. Activating this is especially valuable for complex or novel prompts where ambiguity could send the LLM in the wrong direction. It's the difference between getting exactly what you envisioned and getting something that's close-but-wrong. Leave it off for straightforward, well-specified prompts.
📡 New to SCORM? The Canvas & LMS section includes a collapsible SCORM guide explaining what SCORM is, when you need it, and which chips to combine for completion tracking vs. full gradebook integration. Expand it before activating SCORM-related chips.
AI Resistance Score When you activate AI Resistance chips, a score meter appears showing your activity's resistance potential. The score is a helpful signal, but it's only realized if students are required to submit the relevant outputs — process logs, reflections, uploads. A high score on the prompt doesn't automatically produce a high-resistance assignment.

Reference Materials

The Reference Materials section lets you tell the AI that you'll be attaching files when you paste the prompt. Activating the 📎 Include uploaded reference file(s) chip reveals a second set of chips for selecting what kinds of files you'll attach.

The prompt will then instruct the AI to use your uploaded document as its primary content source — pulling facts, examples, terminology, and structure from your own materials rather than generating from scratch.

You can select multiple file types if you're uploading a combination — for example, a slide deck and a rubric. The prompt will acknowledge both.

Include Video

Activating the 🎬 Include Video Quick Option (or the Flipped Classroom bundle) reveals the Multimedia Settings panel. This is a two-step workflow: first you configure your video preferences and generate a search prompt, then you verify the YouTube IDs before copying your final prompt.

The reason for the two-step process: AI tools can't always be trusted to find real, working YouTube videos on their own. Some LLMs hallucinate video IDs that look valid but lead nowhere. The workflow here routes around that problem by separating the search from the generation.

Why this matters A generated activity that embeds a broken or non-existent YouTube video is a dead end for students. The ID verification step takes about 2 minutes and prevents that problem entirely.

🔍 Generate Video Search Prompt

Step 1 — Set your preferences, then click Generate Video Search Prompt. A modal opens with a focused search prompt tailored to your topic, audience, and video preferences. Copy that prompt.

Step 2 — Take the search prompt to Perplexity or Gemini. Both have live YouTube search and will return real, verified video IDs. Paste the full response back into the modal — IDs are extracted automatically.

Step 3 — Send to the ID Checker. Click Send to ID Checker to verify each ID against YouTube's public API. You'll see the video title, thumbnail, and a direct link to confirm before committing. Once verified, click Use These IDs — the IDs are embedded directly into your final prompt.

Copy & Use Your Prompt

When you're ready, click Copy Prompt at the bottom of the tool. The full prompt is copied to your clipboard — you'll see a confirmation message. Then:

  1. Open your LLM of choice (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, etc.) in a new chat.
  2. Paste the prompt.
  3. If you activated Reference Materials, attach your files before hitting send.
  4. Let the AI generate — it will produce a complete HTML file.
  5. Copy the generated code and save it as a .html file.
  6. Embed it in Canvas via the Redirect Tool, host it on GitHub Pages, or share as a standalone file.

Common Workflows

Four faculty scenarios with recommended settings from start to finish.

🖥️

Convert my slides into an interactive

You have an existing PowerPoint and want students to engage with the content, not just read through slides.

  1. Quick Combo: 🖥️ Slides to Interactive
  2. Set Scope to match your class period
  3. Reference Materials → Slide deck / PowerPoint
  4. Quick Option: Student-Ready
  5. Copy prompt, paste into AI, attach your .pptx
🛡️

Build an AI-resistant assignment

You want students to complete the activity genuinely — not shortcut it with ChatGPT.

  1. Choose a high-resistance type (🛡️ shield icon)
  2. Quick Option: AI-Resistant
  3. Optionally: Quick Option: Writing Workshop
  4. Reference Materials if you have course readings
  5. Pair output with AI Resistance Layer tool for assignment instructions
♿

Ensure my activity is accessible

You need to meet WCAG standards for students with disabilities or assistive technology.

  1. Quick Option: Accessible (bundles all 4 a11y chips)
  2. Or switch to Full Mode and select individual accessibility chips
  3. Include Screen Reader Friendly and High Contrast Mode at minimum
  4. Add Reduced Motion for students with vestibular disorders
🎬

Design a flipped lesson

You want students to watch a video and complete a structured activity before coming to class.

  1. Quick Combo: 🎬 Watch & Reflect
  2. Quick Option: Flipped Classroom
  3. Quick Option: Student-Ready
  4. Set Scope to match pre-class time budget
  5. Use the Video Search Prompt generator to find a good YouTube video
📘

Build an interactive textbook chapter

You want a chapter-length resource with explanations, annotated visuals, comprehension checkpoints, and a closing assessment — to replace or supplement a textbook.

  1. Quick Combo: 📘 Textbook Chapter
  2. Set Scope to Full Lesson or Extended Module
  3. Quick Options: Student-Ready + Accessible + Polished Output
  4. Reference Materials → upload your slides, reading, or outline
  5. Copy prompt, paste into AI, attach your files
