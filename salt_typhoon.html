About Salt Typhoon

What is Salt Typhoon?

Salt Typhoon is a sophisticated Advanced Persistent Threat (APT) actor believed to be operated by China's Ministry of State Security (MSS). This state-sponsored hacking group has conducted high-profile cyber espionage campaigns, particularly targeting the United States and other countries around the world.

Primary Targets

Salt Typhoon primarily targets telecommunications providers, government agencies, and critical infrastructure. The group has successfully infiltrated major U.S. broadband providers including AT&T, Verizon, T-Mobile, and Lumen Technologies, gaining access to sensitive communications data.

Advanced Capabilities

The group employs sophisticated techniques including custom malware (such as GhostSpider, SnappyBee, and Masol RAT), exploitation of zero-day vulnerabilities, and "living off the land" tactics to maintain persistent access to victim networks while evading detection.

Timeline of Activities

2022-2023

Initial Infiltration

Salt Typhoon begins targeting telecommunications providers and critical infrastructure, establishing initial access that would remain undetected for months or years.

September 25, 2024

Public Disclosure

The Wall Street Journal breaks news that Salt Typhoon had successfully targeted several cable and broadband service providers in the United States.

October 8, 2024

Government Response

The U.S. House of Representatives Committee on Homeland Security requests an urgent briefing from CISA and FBI on the status of PRC-backed cyber actors attacking U.S. networks.

December 3, 2024

International Collaboration

CISA, NSA, FBI and international partners release guidance on addressing the Salt Typhoon attacks, recommending enhanced security measures for communications infrastructure.

December 4, 2024

Scale Revealed

White House officials confirm Salt Typhoon compromised at least eight telecommunications providers in the U.S. and impacted dozens of countries globally.

February 5, 2025

UK Cisco Device Exploitation

Salt Typhoon exploited CVE-2023-20198 vulnerability in Cisco IOS XE devices registered to two UK companies. Attackers bypassed authentication via "webui_wsma_http" endpoint and executed "show run" commands to exfiltrate device configurations at highest privilege level.

January-February 2025

Ongoing Mitigation

Security agencies continue efforts to remove Salt Typhoon actors from compromised systems while developing strategies to prevent future attacks. Recent focus on Cisco infrastructure vulnerabilities.

Impacts & Implications

National Security Impacts

Salt Typhoon has been described as the "worst telecom hack in our nation's history" by Senator Mark Warner, Chair of the Senate Intelligence Committee. The breach potentially compromised communications of U.S. government officials, including senior political figures.

The attack specifically targeted and gained access to portals that telecommunications companies provide to law enforcement for court-ordered surveillance. This compromised both domestic law enforcement capabilities and U.S. intelligence operations.

Most concerning, Chinese intelligence may have learned which of their own operatives were under surveillance, potentially allowing them to evade detection and compromising counterintelligence operations.

Privacy Concerns

Salt Typhoon gained access to metadata from over a million users, including date and time stamps, source and destination IP addresses, and phone numbers. For some individuals, the content of phone calls and text messages was also exposed.

Users in the Washington D.C. area were particularly targeted, suggesting a focus on individuals connected to government and policy activities.

The breach has prompted recommendations for individuals and organizations to adopt end-to-end encrypted communications tools to protect sensitive information.

Telecommunications Industry

Major providers including AT&T, Verizon, T-Mobile, Spectrum, Lumen, Consolidated Communications, and Windstream were compromised. The attack specifically targeted core network components, including Cisco routers that route large portions of internet traffic.

The telecommunications industry faces increased regulatory scrutiny and pressure to implement enhanced security measures for critical infrastructure protection.

Providers must now balance implementing stronger security with maintaining compliance with legal requirements for lawful intercept capabilities.

Global Implications

Salt Typhoon's operations have affected telecommunications providers in dozens of countries across continents, including in the Indo-Pacific region and Europe. Recent February 2025 attacks on UK companies demonstrate the group's continued global reach.

The attack highlights the vulnerability of critical communications infrastructure worldwide and has accelerated international cooperation on cybersecurity, particularly around Cisco device security.

This incident is likely to influence geopolitical tensions and trade policies, particularly between the United States, China, and UK allies. The targeting of UK infrastructure shows Salt Typhoon's expansion beyond traditional U.S. and NATO targets.

Frequently Asked Questions

How recent are Salt Typhoon's activities?
Salt Typhoon remains highly active as of February 2025. Recent intelligence shows the group exploited CVE-2023-20198 vulnerability in Cisco IOS XE devices belonging to UK companies on February 5, 2025, demonstrating their continued focus on network infrastructure targeting and rapid exploitation of known vulnerabilities.
What is Salt Typhoon?
Salt Typhoon is an advanced persistent threat (APT) actor believed to be operated by China's Ministry of State Security. The group conducts sophisticated cyber espionage campaigns targeting telecommunications providers, government agencies, and critical infrastructure worldwide.
How does Salt Typhoon operate?
Salt Typhoon employs various sophisticated techniques, including custom malware like GhostSpider, exploitation of both known and zero-day vulnerabilities, and "living off the land" tactics that use legitimate system tools to avoid detection. The group has shown a high level of sophistication and uses anti-forensic techniques to evade security measures.
Which companies were affected by Salt Typhoon?
Major telecommunications providers including AT&T, Verizon, T-Mobile, Spectrum, Lumen, Consolidated Communications, and Windstream were compromised. The attack specifically targeted core network components, including Cisco routers that route large portions of internet traffic.
Was my personal data compromised?
Salt Typhoon gained access to metadata from over a million users, primarily in the Washington D.C. area. This included information such as date and time stamps of communications, source and destination IP addresses, and phone numbers. In some cases, the content of communications may have been exposed. If you're concerned, consider adopting end-to-end encrypted communication tools and contacting your service provider for more information.
What is being done to address the threat?
U.S. government agencies including CISA, NSA, and FBI have released guidance for protecting communications infrastructure. They're working with affected companies to remove the attackers from compromised systems. The government has also recommended that individuals and organizations adopt end-to-end encrypted communications and implement enhanced security measures.
How can I protect myself?
To protect yourself, consider using end-to-end encrypted messaging and calling apps, enabling multi-factor authentication on all accounts, keeping your devices and software updated, being cautious about the information you share digitally, and monitoring your accounts for unusual activity.
