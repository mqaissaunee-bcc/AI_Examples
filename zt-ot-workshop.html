Adapting Zero Trust to OT

Faculty Workshop · 30 min · Interactive
5 minutes · Set the stage

When the network controls the physical world.

A compromised database leaks data. A compromised PLC opens a floodgate. This workshop walks through the new joint guidance from CISA, DoW, DOE, FBI, and DOS on adapting Zero Trust principles to Operational Technology — the systems that run power grids, water treatment, building automation, and manufacturing floors.

Working Definition Operational Technology (OT) is a broad range of programmable systems and devices that interact with the physical environment — sensors, actuators, controllers, HMIs. Industrial control systems, building automation, transportation systems, and physical access control all qualify. Source: NIST SP 800-82r3, cited in the joint guidance.

The five-agency premise, in one sentence

Zero Trust assumes a breach has already occurred — and it works for OT only when adapted to the operational realities of safety, availability, and decades-long equipment lifecycles. Blanket application of IT-focused Zero Trust to OT is neither reasonable nor feasible.

Why faculty should care

  • Cybersecurity faculty: Your students will graduate into a world where IT/OT convergence is the dominant attack surface. Volt Typhoon, BlackEnergy, Trisis, and Incontroller are not theoretical.
  • Engineering & electronics faculty: The PLCs and control systems your students learn on are the assets being defended. ZT changes how those systems are accessed and maintained.
  • All STEAM faculty: Critical infrastructure is interdisciplinary by nature. A water utility incident is engineering, communications, public health, business continuity, and policy — all at once.
A note on framing Throughout this lesson, we'll keep returning to a single question: "What's the worst thing that happens if this control fails?" In IT, the answer is usually about data. In OT, the answer often involves physical harm. That difference drives every adaptation.

What to bring back to your classroom.

You've now seen the structure of the guidance, the IT/OT distinction, the architecture, the framework mapping, three judgment calls, and tested your recall. Here's the through-line.

Five takeaways

  1. Assume a breach already happened. Zero Trust is a posture, not a product. In OT, that posture has to coexist with safety and availability.
  2. Inventory before architecture. Passive monitoring first; active scanning only with deep system knowledge. You cannot protect what you cannot see.
  3. Segmentation is the foundation. Microsegmentation is the natural next layer — but the guidance treats segmentation as a dynamic policy, not a one-time decision.
  4. Compensating controls are not failures. Legacy OT can't always meet ideal ZT. Out-of-band monitoring, jump hosts with MFA, and physical safeguards close gaps without disrupting operations.
  5. Cross-disciplinary teams aren't optional. Cyber + IT + OT engineering have to make tradeoffs together. The guidance returns to this point in nearly every section.

Key terms to revisit

Jump Host
Hardened, MFA-protected entry point in the OT DMZ. The only sanctioned remote-access path.
LOTL
Living off the land — adversaries using legitimate tools (PowerShell, vendor utilities) to evade detection.
Microsegmentation
Granular, policy-driven trust boundaries inside an already-segmented network.
MOC
Management of Change — the formal review process every OT change must go through.
SBOM
Software Bill of Materials — a manifest of components used by a product, key for supply chain risk.
SSVC
Stakeholder-Specific Vulnerability Categorization — decision trees for prioritizing patches against downtime.
Compensating Control
A non-ideal control used when the primary control is impossible or disruptive (e.g., out-of-band monitoring).
Break-Glass Account
Emergency-access account with limited lifespan and stringent auditing — non-negotiable in OT.
