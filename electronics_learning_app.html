Electron Shells and Energy Levels

Electron Configuration

Electrons don't randomly orbit the nucleus. They occupy specific energy levels or "shells" at discrete distances from the nucleus.

Shell Characteristics:

üí° Key Insight: Valence Electrons

Valence electrons are the electrons in the outermost shell of an atom.

Why Valence Electrons Matter:

  • Chemical Bonding: Determine how atoms bond with other atoms
  • Electrical Properties: Control how easily electrons can move
  • Material Classification: Number of valence electrons determines if material is conductor, semiconductor, or insulator
  • Energy Requirements: Easier to remove valence electrons than inner electrons

Energy Band Theory

From Individual Atoms to Solid Materials

When atoms come together to form solid materials, individual electron energy levels merge into energy "bands." This band structure determines electrical properties.

Energy Band Formation:

  1. Isolated Atom: Discrete energy levels
  2. Few Atoms: Energy levels split slightly
  3. Many Atoms: Energy levels form continuous bands
  4. Solid Material: Well-defined valence and conduction bands

‚ö†Ô∏è Temperature Effects on Atomic Behavior

Key Concept: Temperature significantly affects electron behavior and material properties.

Effects of Increasing Temperature:

Semiconductors: The Smart Materials

Semiconductors are the foundation of modern electronics. Their unique property is that conductivity can be controlled and modified.

Insulators: The Electron Barriers

Insulator Properties

Insulators prevent the flow of electric current by having very few free electrons available for conduction.

Atomic Structure:

  • 5-8 valence electrons
  • Strong covalent or ionic bonds
  • Large band gap (>3 eV)
  • Electrons tightly bound to atoms

Electrical Characteristics:

  • Very high resistance (>10¬π¬≤ Œ©)
  • Negligible current flow
  • High breakdown voltage
  • Temperature-dependent properties
