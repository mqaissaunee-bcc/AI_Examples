The Canvas Breach
of 2026

An online learning system used by nearly half of US colleges was hacked. Here's what happened, who was affected, and what to do — explained without the jargon.

§ 01

What happened, in plain English

In late April 2026, a group of hackers broke into the company that runs Canvas — the online platform that students, teachers, and parents at thousands of schools use every day for assignments, grades, and messages. The hackers stole information about up to 275 million people, then demanded the company pay them or they'd publish all of it online.

▌ Think of it this way

Imagine your school doesn't keep its records in a file cabinet at the school anymore. Instead, every school in town stores its records in one giant warehouse downtown that they all share. The warehouse company handles the locks, the alarms, and the security guards.

One night, burglars find a weak spot in the warehouse and walk out with copies of the records from every school in town. The schools themselves never had their doors broken open. They didn't do anything wrong. But the warehouse they all trusted got robbed, and now every family in every school is affected at the same time.

That's what happened with Canvas.

~9,000 Schools affected worldwide
275M People whose info was stolen (claimed)
3.65 TB Of data taken — billions of messages
41% Of US colleges run on Canvas
May 12 Hackers' threatened release date

Why this story matters

Schools get hacked all the time. This one stands out for three reasons.

First, no individual school did anything wrong. The break-in happened at the company that runs Canvas — not at any single school. Every school using Canvas got swept up in the same incident at the same moment. There was nothing a teacher, student, or campus IT department could have done to stop it.

Second, the timing was brutal. The hackers showed their hand during finals week at many colleges. Students at Penn logged in to study for exams and saw a ransom note instead of their classwork. Schools across North Carolina, California, and the Netherlands had to disconnect from Canvas entirely.

Third, it isn't over. The hackers gave a deadline of May 12 to release everything they stole. Even after that deadline passes — one way or another — the stolen names, emails, and private messages will be out there for years.

The Canvas breach is a reminder that no platform is immune. — Anton Dahbura, Johns Hopkins University Information Security Institute
§ 02

Meet the hackers

The hackers call themselves ShinyHunters. They're not teenagers messing around, and they're not a foreign government. They're a professional crime crew that has been operating since 2020 — basically a digital mafia that specializes in stealing large amounts of data and demanding ransom.

▌ A simple way to picture them

Think of an old-school protection racket. The mob doesn't break your windows for fun — they break them to make a point, then offer to "protect" your business for a monthly fee. The threat is the business model.

ShinyHunters works the same way, just with stolen data instead of broken windows. Their pattern: hack a big company, copy a huge amount of data, then send a message that says "pay us, or we publish everything we took." They make money whether the company pays or whether the data sells on underground markets.

Their track record

This isn't their first rodeo — not even close. ShinyHunters has been linked to data thefts from companies you've heard of:

  • Ticketmaster — the concert ticket company
  • AT&T Wireless — millions of phone customer records
  • Google — a corporate database
  • Santander Bank, Qantas Airlines, Jaguar Land Rover
  • The European Commission
  • Several universities directly — including Penn, Princeton, and Harvard in late 2025
  • Other school software — they've also breached PowerSchool and Infinite Campus, two of the biggest names in K-12 record systems

Why this is Instructure's second incident in eight months

In September 2025, the same group hit Instructure (Canvas's parent company) the first time, through a different system the company uses for sales and customer relationships. At the time, Instructure said no Canvas student data was taken. But eight months later, the same group came back through a different door — and this time, the door led right into Canvas.

What the hackers actually said

On May 7, the hackers replaced the regular Canvas login page at hundreds of schools with this message. Students trying to do their homework saw it instead.

▌ login.canvas.[redacted].edu — May 7, 2026
> HELLO. WE ARE SHINYHUNTERS.
>
> INSTRUCTURE WAS BREACHED. AGAIN.
> INSTEAD OF CONTACTING US TO RESOLVE IT,
> THEY IGNORED US AND DID SOME 'SECURITY PATCHES.'
>
> YOUR INSTITUTION HAS UNTIL MAY 12 TO NEGOTIATE.
> AFTER THAT, THE DATA GETS PUBLISHED.
>
> MAKE THE RIGHT DECISION.

Approximate reconstruction of the message. The actual message threatened to release "billions of private messages" between students and teachers.

§ 03

How the hackers got in

The break-in happened in two phases. First, the quiet phase: the hackers slipped in, made copies of millions of records, and slipped out without anyone noticing for weeks. Then came the loud phase: when the company tried to lock them out, they came back and made themselves visible to the entire world.

Phase 1 — The quiet copy job

▌ Picture a copy machine, not a getaway car

Hollywood movies show hackers smashing through firewalls while alarms blare. The reality is much more boring — and much more dangerous.

Imagine someone gets a janitor's keycard to a giant office building. They don't run around grabbing files. They walk to the building's photocopier, make copies of every file in the building, neatly put each original back, and walk out the front door with the copies in a duffel bag. The cleaning crew, the security guard, and the receptionist all see someone using the copier — that's a normal thing copiers are for. Nothing seems wrong.

That's how this break-in worked. The hackers used Canvas's own normal data-export features to copy the information out. To Canvas's monitoring tools, it looked like ordinary activity.

▌ The quiet copy job — April 29 to May 1, 2026

Phase 2 — The loud return

Once Canvas's parent company realized something was wrong, they patched the hole the hackers used. They told the press the situation was contained. The hackers disagreed.

▌ The hackers' message, delivered in person

Imagine a robber gets caught on camera leaving the warehouse. The warehouse changes the locks and tells everyone the problem is fixed. The next day, the same robber walks back in through a side door nobody mentioned, climbs onto the loudspeaker system, and announces to every customer in the warehouse: "We're still here. Pay us by Tuesday or we publish everything we took."

That's almost literally what happened. On May 7, the hackers used a different weakness in Canvas to rewrite the login screens of hundreds of schools. Students and teachers logging in to do their work saw a ransom message instead.

The takeaway

The most surprising thing about how this break-in worked is what didn't happen:

  • No virus on any school computer.
  • No phishing email any student or teacher fell for.
  • No "ransomware" that locked up school files.
  • No campus network that got broken into.

The hackers walked into a vendor's online system and used the system's own normal features to copy data out. Every traditional defense at every affected school worked exactly the way it was supposed to — and that didn't matter, because the break-in happened somewhere else entirely.

§ 04

How it all unfolded

Here's the order things happened, in everyday language. The whole story, from the first warning sign to the moment Canvas came back online, took about ten days.

  1. Sep 2025

    The warning shot

    The same group of hackers breaks into a different system at Canvas's parent company through phone-based scams of company employees. The company says no Canvas student data was affected and moves on.

  2. Apr 29, 2026

    The first sign of trouble

    Something unusual is detected inside Canvas's systems. Hackers had quietly broken in by exploiting a flaw the company hadn't known about.

  3. Apr 30

    The status page goes yellow

    The company posts a vague notice that some customers may see "limited disruptions" and takes parts of Canvas offline as a precaution. No one outside the company yet knows what's actually happening.

  4. May 1

    The public learns

    The company publicly confirms a "cybersecurity incident" caused by a "criminal threat actor." They say they've kicked the intruder out and brought in outside investigators.

  5. May 3

    "Pay or leak"

    The hackers reveal themselves on a hidden website used to publish stolen data. They claim they took 3.65 terabytes of data from 9,000 schools and 275 million people. They give the company three days to respond.

  6. May 4–5

    Schools get the call

    The company starts notifying schools and universities directly. Confirmed stolen: names, email addresses, student ID numbers, and private Canvas messages. Confirmed safe: passwords, dates of birth, Social Security numbers, financial info.

  7. May 7

    The defacement

    The hackers come back. They rewrite the Canvas login pages at hundreds of schools to display a ransom note. Students at Penn studying for finals see it. The company takes Canvas offline. New deadline: May 12.

  8. May 7

    Schools start cutting Canvas off

    North Carolina pulls Canvas access from every public K-12 school in the state. The University of California system tells all its campuses to block Canvas. Forty-four universities in the Netherlands disconnect Canvas from their internal systems.

  9. May 8

    Partial restoration

    Canvas comes back online. The company permanently shuts down a free version of Canvas (the part the hackers used to come back the second time). Investigations continue. The May 12 deadline still stands.

§ 05

What was stolen — and who's affected

What the hackers took

▌ What the hackers got — and what they didn't

▌ Why "no passwords stolen" isn't the whole story

People hear "no passwords were taken" and breathe a sigh of relief. But think about what was taken. The hackers now have your name, your school email, your student ID number, and the private messages you sent your professors and classmates.

That's not enough to log into your bank account. But it's more than enough to write you a phony email that says, "Hi [Your Real Name], I'm following up on your message to Professor Smith last Tuesday about the makeup exam in BIO 101..." — and have it look completely real. The danger isn't that the hackers will use this data themselves. It's that they'll sell it to other criminals who specialize in scams that sound true.

Who got hit

A who's-who of education. The hackers' list of affected institutions includes:

▌ Top US universities

Including the Ivy League

Harvard, Princeton, Penn, Columbia, Yale, MIT, Stanford, Penn State, Rutgers, the entire University of California system, Georgetown, Kent State, University of Washington, University of Utah.

▌ Big K-12 districts

Public schools, too

Wake County and Durham (NC), Clark County / Las Vegas, Broward County, Houston ISD, Charlotte-Mecklenburg. North Carolina pulled Canvas access from every K-12 school statewide.

▌ International

Beyond the US

UK universities, 44 institutions in the Netherlands, schools in Sweden, Australia, and New Zealand. Several Dutch universities physically disconnected Canvas from their networks.

▌ Corporate users

Not just schools

Canvas is also used for company training. Apple appears on the leaked customer list. The total list includes about 15,000 organizations of all kinds.

The everyday disruption

Beyond the data theft itself, the immediate disruption was real:

  • Finals got rescheduled. Universities that lost Canvas access during exam week had to extend deadlines and improvise.
  • Whole states pulled the plug. North Carolina removed Canvas from its statewide login system for K-12 schools.
  • Other apps stopped working. Many third-party tools that connect to Canvas — plagiarism checkers, online textbooks, grade syncs — broke and required teachers to reconnect them.
  • A free version is gone. The company permanently shut down its free tier for individual teachers, which it said was the entry point the hackers used to come back the second time.

What happens next

The data doesn't go bad. Names, email addresses, student IDs, and the contents of private messages will be useful to scammers for years. The most likely fallout, working out from this point:

  • Convincing scam emails that mention real course names, real professors, and real conversations.
  • Financial-aid and scholarship scams aimed at students by name.
  • Attempts to break into other accounts (email, social media) where students reused their school email address.
  • Fraud calls to school help desks: "Hi, I'm student #12345, I forgot my password" — with a real student ID number to back it up.
§ 06

How the company has responded

Canvas's parent company is named Instructure. Their response so far has been steady but not without criticism. Here's what they've done, and where the response has fallen short.

What Instructure has done

  • Locked the hackers out as soon as the break-in was detected (April 30 – May 1).
  • Hired an outside cybersecurity firm to investigate.
  • Notified law enforcement.
  • Reset the digital "keys" that other apps use to talk to Canvas (which is why teachers may need to reconnect tools they use).
  • Patched the security flaw the hackers used.
  • Started telling affected schools directly on May 4–5.
  • After the second break-in, took Canvas offline, shut down the free version permanently, and brought Canvas back up on May 8.
  • Set up a public webpage with status updates.
As we respond to this incident, we're focused on three things: completing a rigorous investigation, communicating verified information to impacted customers, and continuing to strengthen the safeguards that protect customer and student data. — Instructure statement, May 8, 2026

Where the response has been criticized

The response has been workmanlike, but several things have raised eyebrows:

  • The patches didn't hold. The second break-in happened after the company publicly said the situation was contained. The hackers still had a way back in.
  • This is the second time in eight months. The same group, the same company, the same playbook of stealing data and demanding ransom. Customers are reasonably asking what changed after September 2025 — and why those changes weren't enough.
  • The break-in went undetected for days. The hackers say they took 3.65 terabytes of data — a huge amount — between April 29 and May 1. Instructure hasn't explained how that much data got out without setting off alarms.
  • The company hasn't said exactly what flaw was used. That makes it hard for outside experts to assess how safe Canvas is now.

One thing the company hasn't said publicly

Whether Instructure has, or hasn't, paid the ransom. Their public statements neither confirm nor deny any negotiation with the hackers. As of May 8, ShinyHunters' May 12 deadline to release the stolen data was still in effect.

§ 07

What you should do — practical steps

You can't undo a break-in that already happened. What you can do is protect yourself from the scams that will follow over the next year or two. Here's a practical checklist.

  1. ▌ Everyone

    Treat any Canvas-related email as suspicious for now

    Don't click links in emails about Canvas, your grades, your assignments, password resets, or financial aid. Open Canvas yourself by typing your school's web address into the browser. The stolen data makes it easy to send scam emails that look very personal and very real.

  2. ▌ Everyone

    Change your school password if anything looked weird

    Instructure says no passwords were taken, but if you saw the ransom message or any odd pop-up while logged in, change your school password just in case. Also change any password you've reused on other sites with your school email — that's good practice anytime.

  3. ▌ Students

    Be extra suspicious of "official" messages that sound very specific

    An email that says "regarding your message to Professor Lee in CHEM 101 about your missing lab report" is more likely to be a scam now than before, not less. The hackers have your real course names, your real professors' names, and your real conversations. The fact that an email knows this stuff isn't proof it's real anymore.

  4. ▌ Students

    Watch for unexpected login alerts on your phone

    If your school uses a phone app to verify logins (Duo, Microsoft Authenticator, or similar), and you get a request to approve a login you didn't try to do — deny it. Don't tap "approve" out of habit. If you keep getting them, contact your school IT help desk.

  5. ▌ Parents

    Talk to your kids about phishing — especially scholarship and grade scams

    K-12 students are on this stolen list too. Expect targeted scams promising scholarships, claiming grade problems, or impersonating teachers. Make sure your kids know to show you any email that asks them to click a link or share information.

  6. ▌ Teachers

    Reconnect outside tools carefully

    Because Instructure reset the digital keys, you may need to reconnect tools like Turnitin, publisher textbooks, or grading apps. Read each permission prompt. If something asks for "full access to all your courses" and you don't recognize it, don't approve it.

  7. ▌ Teachers

    Assume Canvas messages were not actually private

    Treat your past Canvas inbox conversations as if they could become public. For sensitive topics going forward — grades, accommodations, mental health concerns, anything covered by privacy laws — consider using your school email or talking in person, and document accordingly.

  8. ▌ Everyone

    Bookmark your school's IT alerts page

    Things like this play out in waves. The most accurate information for your specific school will come from your campus or district's IT or security office — not the news, not social media. Find that page now and bookmark it.

§ 08

Spot the scam — practice with real examples

In the weeks and months after a breach like this, scam emails get much better at looking real. Here are three example emails to practice on. Click the highlighted parts to see why they're suspicious.

▌ Click the highlighted phrases to see why they're red flags.

From: Canvas Notifications <notifications@canvas-instructure-edu.com>
To: jsmith@brookdalecc.edu
Subject: Action required — grade discrepancy in BIO 101 Spring 2026

Hello Jane Smith,

We've identified a problem with your final grade in BIO 101 — Introduction to Biology (Section 02, Spring 2026). Your instructor flagged this on May 6.

To resolve this within 48 hours, please review the issue and confirm using your Canvas login:

→ Review and confirm here

If you do not respond, the grade will be finalized as currently recorded.

Brookdale Canvas Support
support@brookdale-canvas-support.org

▌ 0 of 4 red flags found

Verdict: This is a scam

Four giveaways: (1) the sender domain looks like Instructure but isn't; (2) a fake 48-hour deadline to make you panic; (3) a link to "resolve" a grade — real Canvas doesn't work that way; (4) a different lookalike support email at the bottom. Notice how the email uses real-sounding details like a real course name. That's exactly the kind of personalization that became easier for scammers after the breach.

From: IT Help Desk <helpdesk@brookdalecc.edu>
To: jsmith@brookdalecc.edu
Subject: Following up on your password reset request

Hi Jane,

We received a password reset request as you requested earlier today.

To verify your identity, please reply to this email with your student ID and Canvas password so we can complete the reset.

⚠ Please reply with both pieces of information for verification.

Once verified, we'll send you a reset link.

— Brookdale IT Help Desk

▌ 0 of 2 red flags found

Verdict: This is definitely a scam

The single most reliable rule in all of email safety: no real IT department will ever ask you to email your password. Not for verification, not for any reason. Even when the email address at the top looks completely correct (and yours can be faked too), an email asking for a password is fake. This one also asks for a student ID — exactly the information that was stolen in the breach, which scammers can then use to call your real help desk pretending to be you.

From: Canvas <noreply@instructure.com>
To: jsmith@brookdalecc.edu
Subject: Re-authorize your integrations

Following the recent security incident, you'll need to reconnect any external tools that work with your Canvas account.

You can do this directly inside Canvas:

  1. Log in to Canvas at your school's normal web address
  2. Go to Account → Settings → Approved Integrations
  3. Reconnect tools you recognize and use
  4. Remove anything you don't recognize

For help, contact your school's IT help desk.

— Instructure Security

▌ Look carefully — what makes this one different?

Verdict: Probably real — but verify anyway

This one passes all the standard tests: no urgent deadline, no link to click, no request for your password or personal info, and the action it tells you to take (going to Canvas yourself through your normal web address) is exactly what good security advice looks like. Even so, the right habit during a security incident is: type the Canvas address yourself in your browser, then check your settings from inside Canvas. If anything seems off, call your help desk using a phone number you find on your school's official website — never a number that came in the email.
