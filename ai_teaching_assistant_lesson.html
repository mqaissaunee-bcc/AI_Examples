1. What Are AI Teaching Assistants?

Understanding the foundation before you build

An AI teaching assistant is a customized version of a large language model (LLM) that you configure with specific instructions, knowledge, and constraints to serve a defined educational purpose. Unlike a general chatbot you simply open and type into, these tools are shaped by you — your course context, your tone, your goals.

Think of it this way: a general AI is like a knowledgeable stranger. A custom AI teaching assistant is like a well-briefed colleague who knows your syllabus, your students' level, and your pedagogical approach.

🎯 What it CAN do

Answer student questions 24/7, explain concepts in multiple ways, generate practice problems, summarize course readings, guide students through tasks with scaffolding.

⚠️ What it CANNOT do

Replace human feedback and mentorship, access your LMS gradebook, guarantee factual accuracy, or handle sensitive student issues (mental health, accommodations, etc.).

The two main platforms

Two tools have emerged as the most faculty-accessible options for building custom AI assistants without any coding:

Google Workspace

Google Gems

Custom AI assistants built within Google's Gemini ecosystem. Available to users with a Google account (Gemini Advanced for full features).

OpenAI / ChatGPT

Custom GPTs

Customized ChatGPT assistants built in the GPT Editor. Available to ChatGPT Plus, Team, and Enterprise subscribers.

2. Google Gems vs. Custom GPTs

Choosing the right tool for your context

Both platforms let you create a custom AI persona with tailored instructions and knowledge. The right choice often comes down to what tools your institution already uses and what features matter most to your workflow.

Example

If your college uses Google Workspace (Gmail, Drive, Docs), a Google Gem integrates seamlessly with files in your Drive. If you prefer ChatGPT's conversational quality and want to upload PDFs like a syllabus or textbook chapters, a Custom GPT may suit you better.

Bottom line for faculty: Either tool works well for a classroom teaching assistant. If your students already use Google, start with Gems. If your campus has ChatGPT Plus access, Custom GPTs offer slightly more robust knowledge-base uploads and sharing options.

3. Building a Google Gem

Step-by-step guide to your first teaching assistant

A Google Gem is created entirely through the Gemini web interface — no coding required. You write instructions in plain English, optionally attach files, and give the Gem a name. Here's the full process:

Go to gemini.google.com and sign in with your Google account. In the left sidebar, click Gems (or "Explore Gems"). You'll need a Gemini Advanced subscription (included in Google One AI Premium or Google Workspace add-ons). Click "New Gem" to open the editor.

Tip

Check with your institution's IT department — some colleges have Gemini Advanced available through their Workspace for Education agreement at no extra cost.

Give your Gem a clear, descriptive name (e.g., "ENG 101 Study Buddy" or "Bio 200 Lab Guide"). Write a short description so you remember its purpose. This name is visible to any students you share it with, so keep it professional and course-specific.

This is the most important step. In the Instructions field, describe:

  • Role: "You are a study assistant for ENG 101 at Brookdale Community College."
  • Tone: "Use an encouraging, conversational tone appropriate for first-year college students."
  • Tasks: "Help students analyze essay prompts, brainstorm thesis statements, and review MLA citation format."
  • Limits: "Do not write complete essays for students. Instead, guide them with questions and examples."
  • Escalation: "For questions about grades or accommodations, direct students to contact the instructor."
Example instruction snippet

"When a student asks for help with an essay, first ask them to share their thesis. Then respond with specific questions to help them strengthen it, rather than rewriting it for them."

You can attach files from Google Drive — your syllabus, a course schedule, lecture notes, or a reading list. The Gem will use this content to answer student questions with course-specific accuracy. For example, attaching your MLA guide means the Gem can answer citation questions precisely as covered in your class.

Privacy reminder

Do not upload documents containing student PII (names, grades, IDs). Only upload general course materials.

Use the preview panel to test your Gem before sharing. Try questions a student might ask — both on-topic and off-topic — to see if the Gem respects your instructions. Adjust the instructions as needed. Once satisfied, click Save. You can then share the link with students via Canvas, email, or your course page.

4. Building a Custom GPT

Step-by-step guide using ChatGPT's GPT Editor

ChatGPT's Custom GPT builder offers two creation methods: a conversational GPT Builder (you describe what you want and it builds it) or a manual Configure tab where you write everything yourself. Most faculty find the Configure tab gives more precise control.

▶

Walkthrough: Creating a Custom GPT

Video: Custom GPT builder walkthrough. If unavailable, search YouTube for "how to create custom GPT ChatGPT tutorial".

Log into ChatGPT at chatgpt.com. In the left sidebar, click Explore GPTs, then "+ Create" in the top-right corner. This opens the GPT Editor. Click the Configure tab for full manual control over your settings.

Give your GPT a course-specific name. You can upload a custom avatar image or have DALL-E generate one for you. The description appears in the GPT Store (if public) or on the share page students see when they open the link. Keep it clear and inviting.

Example

Name: "PSY 101 Concept Coach" | Description: "Helps students understand core psychology concepts from Dr. Rivera's Introduction to Psychology course."

In the Instructions box (equivalent to a system prompt), define the GPT's role, behavior, tone, and limits. Be explicit — the more specific your instructions, the more reliably the GPT stays on task.

  • Define what the GPT will help with (e.g., concept review, study guides)
  • Define what it will not do (e.g., complete assignments, share other students' work)
  • Set the tone: academic but approachable
  • Tell it how to handle off-topic questions ("Redirect to course topics only")

ChatGPT lets you add up to 4 conversation starters — button-style prompts students see when they open the GPT. These lower the activation energy for students unsure how to begin. Examples: "Help me study for the midterm", "Explain the difference between classical and operant conditioning", or "Quiz me on this week's vocab."

Under Knowledge, upload PDFs, Word documents, or text files. This is where you'd upload your syllabus, course notes, or a terminology guide. The GPT will reference these when answering questions, keeping its answers grounded in your course content. File size limit is 20MB per file.

Best practice

Create a simple "Course FAQ" document that covers common student questions (late policy, office hours, assignment formats) and upload it. The GPT can then answer these accurately without fabricating details.

Click Save then choose your sharing level: Only me (testing), Anyone with the link (recommended for course use), or Public in GPT Store. For classroom use, "Anyone with the link" gives you control. Share the link in Canvas, your syllabus, or an announcement. Students with a free ChatGPT account can use your GPT up to a usage limit; Plus accounts have higher limits.

5. Designing Effective System Prompts

The heart of a well-behaved AI assistant

The system prompt (called "Instructions" in both platforms) is your most powerful lever. It shapes everything the AI does — its personality, its knowledge scope, its guardrails. A vague prompt produces a generic assistant. A precise, thoughtful prompt produces one that genuinely supports your students.

A good system prompt answers five questions: Who is this AI? · What should it do? · What should it NOT do? · How should it speak? · What should it do when it doesn't know?

The TRACE Framework for Teaching Prompts

Use this structure when writing your instructions:

State clearly what the AI is and does: "You are a Socratic tutor for Philosophy 101. Your job is to help students think through ethical dilemmas using guided questioning, not to provide final answers."

Explicitly list limits: "Do not write essays or complete assignments. Do not discuss topics outside course content. If asked about grades, tell the student to email the instructor directly." Restrictions are just as important as capabilities.

Help the AI calibrate its language: "You are speaking with first-year community college students, many of whom are returning adults balancing work and family. Use plain language, avoid jargon, and be patient and encouraging."

Embed key course information: "This course uses APA 7th edition citations. The required textbook is 'Psychology: Themes and Variations' by Weiten. Major assignments include three reaction papers and a final research paper."

Always include a referral instruction: "For any concerns about mental health, academic difficulty, or disability accommodations, respond with: 'I'm not the right resource for that, but [instructor name]'s office hours are [X] or you can contact Student Services at [number].'"

6. Ethics, Privacy & Academic Integrity

Deploying responsibly in higher education

Deploying an AI assistant in your classroom is an act of instructional design — and it carries the same responsibilities as any other design choice. Students will interact with this tool as if it represents your course, so getting the ethics right matters.

Key considerations

Neither Google Gems nor ChatGPT Custom GPTs are certified FERPA-compliant platforms by default. Never include student names, grades, ID numbers, or any personally identifiable information (PII) in your Gem's knowledge files or instructions. Design the assistant to support learning in the aggregate, not track individual students.

Check with your institution's privacy officer or IT department before deploying AI tools in courses. Some institutions have negotiated data agreements with Google or OpenAI.

Be explicit with students about how the AI should be used. Include a statement in your syllabus: what the AI assistant is for, what it's not for, and whether they must disclose when they've used it. A well-designed AI assistant that guides rather than completes is actually an academic integrity-supporting tool — it helps students do their own thinking.

Syllabus language example

"This course uses an AI study assistant to help you review concepts and get feedback on your thinking. The AI will not write your assignments. If you use the AI to help you brainstorm or understand a concept, you do not need to cite it — but if it generates text you include in submitted work, that must be disclosed and cited."

Not all students have equal access to ChatGPT Plus or Gemini Advanced. If you build a teaching assistant on a paid tier, ensure there is always an alternative path for students who cannot access the tool. Consider whether your institution provides access, or whether a free-tier option (like a basic Gem or a free Custom GPT within ChatGPT's free limits) would suffice.

AI tools can "hallucinate" — confidently state incorrect information. This is especially risky in factual or procedural domains. Mitigate this by uploading authoritative source documents (your notes, the textbook chapter) and by instructing the AI to say "I'm not certain — please verify with your instructor or textbook" when it encounters gaps. Never present the AI as infallible.

7. Other Options Worth Knowing

Beyond Google Gems and Custom GPTs — a landscape tour

Google Gems and ChatGPT Custom GPTs are the most widely adopted platforms for building faculty-designed AI teaching assistants, but they're not the only options. Depending on what your institution already uses, your comfort level, and your goals, one of the tools below might be a better fit — or a useful complement.

We've grouped them into two categories: Configurable Assistants (tools where you build and customize the AI, like Gems and Custom GPTs) and Purpose-Built Education Tools (tools that come pre-configured for classroom tasks, but with less flexibility to customize).

🔧 Configurable Assistants

These platforms — like Gems and Custom GPTs — let you write instructions, upload documents, and shape the AI's behavior. You're building something, not just using something.

M

Microsoft Copilot Studio

Microsoft 365 Low-Code Builder
May be free via M365

If your institution runs Microsoft 365 (Teams, SharePoint, Outlook), Copilot Studio is the Microsoft equivalent of building a Custom GPT — and it may already be included in your existing license. Faculty can create custom AI assistants using a drag-and-drop, low-code interface, attach SharePoint or OneDrive documents as a knowledge base, and deploy the assistant directly inside Microsoft Teams or as a shareable link.

What makes this particularly compelling for higher ed: Copilot Chat (the underlying assistant) is included at no extra cost with Microsoft 365 A1/A3/A5 education licenses, and Microsoft has announced Canvas LTI integration, meaning your AI assistant could eventually live directly inside your course shell. Governance and data privacy are enterprise-grade — Microsoft explicitly states that data is not used to train foundation models, which addresses a common faculty concern.

Best for

Faculty at institutions heavily invested in Microsoft 365 who want tighter data controls and potential LMS integration. Slightly steeper learning curve than Gems or Custom GPTs, but the institutional safety story is stronger.

🌐 copilotstudio.microsoft.com 💰 Included with M365 Copilot license ($18/user/mo academic) or free Copilot Chat with A1/A3/A5
N

Google NotebookLM

Google Document Intelligence
Free

NotebookLM is Google's free document-intelligence tool, and it's become a faculty favorite for a specific and powerful use case: you upload your course materials (syllabus, lecture notes, readings, PDFs), and the tool becomes an expert on exactly those documents. Students can then ask it questions and it responds with answers grounded exclusively in what you uploaded — no hallucinated outside information, no Wikipedia rabbit holes.

It's not quite a "configurable assistant" in the same way as Gems — you can't give it a persona, set conversation starters, or enforce guardrails through system instructions. But for a document Q&A bot tied to your course readings, it's the fastest and most accurate option available, and it's completely free. NotebookLM also generates audio overviews (a two-voice podcast-style summary of your materials) that students find genuinely useful for review.

Best for

Faculty who want students to interrogate course texts and primary sources directly, without the AI straying into general internet knowledge. Upload a dense research article or case study and let students explore it conversationally. No prompt engineering required.

🌐 notebooklm.google.com 💰 Free (Google account required)
C

Claude (Anthropic) — Projects Feature

Anthropic Persistent Context
Pro: $20/mo

Claude is Anthropic's AI assistant, known for its strength with long, complex documents and nuanced writing tasks — which makes it particularly well-suited to courses with heavy reading or writing demands. Claude's Projects feature (available on the Pro plan) functions similarly to a Custom GPT: you set persistent instructions for how Claude should behave, upload course documents, and every conversation within that Project inherits your context.

The main limitation for classroom use is shareability — unlike a Custom GPT link you can drop into Canvas, a Claude Project is personal to your account. It's excellent for your own course prep work (drafting rubrics, generating assignment variants, analyzing student writing patterns), but less suited to building a tool you hand off to students directly. That may change as Anthropic develops its platform further.

Best for

Faculty professional use: drafting assessments, generating differentiated versions of materials, analyzing dense texts, or building a persistent "course brain" that remembers your teaching context across sessions. Not the strongest choice for a student-facing chatbot today.

🌐 claude.ai 💰 Free tier available; Pro at $20/month for Projects and file uploads

🎓 Purpose-Built Education Tools

These tools come ready to use for classroom tasks — less customization, but no prompt engineering required. Think of them as pre-configured teaching assistants rather than platforms where you build your own.

🪄

MagicSchool.ai

Free Forever plan

The most comprehensive educator-facing AI suite available today, with over 80 tools covering lesson planning, rubric generation, IEP support, differentiation, parent communications, and more. Built by educators specifically for educators — no prompting skills required, just fill in the fields. Used by over 3 million educators worldwide. The student-facing side ("Raina") provides guided AI interaction within guardrails. If you want a faculty productivity tool rather than a custom student assistant, MagicSchool is the strongest all-in-one option.

🌐 magicschool.ai 💰 Free; Plus plan ~$8.33/mo
📐

Khanmigo (Khan Academy)

Free for educators

Khan Academy's AI tutor, designed from the ground up to guide students toward answers through Socratic questioning rather than simply providing them. It's built on GPT models but heavily guardrailed for educational safety. Particularly strong in math, science, and humanities aligned to Khan Academy's content library. Free for teachers, and now used by over 400,000 educators across 50+ countries through a Microsoft partnership. Ideal if your course content maps well to Khan Academy's subject areas; less useful for niche or specialized college-level topics where the content library doesn't reach.

🌐 khanmigo.ai 💰 Free for educators; ~$9/mo for students
📋

Eduaide.ai

Free trial

A focused content-generation workspace for educators, offering over 100 content types — from syllabi and discussion prompts to worksheets, IEP plans, and differentiated readings. Eduaide's strength is speed and specificity: give it a topic, grade level, and content type, and it produces a polished, curriculum-aligned draft in seconds. It also supports 15+ languages, making it useful for ESL-adjacent contexts. Less of a student-facing chatbot and more of a faculty prep accelerator — the "Teaching Assistant" bot helps plan while the "Assessment Builder" generates aligned quizzes.

🌐 eduaide.ai 💰 Free trial; paid plans available
📊

Diffit

Free tier available

A differentiation-focused tool that takes any text, topic, or URL and instantly generates leveled reading materials — making it easy to adapt a dense academic article into accessible versions for students reading at different levels. For community college faculty serving diverse populations (developmental education, ESL, returning adults), Diffit fills a real gap. It generates vocabulary lists, comprehension questions, and graphic organizers alongside the adapted text. Not a conversational assistant, but a highly practical content-adaptation engine.

🌐 diffit.me 💰 Free tier; Pro plan for more features

What's next?

Now that you've completed this lesson, here are your next steps for building your first AI teaching assistant:

  1. Identify one specific task in your course where students commonly need support (e.g., understanding a concept, reviewing for an exam, getting citation help).
  2. Draft your system prompt using the TRACE framework from Section 5.
  3. Choose your platform: Google Gems if your institution uses Google Workspace, or Custom GPT if you have ChatGPT Plus.
  4. Upload your syllabus and one or two relevant course documents as knowledge files.
  5. Test the assistant yourself for 10 minutes before sharing with students.
  6. Add a brief statement to your syllabus explaining how the assistant should be used.
Remember

Your first AI teaching assistant doesn't have to be perfect. Start simple, gather student feedback, and iterate. The goal is to support student learning — and that goal will guide every design decision.
