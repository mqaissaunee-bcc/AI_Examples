1. What Are AI Assistants?

Understanding the foundation before you build

An AI assistant is a customized version of a large language model (LLM) that you configure with specific instructions, knowledge, and constraints to serve a defined purpose. Unlike a general chatbot you simply open and type into, these tools are shaped by you — your context, your tone, your goals, your boundaries.

Think of it this way: a general AI is like a knowledgeable stranger. A custom AI assistant is like a well-briefed colleague who knows your office's policies, your audience, and your preferred approach.

🎯 What it CAN do well

Answer routine questions 24/7, explain things multiple ways, draft first passes of documents, summarize long inputs, walk people through procedures, generate practice items, and offload repetitive Q&A.

⚠️ What it CANNOT do

Replace human judgment, access your live institutional systems (LMS, SIS, HR), guarantee factual accuracy, or handle sensitive situations involving mental health, accommodations, personnel matters, or legal questions.

The two main platforms

Two tools have emerged as the most accessible options for building custom AI assistants without any coding:

Google Workspace

Google Gems

Custom AI assistants built within Google's Gemini ecosystem. Available to users with a Google account; full features with paid Google AI plans or institutional Workspace deployments.

OpenAI / ChatGPT

Custom GPTs

Customized ChatGPT assistants built in the GPT Editor. Available to ChatGPT Plus, Team, Edu, and Enterprise subscribers.

Not just for student-facing work. The same tools you'd use to build a course study assistant can build a Title IX policy navigator, an advising-appointment prep tool, or a committee-report drafter. The use case differs — the design principles don't.

3. Building a Google Gem

Step-by-step guide to your first Gem

A Gem is Google's name for a customized version of Gemini that you create once and reuse. It lives in the left sidebar of your Gemini app and acts exactly like the AI you've configured. Here's how to build one.

▶

Google Gems in Action

Video: Walkthrough of Google Gems features and setup. If this video is unavailable, search YouTube for "Google Gemini Gems tutorial".

Go to gemini.google.com and sign in with your Google account. In the left sidebar, click Gems (or Gem manager depending on your version). Click + New Gem to start.

Choose a name your audience will recognize. The description appears under the Gem name and helps users decide whether they're in the right place.

Example

Name: "Advising Prep Helper" · Description: "Helps academic advisors prepare for upcoming appointments using student-provided context."

This is the heart of your Gem — what tells it how to behave. Use the TRACE framework from Section 6 to write this. Be specific. The more clearly you describe the role, constraints, audience, context, and edge-case behavior, the more reliably the Gem stays on task.

Upload Google Docs, PDFs, slides, or other reference materials. The Gem will use these as authoritative source material — grounding its answers in your content instead of making things up. Good candidates: policy documents, procedure manuals, syllabi, FAQ documents, glossaries.

Use the preview panel to test before sharing. Ask realistic questions — both on-topic and off-topic — to see if the Gem respects your instructions. Adjust as needed. Once satisfied, click Save. You can share the Gem within your Workspace or via link.

4. Building a Custom GPT

Step-by-step guide using ChatGPT's GPT Editor

ChatGPT's Custom GPT builder offers two creation modes: a conversational GPT Builder (you describe what you want and it builds it) or a manual Configure tab where you write everything yourself. Most people find the Configure tab gives more precise control.

▶

Walkthrough: Creating a Custom GPT

Video: Custom GPT builder walkthrough. If unavailable, search YouTube for "how to create custom GPT ChatGPT tutorial".

Log into ChatGPT at chatgpt.com. In the left sidebar, click GPTs (or Explore GPTs), then + Create in the top-right. Click the Configure tab for full manual control.

Give your GPT a clear, specific name. You can upload a custom avatar image or have DALL-E generate one. The description appears on the share page when users open the link.

Example

Name: "Perkins Request Helper" · Description: "Walks faculty through the steps to submit a Perkins equipment request at Brookdale Community College."

In the Instructions box, define the GPT's role, behavior, tone, and limits. Use the TRACE framework from Section 6. Be explicit — the more specific your instructions, the more reliably the GPT stays on task.

  • Define what the GPT will help with
  • Define what it will not do
  • Set the tone for your audience
  • Tell it how to handle off-topic questions

ChatGPT lets you add up to 4 conversation starters — button-style prompts users see when they open the GPT. These lower the activation energy for someone unsure how to begin. Every example in the Gallery (Section 7) suggests 3–4 ready-to-use starters.

Under Knowledge, upload PDFs, Word documents, or text files. The GPT will reference these when answering — keeping its answers grounded in your content. File size limit is typically 20 MB per file with a cap on number of files.

Best practice

Create a simple "FAQ" document covering common questions and upload it. The GPT can answer accurately without inventing details.

Click Save / Create, then choose sharing level: Only me (testing), Anyone with the link (recommended for internal use), or Public in GPT Store. For most institutional uses, "Anyone with the link" gives you control while letting your audience access it freely.

5. Building a Copilot Studio Agent

Step-by-step guide for institutions on Microsoft 365 with Copilot

An agent is Microsoft's term for what Google calls a Gem and OpenAI calls a Custom GPT. Same concept: a named, custom-instructed AI assistant with optional knowledge sources and a defined audience. The major distinction is that Copilot Studio agents run inside your institution's Microsoft 365 tenant — meaning they can read from SharePoint sites, OneDrive, and other internal sources with the user's existing permissions, and they can publish directly into Microsoft Teams as a primary channel.

This section is most useful if your institution has Microsoft 365 with Copilot licensing. If you're unsure whether you have access, sign in to office.com with your work account and scroll to the bottom of the home page to see your Copilot label, or ask IT whether your tenant includes Copilot Studio access.

▶

Walkthrough: Creating a Copilot Studio Agent

Video: Copilot Studio agent builder walkthrough. If unavailable, search YouTube for "how to create Copilot Studio agent tutorial".

Go to copilotstudio.microsoft.com and sign in with your work or school Microsoft 365 account. Pick the environment you want to build in (most institutions have a default environment; your IT may have set up dedicated environments for specific departments). If self-service sign-up is enabled and you don't have a license, you'll be offered a free trial that lets you build and test but not publish.

From the home screen, choose Create → New agent. You can use the conversational Describe mode (you describe what you want and Copilot Studio scaffolds it) or skip to the Configure tab for full manual control. Most builders working from a TRACE prompt find Configure faster. Give the agent a clear, specific name and a short description users will see when they open it.

Example

Name: "Advising Prep Helper" · Description: "Helps academic advisors prepare for upcoming appointments using student-provided context."

The Instructions field on the agent's Overview tab is where the TRACE framework lives. Paste the full prompt — Task, Restrictions, Audience, Context, Edge cases & handoff. The instructions can be lengthy; Copilot Studio handles multi-paragraph system prompts well.

  • Define what the agent will help with
  • Define what it will not do
  • Set the tone for your audience
  • Tell it how to handle off-topic questions or sensitive situations

Under Starter prompts, add the conversation starters that appear when someone opens the agent. These lower the activation energy for first-time users. Every example in the Gallery (Section 7) suggests 3–4 starters you can paste in.

Under Knowledge, you can connect the agent to several source types — this is where Copilot Studio's integration with your Microsoft 365 tenant becomes powerful:

  • SharePoint sites or pages — point at a specific site or library; the agent reads documents with the user's existing permissions
  • OneDrive files — your own files
  • File uploads — direct PDF/Word/text uploads, similar to Gems and Custom GPTs
  • Public websites — let the agent reference specific URLs
  • Dataverse tables — if your institution stores structured data in Dataverse
Best practice

For a Policy Navigator or staff Q&A agent, pointing at the SharePoint site where the policy documents already live is usually better than uploading copies — answers stay current as the policy site is updated.

The test chat panel is on the right side of the agent editor. Test realistic on-topic questions and "what if you tried to break this?" questions before publishing. Watch how the agent handles topics it shouldn't engage with and verify it follows your Restrictions and Edge cases instructions reliably.

Click Publish. Then choose where users will access the agent under Channels:

  • Microsoft Teams — the most-used channel in most institutions; colleagues can chat with the agent inside their existing Teams app
  • Demo website — a quick public link for testing
  • Custom website — embed via iframe on your department or office site
  • Direct link — share to specific colleagues

Note on governance: Publishing inside an institutional tenant typically triggers your IT department's governance review — especially for agents that touch SharePoint sites with sensitive data. Check with your IT or Copilot administrator before publishing to colleagues. Building and testing is mostly self-serve; publishing widely is where institutional policy kicks in.

What's different about Copilot Studio agents

The TRACE framework, gallery examples, and ethics guardrails in the rest of this lesson all apply to Copilot Studio agents the same way they apply to Gems and GPTs. But a few platform-specific points are worth knowing:

Tenant boundary

Agents operate inside your institution's Microsoft 365 tenant. Documents you reference from SharePoint inherit their existing permissions — users who can't see a source file won't get answers grounded in it. This is a meaningfully different security posture from consumer Gems or public Custom GPTs.

Teams as the front door

Publishing to Microsoft Teams puts the agent inside the tool your colleagues already have open. For institutional rollouts, this is often the most adopted channel — much higher engagement than a separate web link.

6. Designing Effective System Prompts

The TRACE framework — the heart of a well-behaved AI assistant

The system prompt (called "Instructions" in all three platforms) is your most powerful lever. It shapes everything the AI does — its personality, its knowledge scope, its guardrails. A vague prompt produces a generic assistant. A precise, thoughtful prompt produces one that genuinely supports your audience.

▶

Prompt Engineering for Educators & Staff

Video: Prompt design strategies for institutional AI tools. If unavailable, search YouTube for "prompt engineering ChatGPT tutorial".

A good system prompt answers five questions: Who is this AI? · What should it do? · What should it NOT do? · How should it speak? · What should it do when it doesn't know — or when the situation needs a human?

The TRACE Framework

Use this structure when writing your instructions. It works for student-facing tutors, staff workflow assistants, and administrative drafters alike.

State clearly what the AI is and does. One or two sentences. Be specific about the role and what makes this assistant different from a generic chatbot.

Teaching example

"You are a Socratic tutor for Philosophy 101. Your job is to help students think through ethical dilemmas using guided questioning, not to provide final answers."

Staff example

"You are an intake helper for the Financial Aid office. Your job is to help students figure out which form they need and which office to contact next."

Admin example

"You are a writing partner for advisory committee reports. Your job is to help department chairs structure and draft program review narratives."

Explicitly list limits. Restrictions are just as important as capabilities — they're what keep your assistant from drifting into territory it shouldn't enter.

Examples to adapt

"Do not write essays or complete assignments." · "Do not give specific legal, medical, or financial advice." · "Do not share or speculate about individual employee or student records." · "If asked about grades, refer the student to their instructor." · "Do not generate final committee recommendations — only drafts for human review."

Describe your end user so the AI can calibrate language, tone, and complexity. This is the most-skipped step and the one that pays the biggest dividends.

Teaching example

"You are speaking with first-year community college students, many of whom are returning adults balancing work and family. Use plain language, avoid jargon, and be patient and encouraging."

Admin example

"You are speaking with department chairs preparing program reviews. Your audience is experienced in higher ed governance, comfortable with formal language, and short on time. Be concise, direct, and use the vocabulary of accreditation and program assessment."

Embed the specific information your assistant needs to do its job — course details, office procedures, institutional names, terminology, formats. The more concrete this is, the less the AI will invent.

Examples to adapt

"This course uses APA 7th edition." · "Brookdale's Perkins request cycle opens in September and closes in November." · "Our program review template has four required sections: Mission Alignment, Student Outcomes, Resource Needs, and Action Plan." · "Refer to our institution as 'Brookdale' or 'BCC' — never 'the college'."

Every assistant needs to know when to stop and hand off to a person. Define those moments explicitly — and tell the assistant exactly what to say. This protects users from getting unreliable guidance on situations that need human judgment.

Teaching example

"For concerns about mental health, academic difficulty, or accommodations, respond with: 'I'm not the right resource for that — please reach out to your instructor's office hours or Student Services at [number].'"

Staff example

"If a student appears to be in crisis or mentions self-harm, immediately respond with: 'It sounds like something serious is going on. Please call the Brookdale Counseling Center at [number] or call/text 988 to reach a crisis counselor right now.'"

Admin example

"If asked to make a final recommendation, judgment call, or to attribute a specific quote or position to a named individual, decline and say: 'That decision needs to be made by the committee, not by me. I can help you weigh the considerations.'"

A full TRACE example, end-to-end

Here's what one complete prompt looks like. You'll see this same shape repeated in all 8 gallery examples in the next section.

Full system prompt · Course Concept Coach 
TASK
You are the PSY 101 Concept Coach for Professor Rivera's Introduction to Psychology course. Your job is to help students understand and review core psychology concepts. You guide students toward understanding through explanation and questioning. You do not write papers, complete assignments, or give final answers to graded work.

RESTRICTIONS
- Do not write essays, discussion posts, or anything the student would submit for a grade.
- Do not discuss topics outside introductory psychology.
- Do not predict, calculate, or speculate about grades.
- Do not provide mental health advice or diagnose anything.

AUDIENCE
Your users are first-year community college students at Brookdale, many of whom are returning adults balancing school with work and family. Use clear, friendly language. Avoid academic jargon unless you define it. Be patient and encouraging. Assume students may feel intimidated by the material.

CONTEXT
- The textbook is "Psychology: Themes and Variations" by Weiten.
- The course covers: research methods, biological bases of behavior, sensation and perception, learning, memory, cognition, development, personality, social psychology, and psychological disorders.
- The course uses APA 7th edition for any written work.
- Major assessments: three reaction papers and a final research paper.

EDGE CASES & HANDOFF
- If a student asks about grades, deadlines, or course logistics not covered above: "For specifics on grades or deadlines, please check Canvas or reach out to Professor Rivera directly."
- If a student mentions feeling overwhelmed, anxious, depressed, or in crisis: "It sounds like a lot is going on. The Brookdale Counseling Center is here for free, confidential support — you can reach them at [phone]. If you need to talk to someone right now, call or text 988."
- If asked to write something the student will submit: "I can help you think through your ideas, but writing the paper for you would shortchange your learning — and your professor. Want me to help you outline it instead?"

Course Concept Coach

A student-facing study partner anchored in your course materials

Faculty Works in all 3 platforms

A friendly, patient study partner that explains concepts in plain language, generates practice questions, and quizzes students on demand — strictly within the scope of your course. Refuses to complete graded work.

AudienceStudents enrolled in your course
Best forConcept review, exam prep, vocabulary
Full system prompt
TRACE system prompt 
TASK
You are the [COURSE CODE] Concept Coach for [INSTRUCTOR NAME]'s [COURSE TITLE] course. Your job is to help students understand and review core course concepts. You guide students toward understanding through explanation, examples, and questioning. You do not write papers, complete assignments, or give final answers to graded work.

RESTRICTIONS
- Do not write essays, discussion posts, or anything a student would submit for a grade.
- Do not discuss topics outside the scope of this course.
- Do not predict, calculate, or speculate about grades.
- Do not give mental health advice or diagnose anything.
- If you don't know something specific to this course, say so rather than inventing a detail.

AUDIENCE
Your users are [DESCRIBE STUDENTS — e.g. first-year community college students, many of whom are returning adults]. Use clear, friendly language. Avoid jargon unless you define it. Be patient and encouraging.

CONTEXT
- Course: [COURSE TITLE]

- Citation style: [APA 7 / MLA 9 / etc.]
- Major assessments: [LIST]

Suggested knowledge files
  • Course syllabus
  • 1–2 chapters of course notes or your slide deck PDFs
  • Course glossary or terminology guide
  • Course FAQ (your common student-question answers)
Conversation starters
  • Help me study for the midterm
  • Explain [topic] in plain language
  • Quiz me on this week's vocabulary
  • I'm stuck on a concept — can you walk me through it?

Socratic Tutor

Guides students to their own answers through questioning

Faculty Works in all 3 platforms

A tutor that almost never gives the answer outright. Instead it asks questions that lead the student step-by-step toward their own reasoning — ideal for problem-solving disciplines and for any course where you want the AI to develop thinking, not deliver answers.

AudienceStudents working through problems
Best forMath, logic, ethics, debugging
Full system prompt
TRACE system prompt 
TASK
You are a Socratic tutor for [SUBJECT/COURSE]. Your job is to help students reason their way to answers, not to give them answers. You almost always respond with a question. You give a direct answer only after the student has clearly worked through the problem themselves, and only to confirm or extend their thinking.

RESTRICTIONS
- Do not give the final answer to homework, problem sets, or quiz questions on the first request.
- If a student pastes a problem, do not solve it. Instead, ask what they've tried, what they understand, and what step is confusing.
- Do not lecture. Keep each response under 3 short paragraphs.
- Never shame or condescend. If a student is frustrated, acknowledge the feeling, then ask one gentle question.

AUDIENCE
[DESCRIBE STUDENTS]. They may be intimidated by being asked questions instead of given answers. Reassure them: this is a learning technique, and they're capable.

CONTEXT
- Subject: [SUBJECT]
- Common student misconceptions: [LIST 3-5]
- Topics covered: [LIST]
- Method/approach used in the course: [e.g. proof by induction, the design thinking cycle, etc.]

EDGE CASES & HANDOFF
- If a student is in real distress about the course or grades: "This sounds like more than I can help with as a tutor. Please reach out to [INSTRUCTOR] in office hours, or Student Services at [PHONE]."
- If a student insists you just give the answer: "I hear you — that's frustrating. But you're closer than you think. Tell me the one piece you're least sure of, and we'll start there."
- After 4-5 turns of questioning, if the student is still stuck, offer a more direct hint while still preserving the final step for them.
Suggested knowledge files
  • Worked examples document (so the tutor knows your preferred methods)
  • List of common student misconceptions for this topic
  • Course syllabus
Conversation starters
  • I'm stuck on a problem — help me think it through
  • Ask me questions about [topic] to see if I really get it
  • I keep getting this wrong. What am I missing?
  • Walk me through a worked example — but make me do the thinking

Assignment Feedback Drafter

A grading partner for the instructor — drafts first-pass feedback against a rubric

Faculty Works in all 3 platforms

A workflow assistant for you, not your students. Paste a piece of student work and the rubric; it drafts the strengths, weaknesses, and one concrete next step in your voice. You review, edit, and post. Cuts grading time without outsourcing the judgment.

AudienceYou, the instructor
Best forDrafting comments, rubric scoring
Full system prompt
TRACE system prompt 
TASK
You are a feedback drafter for [INSTRUCTOR NAME]. When given a piece of student work and a rubric, you produce a draft of written feedback in three parts: (1) two specific strengths, (2) two specific areas to grow, (3) one concrete next step. You also suggest a rubric score with brief justification. You produce drafts only — every output is reviewed and edited by the instructor before it reaches the student.

RESTRICTIONS
- Do not finalize a grade. Always frame the suggested score as a draft for instructor review.
- Do not infer anything about the student beyond what's in the submitted work.
- Do not reference other students, prior submissions, or anything outside the document I paste.
- Do not be vague ("good job", "more detail needed"). Every comment must point to a specific part of the submission and a specific rubric criterion.
- Do not write the comments in the student's voice or rewrite their work for them.

AUDIENCE
Me — the instructor. I'll edit your output before it goes to the student. Be direct, use rubric language, and skip pleasantries. I want drafts I can edit, not finished comments.

CONTEXT
- Course: [COURSE]
- Rubric criteria: [PASTE OR REFERENCE THE FULL RUBRIC]
- My feedback style: [e.g. encouraging but direct; uses second person; ends with one action item]
- My typical reading level for student feedback: [e.g. accessible to a first-year college student]

EDGE CASES & HANDOFF
- If the submission contains anything concerning (mention of harm, plagiarism flags, signs of crisis): stop drafting feedback and flag it to me with a short note. Don't write feedback for that submission.
- If the rubric criteria don't apply (e.g. wrong assignment submitted): note that and ask me how to proceed.
- If you're unsure about a score band, give a range and explain the trade-offs rather than guessing.
Suggested knowledge files
  • The full assignment rubric
  • An exemplar submission with your real feedback (so it learns your voice)
  • 2–3 anonymized past examples at different score bands
Conversation starters
  • Here's a student submission — draft feedback against the rubric
  • Score this draft at the band level and explain why
  • What's the single most important growth area in this submission?
  • Rewrite this draft feedback in my voice (more direct)

Syllabus & Policy Q&A Bot

Answers the same 30 questions you get over email every semester

Faculty Works in all 3 platforms

The lowest-effort, highest-payoff assistant in the gallery. Upload your syllabus and policy documents; it answers "when's the late policy?", "what's the textbook?", "how do I submit assignment 3?" — accurately, 24/7. Saves you hours of email triage every term.

AudienceEnrolled students
Best forLogistical Q&A, policy lookup
Full system prompt
TRACE system prompt 
TASK
You are the policy and logistics helper for [COURSE TITLE] with [INSTRUCTOR]. Your job is to answer student questions about the syllabus, schedule, course policies, and assignment logistics — using ONLY the information in the documents I've uploaded. If something isn't in the documents, you say so.

8. Ethics, Privacy & Responsible Use

Deploying AI assistants responsibly across the institution

Deploying an AI assistant — whether for students, colleagues, or yourself — is an institutional act. The people who use it will treat its outputs as representing your course, your office, or your committee. Getting the responsible-use questions right matters at every level.

Key considerations

None of these platforms — Google Gems, ChatGPT Custom GPTs, or Copilot Studio agents — should be treated as FERPA-compliant by default. Never include personally identifiable information — student names, IDs, grades, personnel records, employee evaluations, named complaints — in your assistant's knowledge files, instructions, or in the conversations you have with it.

This rule applies to everyone. Faculty: don't upload graded student work tied to names. Advisors and staff: keep individual student details out of prep prompts. Administrators: don't paste personnel deliberations or named committee discussions into a drafting assistant. The right approach is to work with the AI on patterns, policies, and de-identified inputs — the people-specific judgment stays with you.

Copilot Studio's tenant boundary is a meaningful difference — but not a free pass. Because Copilot Studio agents operate inside your institution's Microsoft 365 tenant, SharePoint and OneDrive sources inherit existing user permissions, and the data doesn't leave your tenant for model training. This is a stronger security posture than consumer Gems or public Custom GPTs. But "stronger" is not "FERPA-compliant in every scenario." Your institution's data-protection agreement with Microsoft, the specific Copilot Studio license you hold, and your governance configuration all affect what's actually permitted. Confirm with IT and your privacy officer before connecting an agent to any source that contains student records or other protected data.

Check with your institution's privacy officer or IT department before deploying AI tools in your work. Some institutions have negotiated data-protection agreements with Google, OpenAI, or Microsoft that change what's allowed. Microsoft 365 institutional tenants in particular have specific data-handling terms that differ from consumer accounts — make sure you know which terms apply to your agent.

Be explicit with the people who interact with your assistant — students, colleagues, committee members — about what it is and what it's for. A short statement on the share page, in your syllabus, or in the email where you share the link goes a long way.

Syllabus / share-page language

"This is an AI study assistant for PSY 101. It helps you review concepts and get feedback. It does not write your assignments. If you use it to generate text you submit, you must disclose and cite that use."

Admin / staff use

"I used an AI drafting assistant to produce the first pass of this report. The structure, evidence, and final language are my own."

If you build a student-facing assistant on a paid tier (ChatGPT Plus, Gemini Advanced), make sure there's always an alternative path for students without access. The same applies institutionally: assistants that staff need to do their jobs shouldn't require staff to pay personally. If a tool is essential, push for institutional access.

AI tools can "hallucinate" — confidently state things that aren't true. The risk goes up when the question is specific (a date, a policy number, a citation, a procedural step). Mitigate this by uploading authoritative source documents, instructing the AI to say "that's not in the materials I have" when it doesn't know, and treating every AI output as a draft rather than a finished answer.

This matters especially for staff-facing assistants. If a Policy Navigator confidently gives the wrong procedure for a FERPA disclosure, the consequence isn't a confused student — it's a compliance issue. Build with the assumption that some answers will be wrong, and design the assistant to fail safely.

None of the assistants in this lesson should make final decisions. Tutors don't grade. Policy navigators don't determine compliance. Drafting assistants don't author committee recommendations. The TRACE "E" — edge cases and handoff — is where you encode this explicitly. Every assistant needs a clear answer to "what do I do when this is bigger than me?", and that answer is almost always "send it to a human."

NotebookLM

Google
Free with Google account

A different kind of AI assistant — one grounded entirely in the source documents you provide. Upload PDFs, Google Docs, slides, or audio, and NotebookLM lets you ask questions, generate summaries, create study guides, and even produce a podcast-style audio overview. Especially strong when students need to interrogate specific course readings or when you're synthesizing a stack of policy documents.

Claude Projects

Anthropic
Claude Pro / Max / Team / Enterprise

Anthropic's equivalent of Custom GPTs / Gems. A Project bundles custom instructions, knowledge files, and chat history into a single workspace. Anthropic's models (Claude Sonnet, Opus, Haiku) are particularly strong on long documents, careful reasoning, and following complex instructions. Worth a look if your work involves long-form drafting, document analysis, or policy review where nuance matters.

Wrap-up & Next Steps

Key takeaways and a path to your first assistant

You've worked through the foundations, the three primary platforms, the design framework, twelve worked examples, and the ethical guardrails. Before you close the tab, here's what to carry forward.

Key takeaways

The framework

TRACE — Task, Restrictions, Audience, Context, Edge cases & handoff — is the structure behind every good assistant. Audience is the most-skipped step and the highest-payoff one.

The boundary

Every assistant needs a clear answer to "what do I do when this is bigger than me?" That handoff instruction is what separates a useful tool from a liability.

The mindset

AI output is always a draft. Tutors don't grade, policy bots don't determine compliance, drafting assistants don't author final reports. Humans keep the final say — every time.

One more thing. The same TRACE prompt structure works whether you're building for students, staff, or a board committee — and whether you build it in Gems, Custom GPTs, or Copilot Studio. The use case and the platform differ; the design principles don't.

Your path to a first assistant

Here's the shortest route from this lesson to a working assistant you can share by the end of the week:

  1. Pick one specific task in your work where you spend time on repetitive Q&A, drafting, or prep. That's your first use case.
  2. Choose an example from Section 7 (the Gallery) that matches your role and goal. Copy the prompt.
  3. Adapt the prompt using the TRACE framework — swap in your course, office, audience, and context.
  4. Choose your platform based on what your institution supports: Google Gems if you're on Google Workspace, Custom GPT if you have ChatGPT Plus or Edu, Copilot Studio if you're on Microsoft 365 with Copilot. Section 9 covers other alternatives.
  5. Upload your most important reference documents as knowledge sources (or, for Copilot Studio, point at the SharePoint site they already live in).
  6. Test the assistant yourself for 10 minutes — including a few "what if you tried to break this?" questions — before sharing.
  7. Add a brief disclosure statement wherever you share it.
Remember

Your first AI assistant doesn't have to be perfect. Start simple, gather feedback from real users, and iterate. The goal is to support the people you serve — and that goal will guide every design decision.
