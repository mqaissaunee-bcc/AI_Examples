📊 Understanding Sensitive Information Types

Sensitive information is any data that, if disclosed, could harm individuals, violate privacy laws, or damage institutional interests.

🆔 Personally Identifiable Information (PII)Any information that can be used to identify, contact, or locate a specific individual, either alone or combined with other data

Any information that can identify an individual, directly or indirectly

Higher Education Examples:
  • Student names, ID numbers, grades, transcripts
  • Employee Social Security numbers, addresses
  • Personal email addresses, phone numbers
  • Driver's license or passport information

🏥 Personal Health Information (PHI)Medical records and health-related data protected under HIPAA, including mental health, disability, and medical treatment information

Medical records and health-related data protected under HIPAAHealth Insurance Portability and Accountability Act - federal law protecting medical information privacy

Higher Education Examples:
  • Student health records and accommodations
  • Counseling and disability documentation
  • Employee medical leave requests
  • Research participant health data

💰 Financial Information

Data related to financial status and transactions

Higher Education Examples:
  • Student financial aid information
  • Institutional budgets and contracts
  • Credit card and bank account details
  • Donation and payment records

🎓 Academic & Research Confidentiality

Unpublished research and proprietary academic information

Higher Education Examples:
  • Unpublished research and grant proposals
  • Patent applications and trade secrets
  • Peer review comments and evaluations
  • Admission decisions and assessments

⚠️ Understanding AI Data Risks

Critical Understanding: Many AI platforms collect data from user interactions and may use this information to train their models, potentially sharing it with third parties.

🔍 How AI Tools Handle Your Data

When you interact with AI tools, several concerning things happen behind the scenes:

1. Permanent Data Integration

Once personal or sensitive data is entered into an AI model's training dataset, there is no practical mechanism to delete it. Information becomes embedded in the model permanently.

2. Uncontrolled Data Sharing

Many platforms share user data with unknown third parties, leaving users with little control. Data may be used for advertising, research, or other purposes without your knowledge.

3. Potential Data Breaches

AI platforms are vulnerable to security breaches that could expose stored conversations and sensitive information to unauthorized parties.

4. Model Training and Reuse

Contact details or proprietary information may become embedded in the model's training data and potentially surface in interactions with other users.

  • Reputation Damage: Loss of trust from students, faculty, and community
  • Financial Penalties: Fines and legal costs from regulatory violations

✅ Safe AI Usage Best Practices

🏆 The Golden Rule

Never share sensitive information with public AI tools. Treat AI chats like public forums – never share confidential or personally identifiable information.

✅ Safe Information to Share

  • General questions about topics or concepts
  • Public information already available online
  • Hypothetical scenarios (without real names)
  • General research questions without specific data

❌ NEVER Share

  • Student names, IDs, or academic records
  • Personal contact information
  • Health information or accommodations
  • Financial data or payment information
  • Confidential research data
  • Internal institutional information

🎯 Practical Scenarios - Test Your Knowledge

Scenario 1: Course Planning

Professor Maria Chen is redesigning her Introduction to Psychology course for next semester. She's teaching 180 students and wants to make the lectures more interactive. After attending a workshop on AI tools for educators, she's excited to try using ChatGPT to generate ideas. She has her course roster open on one screen with all 180 student names, email addresses, and demographic information from their registration forms. On the other screen, she's drafting a message to ChatGPT.

Which approach should Maria take?

  • Ask: "Suggest interactive activities for a large lecture course with 180 students on introductory psychology topics"
  • Share her class roster and ask: "Based on these student backgrounds and majors, suggest optimal group formations for collaborative projects"
  • Upload end-of-semester evaluations from last year and ask: "Review these student comments and suggest specific improvements to my teaching"
  • Paste student essay submissions with names and ask: "Help me grade these essays and identify which students need additional support"

Scenario 2: Research Assistance

Dr. James Rodriguez is in the final stages of analyzing data for his study on student wellness. He's collected survey responses from 500 students, including questions about mental health, stress levels, and coping strategies. The Excel file contains columns with student ID numbers, email addresses, age, gender, and their detailed responses about anxiety and depression symptoms. It's 11 PM, he's exhausted, and he's struggling to decide which statistical test to use for his analysis. He remembers that AI tools can help with research methodology questions. His hand hovers over the "upload file" button in ChatGPT.

What should Dr. Rodriguez do?

  • Upload the complete data file and ask: "Analyze this survey data and recommend the best statistical approach for identifying patterns"
  • Close the data file and ask: "I have survey data with ordinal scales measuring stress levels across different student demographics. What statistical methods would be appropriate for analyzing group differences?"
  • Upload his IRB application with participant information and ask: "Review this research protocol and help me improve the methodology section"
  • Share the raw survey responses and ask: "Help me interpret these mental health questionnaire results and identify students who may be at risk"

Scenario 3: Administrative Tasks

Sarah Thompson, Director of Operations, has been asked by the Dean to develop a comprehensive remote work policy for staff. She has mountains of work piling up: annual performance reviews for her 12 direct reports (including detailed notes about each person's strengths and areas for improvement), salary data showing what each department pays employees in similar roles, and budget spreadsheets with departmental spending. She's heard that AI can help draft policies quickly. She opens Claude.ai, ready to get this task done efficiently before her 2 PM meeting. She looks at all the sensitive documents spread across her desk.

How should Sarah proceed to get help with creating the remote work policy?

  • Upload John Doe's performance evaluation as an example and ask: "Based on this employee's work patterns, help me draft remote work guidelines that address productivity concerns"
  • Share the salary spreadsheet and ask: "Analyze these compensation levels by department and help me determine if remote work should affect pay structures"
  • Put away the sensitive documents and ask: "Draft a general remote work policy template for higher education staff that addresses productivity expectations, communication protocols, and equipment needs"
  • Upload redacted financial aid applications showing student work-study positions and ask: "Review these to understand our student worker needs for a remote work policy"

🛡️ Key Best Practices Summary

  1. Data AnonymizationProcess of removing all identifying information from data before analysis or sharing: Remove all identifiers before using AI for analysis
  2. Use Approved Tools: Check with the IT Helpdesk about institutionally approved AI platforms
  3. Verify Output: Always fact-check AI-generated content
  4. Clear Chat History: Regularly clear conversations and use privacy settings

🏛️ Institutional Policies and Resources

📋 Understanding Your Institution's AI Policy

Most institutions classify data into different levels with specific rules for each. It's crucial to understand these classifications and how they apply to AI usage.

🟢 Safe for AI Use

Public Data

  • Published research papers
  • Course catalogs and schedules
  • Public event information
  • General institutional facts

🟡 Use with Caution

Internal Data - Approved Tools Only

  • Internal memos (non-sensitive)
  • Meeting agendas
  • General operational data
  • Anonymous survey results

🔴 Never Use with Public AI

Confidential & Restricted Data

  • Student records (PIIPersonally Identifiable Information - data that can identify specific individuals)
  • Employee information
  • Financial data and payment information
  • Research participant data
  • PHIPersonal Health Information - medical and health-related data protected under HIPAA and medical records
  • Social Security numbers
  • Financial account information
  • Classified research data

🚨 When in Doubt

  • Ask before sharing: Contact your supervisor, the IT Helpdesk, or the Information Security Committee
  • Report incidents: Immediately report suspected data incidents to the IT Helpdesk
  • Stay informed: Keep up with Brookdale's data governance policies and updates
  • Remember: It's better to be cautious than sorry
