Introduction to Cryptography

What is Cryptography?

Cryptography is the science of protecting information by transforming it into a secure format. It's a fundamental pillar of information security that enables:

  • Confidentiality: Keeping information secret from unauthorized parties
  • Integrity: Detecting unauthorized modifications to data
  • Authentication: Verifying the identity of users and systems
  • Non-repudiation: Preventing denial of actions or communications

Core Terminology

Plaintext: The original, readable message or data.

Ciphertext: The encrypted, unreadable output after applying encryption.

Encryption: Converting plaintext into ciphertext using an algorithm and key.

Decryption: Converting ciphertext back to plaintext using the appropriate key.

Cryptographic Algorithm: The mathematical procedure for encryption/decryption.

Key: The secret value that controls the cryptographic algorithm.

Why Cryptography Matters for Ethical Hackers

As ethical hackers, understanding cryptography is essential for:

  • Testing security implementations in target systems
  • Identifying weak cryptographic configurations
  • Understanding how attackers compromise encrypted data
  • Recommending proper security controls
  • Complying with security standards (PCI DSS, HIPAA, GDPR)

‚ö†Ô∏è Ethical and Legal Guidelines

Cryptographic knowledge is powerful and must be used responsibly:

  • Always obtain written authorization before testing cryptographic systems
  • Follow all applicable laws regarding encryption export and use
  • Respect privacy and data protection regulations
  • Never use password cracking tools without explicit permission
  • Maintain confidentiality of discovered vulnerabilities

Modern Applications of Cryptography

  • Web Security: HTTPS/TLS protecting billions of web transactions daily
  • Financial Systems: Banking, credit cards, cryptocurrency

  • IoT: Securing Internet of Things devices

History of Cryptography

Cryptography has been used for thousands of years to protect sensitive information. From ancient military communications to modern digital security, the need to keep secrets has driven continuous innovation.

1900 BCE - Ancient Egypt

Egyptian hieroglyphics used non-standard symbols as one of the earliest forms of cryptography.

600-500 BCE - Hebrew Ciphers

The Atbash cipher was used in Hebrew texts, including parts of the Book of Jeremiah - a simple substitution cipher where letters are replaced systematically.

100-44 BCE - Caesar Cipher

Julius Caesar used a substitution cipher to protect military messages. Each letter was shifted three positions (A‚ÜíD, B‚ÜíE, C‚ÜíF, etc.). This became known as the Caesar cipher and is still taught today as an introduction to cryptography.

1917 - The Zimmermann Telegram

British cryptanalysts decoded a German diplomatic telegram, helping bring the United States into World War I. This demonstrated the strategic importance of cryptanalysis.

1918-1945 - The Enigma Machine

Developed by Arthur Scherbius, the Enigma machine was Germany's primary encryption device in WWII. Polish cryptographers first broke it, followed by British and American efforts at Bletchley Park using the Bombe machine. This breakthrough is estimated to have shortened the war by 2-4 years.

1930s-1945 - The Purple Machine

Japan's encryption machine during WWII. William Frederick Friedman, the "Father of U.S. Cryptanalysis," successfully broke Purple codes, providing crucial intelligence to Allied forces.

1976 - Diffie-Hellman Key Exchange

Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman published the first public-key cryptography method, revolutionizing how keys could be exchanged securely over insecure channels.

1977 - RSA Algorithm

Ron Rivest, Adi Shamir, and Leonard Adleman invented RSA, the first practical public-key cryptosystem for both encryption and digital signatures.

1977 - Data Encryption Standard (DES)

NIST adopted DES as the first federal encryption standard, bringing cryptography into the commercial mainstream.

1991 - Pretty Good Privacy (PGP)

Phil Zimmermann released PGP, making strong encryption available to everyday users. He faced legal challenges as strong encryption was considered a weapon at the time.

2001 - Advanced Encryption Standard (AES)

NIST selected Rijndael as the new encryption standard, replacing DES. AES remains the gold standard for symmetric encryption today.

2008 - Bitcoin

Satoshi Nakamoto's cryptocurrency demonstrated practical applications of cryptographic hash functions, digital signatures, and distributed consensus.

2010s-Present - Post-Quantum Cryptography

Research focuses on developing algorithms resistant to quantum computer attacks, as quantum computing threatens to break many current cryptographic methods.

Cryptanalysis: Breaking Codes

Cryptanalysis is the other side of cryptography - the art and science of breaking encryption. Every new encryption algorithm must undergo rigorous testing to ensure it's secure.

An algorithm is considered weak when breaking it becomes practical with reasonable computing power. The goal is to create algorithms where breaking them is either mathematically impossible or would require more time and resources than the protected information is worth.

Steganography: Hiding in Plain Sight

Steganography is different from cryptography. Instead of making data unreadable, it hides the existence of the data itself.

Examples:

  • Hiding messages in the least significant bits of image pixels
  • Embedding data in audio files
  • Invisible ink (historical example)
  • Hiding messages in video frames

Modern steganography combined with encryption provides defense in depth - even if the hidden data is found, it's still encrypted.

Cryptography Fundamentals

The Cryptosystem

A cryptosystem consists of:

  • Algorithm: The mathematical function performing encryption/decryption
  • Key: The secret parameter controlling the algorithm
  • Keyspace: The set of all possible keys
  • Protocol: Rules for using the system securely

Understanding Keys and Keyspace

A key is generated from a keyspace. Security depends on:

  • Key Length: Measured in bits (128-bit, 256-bit, etc.)
  • Keyspace Size: 2n possible keys (n = key length)
  • Randomness: Keys must be unpredictable
  • Protection: Keys must be kept secret

Example: A 128-bit key has 2128 = 340,282,366,920,938,463,463,374,607,431,768,211,456 possible combinations!

‚ö†Ô∏è Kerckhoffs's Principle

"A cryptosystem should be secure even if everything about the system, except the key, is public knowledge."

This means:

  • Security depends on the key, NOT the algorithm's secrecy
  • Algorithms should be publicly scrutinized
  • "Security through obscurity" is not real security
  • Open algorithms receive more testing and are generally more trusted

Three Main Types of Cryptography

Symmetric

Keys: One shared key

Speed: Very fast

Key Exchange: Difficult

Authentication: No

Use Case: Data encryption

Examples: AES, DES, 3DES

Asymmetric

Keys: Public/private pair

Speed: Slow (1000x slower)

Key Exchange: Easy

Authentication: Yes

Use Case: Key exchange, signatures

Examples: RSA, ECC

Hashing

Keys: None required

Speed: Very fast

Key Exchange: N/A

Authentication: Indirectly

Use Case: Integrity verification

Examples: SHA-256, BLAKE2

Hybrid Cryptography

Modern systems use hybrid cryptography combining both types:

  1. Asymmetric crypto securely exchanges a symmetric key
  2. Symmetric crypto encrypts the actual data (faster)
  3. Digital signatures provide authentication

Real-World Example: When you visit an HTTPS website, your browser uses RSA or ECDHE to establish a secure channel, then switches to AES for encrypting the actual web traffic.

Symmetric Cryptography

Overview

Symmetric encryption uses the same key for both encryption and decryption. Both parties must securely share this secret key before communication.

Advantages:

  • Much faster than asymmetric (1000x+)
  • Efficient for large data volumes
  • Simple concept - one key does everything
  • Lower computational requirements

Disadvantages:

  • Key distribution problem (how to share securely?)
  • Poor scalability (many keys needed)
  • No authentication or non-repudiation
  • If key compromised, all data at risk

Stream vs Block Ciphers

Stream Ciphers

Encrypt data one bit or byte at a time:

  • Generate keystream from key
  • XOR keystream with plaintext
  • Fast and low error propagation
  • Examples: RC4, ChaCha20, Salsa20

Block Ciphers

Encrypt fixed-size blocks (typically 128 bits):

  • Process data in chunks
  • Use substitution and permutation
  • Require padding for incomplete blocks
  • Examples: AES, DES, 3DES, Blowfish

Major Symmetric Algorithms

Data Encryption Standard (DES)

Developed: 1970s by IBM, standardized by NIST in 1977

Specifications: 64-bit blocks, 56-bit key, 16 rounds

Status: ‚ùå BROKEN - Do not use

DES was broken in 1998 when a specialized computer cracked it in just 3 days. The 56-bit key is too short for modern threats.

Triple DES (3DES)

Purpose: Quick fix for DES by applying it three times

Specifications: 112 or 168-bit effective key strength

Process: Encrypt-Decrypt-Encrypt with different keys

Status: ‚ö†Ô∏è Legacy - Being phased out

While more secure than DES, 3DES is slow and being replaced by AES.

Advanced Encryption Standard (AES)

Selected: 2001 after NIST competition (winner: Rijndael)

Specifications:

  • Block size: 128 bits
  • Key sizes: 128, 192, or 256 bits
  • Rounds: 10, 12, or 14 (based on key size)

Status: ‚úÖ Current Standard - Widely Used

Applications: HTTPS, VPNs, file encryption, WiFi (WPA2/WPA3), government classified data

AES is the gold standard for symmetric encryption. Even the NSA approves AES-256 for TOP SECRET information.

Blowfish & Twofish

Blowfish: Created by Bruce Schneier in 1993

  • 64-bit blocks, 32-448 bit keys
  • Public domain
  • Fast and flexible

Twofish: AES finalist, successor to Blowfish

  • 128-bit blocks, up to 256-bit keys
  • Very secure design

Status: ‚úÖ Both secure and usable

ChaCha20

Developer: Daniel J. Bernstein

Type: Stream cipher

Advantages:

  • Faster than AES on devices without hardware acceleration
  • Resistant to timing attacks
  • Used in TLS, SSH, VPNs

Status: ‚úÖ Modern choice for software-based encryption

RC4

Developer: Ronald Rivest (1987)

Historical Use: Was the most common stream cipher (WEP, old SSL)

Status: ‚ùå BROKEN - Never use

Multiple serious vulnerabilities discovered. Removed from all modern protocols.

Block Cipher Modes

Block ciphers can operate in different modes with different security properties:

Mode Description Security
ECB Each block encrypted independently ‚ùå Insecure - reveals patterns
CBC XOR with previous ciphertext block ‚úÖ Good (needs unique IV)
CTR Counter mode - parallel processing ‚úÖ Good, fast
GCM CTR with authentication ‚úÖ Excellent - recommended

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Implementation Mistakes

  • Using ECB mode: Never use - it reveals patterns in data
  • Reusing IVs: Initialization vectors must be unique
  • Weak keys: Use cryptographically secure random number generators
  • No authentication: Use authenticated encryption (GCM)
  • Hardcoded keys: Never embed keys in source code

Asymmetric Cryptography

The Revolutionary Concept

Public key cryptography uses two mathematically related keys:

  • Public Key: Can be shared freely with anyone
  • Private Key: Must be kept secret by owner

Key Property: Data encrypted with one key can only be decrypted with the other key.

Two Primary Use Cases

1. Confidentiality (Encryption)

Alice wants to send a secret to Bob:

  1. Alice encrypts with Bob's public key
  2. Only Bob can decrypt (with his private key)
  3. Even Alice cannot decrypt it

2. Authentication (Digital Signatures)

Alice wants to prove she sent a message:

  1. Alice "signs" (encrypts hash) with her private key
  2. Anyone can verify with Alice's public key
  3. Proves Alice sent it (only she has private key)
  4. Alice cannot deny sending it (non-repudiation)

Major Asymmetric Algorithms

RSA (Rivest-Shamir-Adleman)

Published: 1977 by Ron Rivest, Adi Shamir, and Leonard Adleman

Mathematical Basis: Difficulty of factoring large prime numbers

Key Sizes: 2048 bits (minimum), 3072-4096 bits (recommended)

Status: ‚úÖ Still secure with adequate key sizes

Applications: TLS/SSL, email encryption, digital signatures, code signing

Note: 1024-bit keys are no longer secure!

Diffie-Hellman Key Exchange

Developers: Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman (1976)

Purpose: Securely exchange symmetric keys over insecure channel

Mathematical Basis: Discrete logarithm problem

Important: Does NOT encrypt data itself - only exchanges keys

Vulnerability: Susceptible to man-in-the-middle without authentication

Modern Variants: DHE (Ephemeral), ECDHE (Elliptic Curve)

Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC)

Introduction: 1985, popularized in 2000s

Key Advantage: Much smaller keys for equivalent security

Key Size Comparison:

  • 256-bit ECC ‚âà 3072-bit RSA
  • 384-bit ECC ‚âà 7680-bit RSA

Benefits:

  • Faster computations
  • Less memory/bandwidth
  • Perfect for mobile/IoT

Status: ‚úÖ Increasingly preferred for new implementations

Used In: Bitcoin, TLS, SSH, modern certificates

ElGamal

Developer: Taher Elgamal (1985)

Capabilities: Encryption and digital signatures

Mathematical Basis: Discrete logarithm problem

Characteristic: Produces ciphertext 2x size of plaintext

Used In: PGP/GPG (variant)

Why Asymmetric is Slower

Asymmetric algorithms involve complex mathematical operations:

  • Large number exponentiation
  • Modular arithmetic
  • Prime number operations

This makes them approximately 1000x slower than symmetric algorithms, which is why hybrid systems are used.

‚ö†Ô∏è Post-Quantum Threat

Current asymmetric algorithms (RSA, ECC, Diffie-Hellman) will be vulnerable to quantum computers.

Timeline: Possibly 10-30 years

Solution: NIST is standardizing post-quantum algorithms:

  • CRYSTALS-Kyber (key exchange)
  • CRYSTALS-Dilithium (signatures)
  • FALCON (signatures)
  • SPHINCS+ (signatures)

Hashing Algorithms

What is Hashing?

Hashing is fundamentally different from encryption:

  • Takes any size input ‚Üí Fixed size output
  • One-way function (cannot be reversed)
  • Deterministic (same input = same output)
  • No keys required

Think of it like a fingerprint: Identifies uniquely, but you can't reconstruct the person from just the fingerprint.

Purpose of Hashing

  • Data Integrity: Detect modifications
  • Password Storage: Never store passwords directly!
  • Digital Signatures: Sign hashes, not entire documents
  • File Verification: Verify downloads
  • Blockchain: Link blocks immutably
  • Checksums: Quick integrity checks

Properties of Good Hash Functions

  • Deterministic: Same input always gives same output
  • Fast: Quick to compute
  • Pre-image Resistant: Can't find input from hash
  • Avalanche Effect: Small change = completely different hash
  • Collision Resistant: Hard to find two inputs with same hash

Understanding Collisions

A collision occurs when two different messages produce the same hash value.

Why it matters: If an attacker finds a collision, they can substitute one message for another while maintaining the same hash - defeating integrity checks and digital signatures.

Major Hashing Algorithms

MD5 (Message Digest 5)

Developer: Ronald Rivest (1991)

Output: 128 bits (32 hex characters)

Status: ‚ùå BROKEN - Never use for security

Collisions can be found in seconds. Only acceptable for non-security purposes like database keys.

SHA-1 (Secure Hash Algorithm 1)

Designed: 1995 by NSA

Output: 160 bits (40 hex characters)

Status: ‚ùå BROKEN since 2005

Google achieved practical collision in 2017. Browsers no longer trust SHA-1 certificates.

SHA-2 Family

Variants: SHA-224, SHA-256, SHA-384, SHA-512

Most Common: SHA-256 (256 bits / 64 hex characters)

Status: ‚úÖ Current Standard

Applications: TLS/SSL, Bitcoin, digital signatures, certificates

No known practical attacks. SHA-256 is the current industry standard.

SHA-3 (Keccak)

Selected: 2015 by NIST after competition

Design: Completely different from SHA-2 (sponge construction)

Status: ‚úÖ Approved alternative to SHA-2

Not intended to replace SHA-2, but provides alternative if SHA-2 is broken.

BLAKE2 & BLAKE3

BLAKE2: Faster than MD5, more secure than SHA-2

BLAKE3: 2020, extremely fast with multi-threading

Status: ‚úÖ Excellent for new applications

Gaining popularity for modern software needing speed and security.

Choosing the Right Hash

Use Case Recommended
General Purpose SHA-256, BLAKE2
Password Storage Argon2, bcrypt, scrypt (NOT SHA/MD5!)
Digital Signatures SHA-256, SHA-384
File Integrity SHA-256, BLAKE2
Blockchain SHA-256, Keccak-256
High Performance BLAKE3, BLAKE2

‚ö†Ô∏è Password Hashing is Special!

NEVER use general hashes (SHA-256, MD5) for passwords!

Why?

  • They're too fast - attackers can try billions per second
  • No built-in salt
  • Not memory-hard

Use Password-Specific Functions:

  • Argon2: Current best practice (winner of competition)
  • bcrypt: Widely used, deliberately slow
  • scrypt: Memory-hard
  • PBKDF2: Acceptable with high iterations

Salt: Essential for Password Security

Salt is random data added to passwords before hashing:

  1. Generate random salt
  2. Combine: salt + password
  3. Hash the combination
  4. Store: salt + hash

Why critical:

  • Makes rainbow tables useless
  • Same password ‚Üí different hashes for each user
  • Must crack each password individually

Public Key Infrastructure (PKI)

What is PKI?

Public Key Infrastructure is the complete system that manages digital certificates and enables secure communications on the Internet.

Components:

  • Digital Certificates: Electronic "passports" binding public keys to identities
  • Certificate Authority (CA): Trusted entity issuing certificates
  • Registration Authority (RA): Verifies certificate requests
  • Certificate Repository: Stores certificates and CRLs
  • Certificate Revocation List (CRL): List of revoked certificates

Digital Certificates

A digital certificate contains:

  • Subject (who it's issued to)
  • Subject's public key
  • Issuer (which CA issued it)
  • Serial number (unique ID)
  • Validity period (start/end dates)
  • Digital signature (CA's signature)
  • Key usage (what it can be used for)

Standard: X.509 - defines certificate structure

Certificate Authority (CA)

The CA is the foundation of trust:

Responsibilities:

  • Verifying identities before issuing certificates
  • Issuing and signing certificates
  • Maintaining certificate repositories
  • Publishing revocation lists
  • Protecting CA private keys

Types:

  • Root CA: Top of trust hierarchy
  • Intermediate CA: Issues certificates on behalf of root
  • Issuing CA: Issues end-user certificates

Major Public CAs:

  • DigiCert
  • Let's Encrypt (free, automated)
  • GlobalSign
  • Sectigo

Certificate Lifecycle

1. Issuance

  1. Generate key pair
  2. Submit Certificate Signing Request (CSR)
  3. CA validates identity
  4. CA issues signed certificate

2. Validation

When receiving a certificate, verify:

  • Not expired
  • CA signature valid
  • CA is trusted
  • Not revoked
  • Subject name matches

3. Revocation

Certificates may be revoked if:

  • Private key compromised
  • Employee left organization
  • Information changed
  • False information provided

Certificate Revocation Methods

Certificate Revocation Lists (CRL)

  • CA publishes list of revoked certificates
  • Contains serial numbers and revocation dates
  • Clients download and check
  • Problem: Can become very large

OCSP (Online Certificate Status Protocol)

  • Real-time certificate status checking
  • Client queries specific certificate
  • Immediate response: good/revoked/unknown
  • More efficient than downloading entire CRL

OCSP Stapling

  • Server provides OCSP response
  • Reduces client queries
  • Improves performance and privacy

Let's Encrypt Revolution

Launched in 2016, Let's Encrypt provides:

  • Free certificates: No cost for basic certificates
  • Automated: ACME protocol for automatic issuance/renewal
  • Short lifetimes: 90-day certificates encourage automation
  • Impact: Encrypted over 300 million websites

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical PKI Security

Key Backup:

  • Lost private keys = lost data forever
  • Back up keys securely (offline, encrypted)
  • Store in physical safe/vault

CA Security:

  • CA compromise = entire PKI compromised
  • Use Hardware Security Modules (HSM)
  • Offline root CA (only online when needed)
  • Strict physical and logical access controls

PKI Applications

  • HTTPS/TLS: Securing web traffic
  • Email Security: S/MIME, PGP
  • Code Signing: Verifying software authenticity
  • VPN: Certificate-based authentication
  • Document Signing: PDFs, office documents
  • Smart Cards: Physical authentication
  • IoT Security: Device authentication

Cryptographic Attacks

Understanding Attacks

Cryptanalysts continuously attempt to break encryption. Understanding these attacks helps us choose and implement cryptography properly.

Two Categories:

  • Passive Attacks: Only observe/collect data
  • Active Attacks: Interact with the system

Birthday Attack

Based on the birthday paradox: in a room of 23 people, there's a 50% chance two share a birthday.

In Cryptography: Used to find hash collisions

Mathematics:

  • Finding specific collision: ~2n attempts
  • Finding any collision: ~2n/2 attempts

Defense: Use longer hashes (SHA-256 requires ~2128 attempts)

Mathematical Attacks

Ciphertext-Only Attack

Attacker has: Only encrypted messages

Goal: Deduce plaintext or key from patterns

Example: Frequency analysis on simple ciphers

Known Plaintext Attack

Attacker has: Some plaintext-ciphertext pairs

Goal: Determine key to decrypt other messages

Historical: Common in WWII cryptanalysis

Chosen Plaintext Attack

Attacker has: Can choose plaintexts and see ciphertexts

Goal: Find patterns to determine key

Defense: Modern algorithms designed to resist this

Side-Channel Attack

Exploits: Physical characteristics

Types:

  • Timing attacks (measure computation time)
  • Power analysis (measure power consumption)
  • Electromagnetic analysis
  • Acoustic attacks
  • Cache attacks

Defense: Constant-time implementations, physical security

Brute-Force Attack

Try every possible key until finding the right one.

Key Length Possible Keys Time to Break
56-bit (DES) 256 Hours to days
128-bit 2128 Billions of years
256-bit 2256 Beyond universe lifetime

Defense: Use adequate key lengths (128+ bits symmetric, 2048+ RSA)

Man-in-the-Middle (MITM)

Attacker positions between two communicating parties:

  1. Intercepts all communications
  2. Pretends to be Bob when talking to Alice
  3. Pretends to be Alice when talking to Bob
  4. Can read, modify, or inject messages

Scenarios:

  • Rogue WiFi access points
  • ARP spoofing
  • DNS spoofing
  • SSL stripping

Defenses:

  • Mutual authentication
  • Certificate validation
  • Certificate pinning
  • HSTS (HTTP Strict Transport Security)
  • VPN tunnels

Dictionary Attack

Use list of common passwords instead of trying all combinations:

  • Much faster than pure brute-force
  • Dictionaries contain millions of known passwords
  • Includes leaked password databases
  • Common patterns and phrases

Why It Works:

  • Most people use weak passwords
  • Top 10 passwords cover ~10% of users
  • "password", "123456", "qwerty" extremely common

Defense:

  • Strong password policies
  • Check against breach databases
  • Account lockouts
  • Multi-factor authentication

Replay Attack

Capture legitimate data and retransmit it later:

  1. Attacker eavesdrops on communication
  2. Captures authentication or transaction data
  3. Later replays the captured data
  4. System accepts it as legitimate

Defenses:

  • Nonces (use-once random numbers)
  • Timestamps (reject old messages)
  • Sequence numbers
  • Session tokens with expiration

Rainbow Table Attack

Pre-computed tables of password hashes:

  • Contains millions/billions of hash:password pairs
  • Instant lookup instead of computing
  • Can be hundreds of GB

Defense: Salt makes rainbow tables useless because each password has unique salt requiring separate table.

Emerging Technologies & Future of Cryptography

The Evolving Landscape

Cryptography continues to evolve to meet new challenges and enable new capabilities. Understanding emerging technologies is crucial for future-proofing security implementations.

Quantum Computing: The Looming Threat

Quantum computers represent both an opportunity and a threat to modern cryptography.

How Quantum Computers Work

Unlike classical computers that use bits (0 or 1), quantum computers use qubits that can be in multiple states simultaneously through:

  • Superposition: Qubits can be 0, 1, or both at once
  • Entanglement: Qubits become correlated in ways impossible classically
  • Interference: Amplifying correct answers, canceling wrong ones

This enables quantum computers to solve certain problems exponentially faster than classical computers.

Cryptographic Implications

Algorithms at Risk:

  • RSA: Shor's algorithm can factor large numbers efficiently
  • Diffie-Hellman: Vulnerable to quantum discrete logarithm solving
  • ECC: Also vulnerable to Shor's algorithm variant
  • Timeline: Estimates range from 10-30 years for practical attacks

Still Safe:

  • Symmetric Encryption (AES): Quantum provides only ~2x speedup (use 256-bit keys)
  • Hash Functions: Grover's algorithm provides square root speedup (use longer hashes)

Current State of Quantum Computing

  • IBM, Google, and others have 50-1000+ qubit systems
  • Current qubits are noisy and error-prone
  • Requires millions of qubits for practical cryptographic attacks
  • Progress is rapid - what seems impossible today may be reality soon

Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC)

New algorithms designed to resist quantum computer attacks, while still running on classical computers.

NIST Standardization Process

In 2016, NIST began a competition to standardize post-quantum algorithms. Winners announced in 2022:

CRYSTALS-Kyber (Key Encapsulation)

Purpose: Establishing shared secrets (like Diffie-Hellman replacement)

Mathematical Basis: Learning with Errors (LWE) problem

Key Sizes: 768-1536 bytes (larger than RSA)

Performance: Fast enough for practical use

Status: Selected as primary standard

CRYSTALS-Dilithium (Digital Signatures)

Purpose: Creating and verifying digital signatures

Mathematical Basis: Module Learning with Errors

Signature Size: 2-4 KB (larger than RSA/ECC)

Status: Primary signature standard

FALCON (Digital Signatures)

Purpose: Alternative signature algorithm

Mathematical Basis: NTRU lattices

Advantage: Smaller signatures than Dilithium

Disadvantage: More complex implementation

SPHINCS+ (Digital Signatures)

Purpose: Hash-based signatures

Mathematical Basis: Only relies on hash function security

Advantage: Most conservative approach

Disadvantage: Large signatures (7-49 KB)

Transition Challenges

  • Larger Keys/Signatures: More bandwidth and storage needed
  • Algorithm Complexity: More difficult to implement correctly
  • Legacy Systems: Older systems can't be easily updated
  • Hybrid Approaches: Combining classical and PQC for safety

Implementation Timeline

  • 2024-2025: Standards finalization and early adoption
  • 2025-2030: Gradual rollout in critical systems
  • By 2035: Expected widespread deployment

"Harvest Now, Decrypt Later" Threat

Adversaries may be capturing encrypted data today to decrypt it once quantum computers become available. This makes the transition to PQC urgent for long-term sensitive data.

Homomorphic Encryption

Homomorphic encryption enables computations on encrypted data without ever decrypting it.

How It Works

Perform mathematical operations on encrypted data such that:

Encrypt(a) + Encrypt(b) = Encrypt(a + b) Encrypt(a) √ó Encrypt(b) = Encrypt(a √ó b)

The result, when decrypted, is the same as if operations were done on plaintext.

Types

  • Partially Homomorphic: Supports either addition OR multiplication
  • Somewhat Homomorphic: Limited number of operations
  • Fully Homomorphic (FHE): Unlimited operations on encrypted data

Applications

  • Cloud Computing: Process sensitive data in cloud without exposure
  • Healthcare: Analyze medical records while maintaining privacy
  • Financial Services: Fraud detection on encrypted transactions
  • Machine Learning: Train models on encrypted data

Current Limitations

  • Performance: 1000-1000000x slower than plaintext operations
  • Complexity: Difficult to implement correctly
  • Resource Intensive: Requires significant computing power
  • Research Stage: Not yet widely practical

Leading Implementations

  • Microsoft SEAL: Open-source library
  • IBM HElib: Research implementation
  • Google Private Join and Compute: Specific use cases

Zero-Knowledge Proofs (ZKP)

Zero-knowledge proofs allow one party to prove they know something without revealing what they know.

The Concept

Alice can prove to Bob that she knows a secret without revealing the secret itself. The proof convinces Bob with high probability, but reveals zero information about the secret.

Properties

  • Completeness: If statement is true, honest verifier will be convinced
  • Soundness: If statement is false, no cheating prover can convince verifier
  • Zero-Knowledge: Verifier learns nothing beyond truth of statement

Types

  • Interactive ZKP: Multiple rounds of communication
  • Non-Interactive ZKP (NIZK): Single message proof
  • zk-SNARKs: Succinct, non-interactive arguments (used in cryptocurrencies)
  • zk-STARKs: Scalable, transparent (doesn't require trusted setup)

Applications

  • Cryptocurrencies: Private transactions (Zcash)
  • Authentication: Prove identity without exposing password
  • Voting Systems: Verify votes without revealing choices
  • Supply Chain: Prove compliance without revealing business secrets
  • Blockchain Scalability: Compress many transactions into one proof

Real-World Example: Password Authentication

  1. Instead of sending password to server
  2. Client proves they know password using ZKP
  3. Server verifies proof without seeing password
  4. Even compromised server can't leak password

Key Management: Critical Best Practices

Even the strongest cryptography fails with poor key management. This is often the weakest link.

Key Generation

  • Use Cryptographically Secure RNG: Never use standard random functions
  • Sufficient Entropy: Ensure random seed has enough randomness
  • Appropriate Key Length: Follow current best practices (128-bit symmetric, 2048+ RSA)
  • Avoid Weak Keys: Some keys are mathematically weak - test for these

Key Storage

  • Never Hardcode: Don't embed keys in source code
  • Encrypt Keys: Use Key Encryption Keys (KEK)
  • Hardware Security Modules (HSM): Store critical keys in tamper-resistant hardware
  • Access Controls: Limit who can access keys
  • Secure Deletion: Properly wipe keys when no longer needed

Key Distribution

  • Never Send Keys in Clear: Always use secure channels
  • Use Key Exchange Protocols: Diffie-Hellman, ECDHE
  • Out-of-Band Verification: Verify key fingerprints through separate channel
  • Certificate-Based: Leverage PKI when appropriate

Key Rotation

  • Regular Rotation: Change keys periodically (policy-based)
  • Incident Response: Rotate immediately after suspected compromise
  • Cryptoperiod: Define maximum lifetime for each key type
  • Backward Compatibility: Support multiple key versions during transition

Key Backup and Recovery

  • Secure Backup: Encrypt backups with separate keys
  • Key Escrow: For business continuity (with proper controls)
  • Split Knowledge: Require multiple parties for recovery
  • Disaster Recovery: Test key recovery procedures

Key Destruction

  • Secure Deletion: Overwrite key material (not just delete file)
  • HSM Zeroization: Proper hardware key destruction
  • Audit Trail: Log all key lifecycle events
  • Verify Destruction: Confirm keys cannot be recovered

Other Emerging Technologies

Secure Multi-Party Computation (MPC)

Multiple parties jointly compute a function without revealing their inputs:

  • Used in privacy-preserving data analysis
  • Enables collaborative computation on sensitive data
  • Applications in finance, healthcare, government

Blockchain and Distributed Ledgers

Cryptography enables trustless distributed systems:

  • Hash chains for immutability
  • Digital signatures for authorization
  • Consensus mechanisms for agreement
  • Smart contracts for automated execution

Quantum Key Distribution (QKD)

Uses quantum mechanics to securely distribute keys:

  • Theoretically unbreakable (physics-based security)
  • Eavesdropping causes detectable disturbances
  • Limited by distance and requires specialized hardware
  • Already deployed in some high-security applications

Attribute-Based Encryption (ABE)

Encryption based on attributes rather than specific identities:

  • Fine-grained access control
  • Example: "Only senior managers in finance department"
  • Useful for cloud storage and IoT
